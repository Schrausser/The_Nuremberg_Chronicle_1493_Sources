## 1. Dyonisius / dionisius: Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite (c. 485–518 AD)

Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite is the name given to an ***anonymous*** Christian *theologian and Neoplatonic philosopher* who was active in the late 5th-6th c. AD. 

He is distinguished from the ***historical Dionysius the Areopagite***, a ***1st c. AD*** Athenian judge converted by *St. Paul* (Acts 17:34), whose name he used as a *pseudonym for his influential writings*. 

The timeline of *Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite* is divided into two distinct historical contexts: the life of the 1st c. biblical figure he claimed to be, and the actual late 5th c. author behind the influential ***Corpus Areopagiticum***. 

The dating of his works is primarily based on internal textual clues and their earliest known use in historical records: 

### The Historical & Literary Timeline

1. **c. 1st c. AD**: The life of the *original* *Dionysius the Areopagite*, an Athenian judge converted by *St. Paul*. According to tradition, he became the ***first Bishop of Athens***.
2. **c. 485 AD**: ***Death of*** the *Neoplatonist* philosopher ***Proclus***. Modern scholars use his work as a *terminus post quem* (earliest possible date), as *Pseudo-Dionysius’s* writings show heavy dependence on *Proclus's* ideas.
3. **Liturgical References (Post-476 AD)**: He references the recitation of the Creed in the liturgy, a practice that was only introduced in the Eastern Church around 476 AD or later.
4. **5th – 6th c. (c. 500 AD)**: Composition of the ***Corpus Areopagiticum***. The *author, likely a Syrian monk*, wrote under the pseudonym of the 1st c. Dionysius to lend apostolic authority to his mystical theology.
5. **c. 528–532 AD**: The earliest datable references to the works appear. His works were first clearly cited between 518 and 528 AD by ***Severus of Antioch*** and were famously used in a theological colloquy in Constantinople in 532/533 AD. In 532 AD, the ***writings*** were formally ***cited*** during a colloquy in Constantinople between supporters and opponents of the *Council of Chalcedon*.
6. **7th c.**: ***St. Maximus the Confessor*** wrote extensive scholia (***commentaries***) on the works, effectively *baptizing* them into mainstream Orthodox theology.
7. **827 AD**: The Byzantine Emperor *Michael II* sent a copy of the Corpus to the Frankish King *Louis the Pious*.
8. **c. 850–860 AD**: *John Scotus Eriugena* ***translated*** the works into Latin, making them accessible to the Western Medieval world.
9. **12th–13th c.**: Major Scholastic theologians, including *Thomas Aquinas* and *Albertus Magnus*, wrote ***commentaries*** on his works, solidifying his influence on Western Catholic doctrine.
10. **15th c.**: Renaissance humanists *Lorenzo Valla* and later *Erasmus* began to publicly ***question the 1st c. AD authorship*** based on linguistic and philosophical inconsistencies.
11.  **1895**: Critical studies by *Hugo Koch* and *Josef Stiglmayr* independently proved the works' *dependence on Proclus*, establishing the ***Pseudo-*** prefix in modern scholarship. 

### Works in the Corpus

The timeline of his literary output includes four major treatises and ten letters: 

1. ***The Divine Names***: Exploring the names of God in scripture.
2. ***The Celestial Hierarchy*** (*De Coelesti Hierarchia*): Defining the ***nine orders of angels***.
3. ***The Ecclesiastical Hierarchy***: Relating the earthly church rituals to divine reality.
4. ***The Mystical Theology***: Establishing the *Way of Negation* (apophatic theology). 


### The Historical Dionysius

To clarify the confusion between the author and his pseudonym:

1. ***Dionysius of the 1st c.***: The real biblical figure mentioned in Acts, believed to have died between 117 and 138 AD.
2. ***Pseudo-Dionysius***: The unknown author of the Corpus Areopagiticum, generally dated by modern scholars to roughly 485–518 AD.

While often confused in medieval history, these are three distinct figures—a 1st c. AD biblical convert, a 3rd c. AD French martyr, and a c. 500 AD mystical theologian. 

**Table**. Quick Comparison.
~~~
Feature 	Dionysius the Areopagite	Denis of France (St. Denis)	Pseudo-Dionysius
Era	1st Century	3rd Century (d. c. 250–258)	c. 500 (Early 6th Century)
Location	Athens, Greece	Paris, France	Likely Syria
Identity	Member of the Athenian judicial council (Areopagus).	First Bishop of Paris and missionary to Gaul.	Anonymous Neoplatonist author.
Claim to Fame	Converted by St. Paul (Acts 17:34).	Martyred by beheading; legendary "cephalophore" who carried his own head.	Author of the Corpus Dionysiacum, influential mystical texts.
Key Distinctions
Dionysius the Areopagite: The original figure mentioned in the New Testament who became the first Bishop of Athens. His name was later used as a pseudonym by the 6th-century author.
Denis of France: A missionary sent from Rome to Gaul (modern France). He was beheaded on Montmartre and is the patron saint of France.
Pseudo-Dionysius: An unknown writer (likely a Syrian monk) who wrote under the name of the 1st-century Athenian to gain apostolic authority. His works, such as The Divine Names and The Celestial Hierarchy, deeply influenced medieval theology and figures like Thomas Aquinas. 
~~~

### The History of Confusion

The conflation of these three began in the 9th c. AD. ***Hilduin***, the Abbot of Saint-Denis, wrote a biography (*Areopagitica*) that claimed Denis of Paris was the same person as the 1st c. AD Areopagite. This *holy error* aimed to give the French monarchy and the Abbey of Saint-Denis direct apostolic lineage. It wasn't until Renaissance humanists like Lorenzo Valla and Erasmus questioned the texts in the 15th c. that the identities began to be separated by scholars

## 2. Gregorius: Saint Gregory the Great (c. 540–604 AD)

The most famous historical figure with this name is ***Pope Gregory I*** (c. 540–604), known as St. Gregory the Great. 

> Over 2000 years of catholic tradition, there have been a few saints given the title of “great” for their contributions to the church. They are Basil, Leo, Albert, Anthony, Gertrude, and today’s doctor of the church, Gregory I. Not only is he great, he is the “Greatest of the Great.”, (https://tomperna.org/2013/09/03/saint-pope-gregory-i-the-greatest-of-the-great/)

1. **Role**: He was the 64th Bishop of Rome and the first monk to become pope.
2. **Legacy**: He is credited with the *Gregorian mission* to convert Anglo-Saxons to Christianity and is the ***namesake of Gregorian chant***.
3. **Patronage**: He is the patron saint of musicians, singers, students, and teachers.

Pope Gregory I (*Saint Gregory the Great*) formalized a nine-order angelic hierarchy in his ***Homiliae in Evangelia*** (specifically *Homily 34*). His classification, based on *scriptural references*, divides the angels into three distinct tiers or *triads*. 

### The Nine Choirs of Angels

According to *Gregory the Great*, the celestial hierarchy is ordered as follows, from highest to lowest: 

1. **Highest Triad (Attendants of God)**:
- **Seraphim**: The highest order, characterized by their *fiery* love and proximity to God.
- **Cherubim**: Associated with deep knowledge and insight into God's secrets.
- **Thrones**: Representing divine justice, submission, and peace.

2. **Middle Triad (Governors of Creation)**:

- **Dominions (Dominations)**: Regulate the duties of other angels and manifest God's authority.
- **Principalities**: Preside over good spirits and the protection of nations or large groups.
- **Powers**: Warrior angels who protect the cosmos against evil spirits.

3. **Lowest Triad (Messengers to Humanity)**:

- **Virtues**: Responsible for working miracles and governing the elements of nature.
- **Archangels**: Chief messengers for significant announcements, such as Michael, Gabriel, and Raphael.
- **Angels**: The order closest to humans, delivering prayers and personal messages. 

### Differences from Other Hierarchies

The angelic orders defined by ***Dionysius*** the Areopagite (also known as Pseudo-Dionysius) and Pope ***Gregory I*** (the Great) are the ***foundational sources*** for the traditional Christian hierarchy of nine choirs. 

*Gregory's* list is ***similar to*** that of Pseudo-***Dionysius***, but with ***slight variations*** in the middle and lower ranks. Specifically, Gregory places Principalities in the middle triad and Virtues in the lowest, whereas Dionysius does the opposite. Later theologians like ***Thomas Aquinas*** worked to ***harmonize*** these two influential systems. 

### Source Texts

1. ***Pseudo-Dionysius*** (c. 5th/6th c. AD): His primary work is ***De Coelesti Hierarchia*** (The Celestial Hierarchy). He organized angels into three *spheres* based on their proximity to God.
2. ***Pope Gregory I*** (c. 590–604 AD): He presented his list in a homily, specifically ***Homilia 34*** in ***Evangelia*** (Homily 34 on the Gospels), where he cites the authority of Scripture for the *nine orders*. 

### Comparison of the Hierarchies

Both systems use a *triad of triads* (nine total orders), but they *disagree* on the placement of *Principalities and Virtues*. 

**Table 1.**
~~~
Hierarchy (Sphere) 	Dionysius' Order						Gregory's Order

First (Highest)		Seraphim, Cherubim, Thrones				Seraphim, Cherubim, Thrones
Second (Middle)		Dominions, Virtues, Powers				Dominions, Powers, Virtues
Third (Lowest)		Principalities, Archangels, Angels		Principalities, Archangels, Angels*
~~~
*) Some sources indicate Gregory places Principalities in the third hierarchy, while others note minor variations in how these middle ranks are grouped compared to Dionysius. 

### Foundational Concepts

1. **Biblical Basis**: Both authors drew from the New Testament, particularly ***Ephesians 1:21*** and ***Colossians 1:16***, which mention various *thrones, dominions, principalities, and powers*.
2. **Neoplatonism**: The concept of a vertical, ***tripartite hierarchy*** was heavily influenced by ***Proclus***, an Athenian *Neoplatonist* whose principles of *procession* and *return* provided the structural framework. 

### Core Scriptural Sources Used by Gregory

*Gregory* argued that *almost all the pages of Holy Scripture attest* to the existence of these orders. Key texts he cited include: 

1. ***Prophetic Books (Isaiah, Ezekiel)***: For mentions of ***Seraphim and Cherubim***.
2. ***Ephesians 1:21***: Where *St. Paul* lists ***Principality, Power, Virtue and Domination***.
3. ***Colossians 1:16***: Where *St. Paul* mentions ***Thrones, Dominations, Principalities and Powers***.
4. ***Tobit 12:15***: Used to support the role of ***Archangels*** like Raphael. 

*Gregory* emphasized that *Angel* is the name of their *office*, not their nature; their *nature is spirit*, but they are called angels when they are sent on missions. 

1. **Theological Purpose**: Dionysius viewed the hierarchy as a "sacred order" designed to lead creatures toward union with God through purification, illumination, and perfection.
2. **Legacy**: These systems were *later harmonized* by ***St. Thomas Aquinas*** in his ***Summa Theologica***, which ***became the standard*** Catholic model. (**Not mentioned in NC!**)

## 4. Isidorus / ysidorus: Saint Isidore of Seville (c. 560–636 AD)

Isidore of Seville (c. 560 – 636 AD) was a Spanish scholar, theologian, and archbishop who is famously known as the *Schoolmaster of the Middle Ages* and the patron saint of the internet. He served as the Archbishop of Seville for over 30 years and played a pivotal role in preserving classical knowledge during the transition from the Roman Empire to the Middle Ages.

### Contributions & Legacy

1. **The Etymologiae (Origins)**: His most famous work is a ***20-volume encyclopedia*** that attempted to compile all human knowledge of the time, covering subjects from grammar and medicine to agriculture and history. It was the most popular textbook in medieval libraries for nine centuries.
2. **Patron Saint of the Internet**: Because of his efforts to record and categorise the world's information in a structured way (similar to a modern database), he was *named the patron saint* of the Internet, computer users and programmers by *Pope John Paul II* in 1997.
3. **Education Reform**: He required every diocese to establish seminaries and schools, promoting the study of the liberal arts, Greek, Hebrew, law and medicine.
4. **Unifying Spain**: *Isidore* was instrumental in converting the Visigothic kings from Arianism to Catholicism, helping to unify the diverse cultures of the Iberian Peninsula into a single nation.
5. **Doctor of the Church**: He was officially declared a Doctor of the Church in 1722 by *Pope Innocent XIII*. 

### Historical Significance

*Isidore* is often called the ***last scholar of the ancient world*** because he collected and synthesised classical Greco-Roman traditions with Christian doctrine ***before much*** of that knowledge was ***lost to the Dark Ages***. His writings on kingship also influenced the development of European representative government. 

Isidore of Seville (c. 560–636) played a key role in transmitting the history and legendary ***origins of the Septuagint*** (the Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible) ***to the medieval West***. His accounts, found *primarily* in his encyclopedic masterpiece *Etymologiae*, helped standardise the narrative of how this version was created. 

Isidore's History of the Septuagint

In ***Etymologiae (Book VI, Chapter 3)***, Isidore outlines the history of the Septuagint as a pivotal moment in the *Second Age* of *biblical transmission*. 

1. **The Translation Legend**: *Isidore* recounts the popular legend of the *Seventy Translators* (the Septuaginta), commissioned by King Ptolemy II Philadelphus of Egypt.
2. **Miraculous Coordination**: He perpetuates the belief that the 70 (or 72) Jewish scholars, working in separate cells, produced identical Greek translations of the Torah through divine inspiration.
3. **Authority of the Text**: For *Isidore*, the Septuagint was not merely a translation but an authoritative text used by the *Apostles and the early Church*, which he contrasted with later translations like those of *Aquila or Symmachus*. 

### Context within His Works

Isidore ***integrated the history of the Septuagint*** into a broader framework of sacred and ***world history***:

1. **Universal History**: In his ***Chronica Majora***, he uses the Septuagint's chronology as a basis for dividing the history of the world into Six Ages.
2. **Linguistic Significance**: In ***Etymologiae (Book IX)***, he discusses the ***Septuagint*** as part of the history of the three sacred languages (Hebrew, Greek, and Latin), positioning it as the bridge that allowed the *Old Law* to be understood by the wider Greco-Roman world.
3. **Preservation of Sources**: By including these accounts, *Isidore* preserved snippets of earlier Patristic scholarship (such as that of *Jerome* and *Augustine*) that might otherwise have been less accessible to medieval scholars. 

### Legacy

Isidore’s summary became the *standard reference* for medieval students and theologians. His Etymologiae was one of the most widely copied texts for over a millennium, ***ensuring*** that the legendary ***history of the Septuagint*** remained central to Western Christian identity.

## 5. Matheus: Saint Matthew (c. 1–68 AD)

Saint Matthew (also known as *Levi*) was one of the twelve apostles of *Jesus* and is traditionally recognized as the author of the Gospel of Matthew, the first book of the New Testament. He is one of the four Evangelists, alongside *Mark, Luke*, and *John*.

### The Fourth Age in Context

While the ***Fourth Age*** is traditionally associated with various generational counts in medieval historiography, it is most famously linked to the ***14 generations*** outlined in the ***Genealogy of Jesus*** in the ***Gospel of Matthew***. 

### Life and Ministry Timeline

1. **Early 1st c. AD**: Born in Galilee, Roman Empire.
2. **Pre-Discipleship**: Worked as a tax collector (publican) in Capernaum for *Herod Antipas*.
3. **c. 27–30 AD**: Called by *Jesus* to be a disciple. He immediately left his tax booth to follow him and hosted a feast for *Jesus* and other *sinners*.
4. **c. 30–33 AD**: Witnessed the ministry, death, resurrection, and ascension of *Jesus*.
5. **c. 33 AD**(Pentecost): Present with the other apostles when the Holy Spirit descended.
6. **c. 33–42 AD**: Preached in Palestine/Judea for approximately nine to fifteen years following the Ascension.
7. **c. 41–50 AD**: Authored the ***Gospel of Matthew***, traditionally believed to have been written first in Aramaic (or Hebrew) for a Jewish audience.
8. **Post-42 AD**: Departed Judea to escape persecution. Traveled as a missionary to regions including Ethiopia, Persia, Parthia, Syria, and Media.
9. **c. 68 AD**: Believed to have *died as a martyr*, though accounts vary on the method (beheading, stoning, or burning) and location (often cited as Ethiopia or Persia). 

### Legacy and Veneration

1. **Patronage**: Accountants, bankers, tax collectors, bookkeepers, and civil servants.
2. **Symbol**: Often depicted as a winged man or an angel, one of the four living creatures of Revelation.
3. **Feast Days**:
- September 21: Western Christianity (Catholic, Anglican, Lutheran).
- November 16: Eastern Christianity (Orthodox). 

### c.f. Matthew Paris (c. 1200–1259)

Matthew Paris (c. 1200–1259), a Benedictine monk and chronicler, and his involvement with medieval concepts of the *age of the world* (etate mundi) or *world mapping* (mappa mundi). 

While *De etate mundi* is specifically a treatise by Walter Odington, Matthew Paris is famous for his historical and cartographic works that contextualised the state of the world in the 13th c.: 

1. ***Chronica Majora***: This is his most significant work, a universal history of the world from Creation until his death. It incorporates the "ages of the world" framework common in medieval scholarship to track human history.
2. ***Mappa Mundi***: *Matthew Paris* created several maps, including a world map and a famous map of Britain. These were visual histories intended to show the *state* (etate) of the world and the paths of history.
3. ***Ages of the World***: In medieval theology, the *etate mundi* (ages of the world) typically divided history into six or seven distinct periods, a concept Matthew utilized to structure his chronicles.
