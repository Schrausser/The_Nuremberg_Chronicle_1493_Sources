# Original sources for Ancient Egyptian history

---

The primary original sources for Ancient Egyptian history are categorized into **monumental remains**, written **records** on papyrus and stone, and physical **artifacts**. These sources are categorized as **primary evidence** because they are **firsthand** accounts or **original material**s created during the civilization's existence. 

Monumental & Archaeological Sites

The most visible remaining sources are the vast architectural structures that served religious or funerary purposes. 

1. **The Giza Necropolis**: Includes the Great Pyramid of Khufu, the Sphinx, and the Pyramid of Khafre.
2. **Valley of the Kings**: A royal burial ground in Thebes containing dozens of tombs, including that of Tutankhamun.
3. **Temple Complexes**: Massive structures like the Karnak Temple Complex (dedicated to Amun-Re), Luxor Temple, and the Mortuary Temple of Hatshepsut.
4. **Abu Simbel & Philae**: Colossal rock-cut temples and the island-based Temple of Isis. 

Written Records & King Lists

Crucial for establishing timelines, these sources often list pharaohs and their regnal years. 

1. **Turin King List (Turin Canon)**: A fragmentary 19th Dynasty papyrus originally listing over 220 kings from the 1st to the 17th Dynasty.
2. **Palermo Stone**: A black basalt stela fragment containing royal annals from the Predynastic period to the 5th Dynasty.
3. **Abydos King List**: A wall relief in the Temple of Seti I listing 76 *legitimate* pharaohs.
4. **The Rosetta Stone**: A granodiorite stela that was key to deciphering hieroglyphs because it contains the same decree in three scripts: Hieroglyphs, Demotic, and Ancient Greek. 

Funerary & Scientific Papyri

Papyrus scrolls ***found in dry desert tombs*** have preserved religious, medical, and literary texts. 

1. **Book of the Dead**: A collection of funerary spells intended to guide the deceased through the afterlife.
2. **Pyramid Texts & Coffin Texts**: The oldest religious writings, found on the walls of Old Kingdom pyramids and Middle Kingdom coffins.
3. **Edwin Smith Papyrus**: The world's *oldest known* surgical treatise, dating back to approximately 1600 BC.
4. **Kahun Gynaecological Papyrus**: The oldest known medical text on women's health. 

Artifacts & Human Remains

1. **Mummies**: Preserved bodies that provide biological data, health history, and genetic evidence of continuity with modern populations.
2. **Ostraca**: Limestone flakes or pottery shards used as *scrap paper* for letters, receipts, and school exercises.
3. **Daily Life Objects**: Tools, jewelry, combs, and pottery found in settlements like Deir el-Medina provide insight into the lives of ordinary citizens. 

External *Primary* Accounts

While not Egyptian in origin, **early Greek and Roman travelers** provided contemporary descriptions of Egypt. 

1. ***Herodotus***: His *Histories (Book II)* contains extensive descriptions of Egyptian geography, customs, and kings.
2. ***Manetho***: A *Greco-Egyptian priest* whose lost work, *Aegyptiaka*, established the system of 30 dynasties used by modern historians today.

# Berber or Amazigh calendar

---

The Berber calendar (or Amazigh calendar) is a traditional agricultural system used by the *Imazighen* (the Berber peoples also known as Amazigh) of North Africa to regulate seasonal farming.

It is based on the ancient Julian calendar, making it roughly 13 to 14 days behind the Gregorian calendar, modernized in the 1960s-1980s by Amazigh intellectuals (most notably ***Ammar Negadi***) to **start from 950 BC**, its formal "starting point" (***Year 1***), the year ***Pharaoh Shoshenq I*** (*of Berber origin*) ascended the *Egyptian throne* and founded the 22nd Dynasty of Egypt.

# Egypt: Third Intermediate Period

---

## Shoshenq I (c. 943–922 BC)

*Shoshenq I* (reigned c. 943–922 BC), the founder of the 22nd Dynasty and a pivotal figure in the *Third Intermediate Period* of ancient Egypt. Of *Libyan* (*Meshwesh*) ancestry, he rose to power from the *military*, eventually succeeding *Psusennes II* as pharaoh. 

Key Biographical Facts

1. *Origins*: He was the son of ***Nimlot A***, a *Great Chief of the Ma* (a *Libyan* tribe), and came from a line of influential chiefs settled in the Nile Delta, likely at *Bubastis*.
2. *Rise to Power*: Before becoming pharaoh, Shoshenq served as Commander-in-Chief of the *Egyptian Army* and was a high-level advisor to his predecessor.
3. Succession Strategy: He consolidated his rule by appointing his sons to key religious and military positions, most notably naming his son *Iuput A* as High Priest of *Amun* at *Thebes*. 
4. Biblical Identification: The *Shishak* Campaign 
Most Egyptologists and archaeologists identify *Shoshenq I* as the *biblical pharaoh Shishak*, who invaded the kingdoms of Israel and Judah during the fifth year of King *Rehoboam* (c. 925 BC). 
5. *Military Success*: His campaign into Canaan is famously recorded on the Bubastite Portal at Karnak, which lists over 150 captured cities and territories, including Megiddo and Gezer.
6. *The Jerusalem Question*: While the Bible states *Shishak* looted the Temple of Solomon in Jerusalem, the city's name is conspicuously absent from *Shoshenq's* own topographical list at Karnak. Scholars suggest Jerusalem may have been spared destruction by paying tribute, or that the name was simply lost from the inscriptions over time.
7. *Archeological Evidence*: A fragment of a victory stela bearing *Shoshenq's* name was discovered at *Megiddo*, providing physical proof of his presence in the region during his campaign. 

Domestic Policy and Legacy

1. *Centralization*: *Shoshenq* broke the hereditary hold of local families on the high priesthood of Amun, instead *appointing his own family* members to ensure loyalty across both Upper and Lower Egypt.
2. *Building Programs*: He initiated major construction at the Temple of Karnak, though many projects remained unfinished at the time of his death.
3. *Burial*: His actual tomb remains *undiscovered*. Some believe he *may* have been buried at ***Tanis***, *Bubastis*, or within the Ptah temple complex at *Memphis*, where his funerary cult continued for several generations.

## Transition to the 22nd Dynasty of Egypt (945-712 BC)

The Twenty-second Dynasty of Egypt, often referred to as the **Libyan or Bubastite Dynasty**, succeeded the 21st Dynasty around 945 BC. 

Transition to the 22nd Dynasty

The transition was *led by Shoshenq I*, a powerful chieftain of Libyan extraction (the Meshwesh tribe) who had risen to prominence during the late 21st Dynasty. Unlike many previous dynastic shifts, this transition was relatively peaceful, solidified through a marriage alliance between *Shoshenq I's* son, *Osorkon I*, and the daughter of the final 21st Dynasty pharaoh, *Psusennes II*. 

Comparison of Successive Dynasties

While the 21st Dynasty saw Egypt *divided* between northern kings in *Tanis* and High Priests in *Thebes*, the 22nd Dynasty initially managed to *reunify* the country under centralized rule. 

Following the 22nd Dynasty, the Twenty-third Dynasty arose as a contemporary offshoot, ruling parallel to the later Libyan kings during a period of further political fragmentation known as the Third Intermediate Period. 

The 22nd Dynasty of Egypt did not have a single, clean successor due to the political fragmentation of the Third Intermediate Period. Instead, *several rival dynasties* emerged and ruled concurrently before being ultimately superseded by the 25th (Kushite) Dynasty. 

The primary successors were:
1. 23rd Dynasty: This was an offshoot of the 22nd Dynasty. It began when Pedubast I proclaimed himself pharaoh during the reign of Shoshenq III, leading to a split where the 22nd Dynasty continued in Tanis and Bubastis while the 23rd Dynasty ruled from Leontopolis and Thebes.
2. 24th Dynasty: A short-lived dynasty founded by Tefnakht of Sais in the Western Delta. Tefnakht and his successor Bakenrenef challenged the remaining Libyan rulers of the 22nd and 23rd dynasties.
3. 25th Dynasty: This Nubian dynasty from Kush (modern Sudan) eventually unified all of Egypt. The Kushite king Piye invaded and defeated the various local "kings" of the 22nd, 23rd, and 24th dynasties, though some, like Osorkon IV (the final king of the 22nd Dynasty), were allowed to remain as local vassals for a time. 

---

## The 26th Dynasty of Egypt (664-525 BC)

The Twenty-sixth Dynasty (664–525 BC), also known as the Saite Dynasty, was the immediate successor to the 25th Dynasty of Egypt. 
While the 25th Dynasty (Kushite) was driven back to their homeland in Nubia by invading Neo-Assyrian forces, the transition to the 26th Dynasty occurred as follows: 

1. Assyrian Puppet State: Following the Assyrian conquest, the Assyrian king Ashurbanipal installed native Egyptian vassals to govern. Necho I of Sais was appointed as a ruler in the Delta.
Establishment of the 26th Dynasty: Necho I's son, Psamtik I (Psammetichus I), is credited as the founder of the 26th Dynasty.
2. Unification of Egypt: Initially ruling as an Assyrian vassal, Psamtik I quickly unified Lower Egypt. By 656 BC, he successfully extended his authority to Thebes in Upper Egypt, effectively ending Kushite control and reuniting all of Egypt under native rule.
3. Late Period: The rise of the 26th Dynasty marks the beginning of the Late Period of ancient Egypt. 

---

## The 27th Dynasty of Egypt (525-404 BC)

The Twenty-seventh Dynasty of Egypt (525–404 BC), also known as the First Egyptian Satrapy, was the immediate successor to the 26th Dynasty. 

Historical Transition

The 26th Dynasty (Saite Period) ended in 525 BC when the Persian Emperor *Cambyses II* invaded Egypt. He defeated *Psamtik III*, the last pharaoh of the 26th Dynasty, at the Battle of Pelusium. Following this victory: 
Egypt was incorporated into the Achaemenid Persian Empire as a province (satrapy).
Cambyses II was crowned as the first pharaoh of the new 27th Dynasty.
This transition marked the end of the last significant phase of independent rule by native Egyptian pharaohs until the 28th Dynasty. 

Key Details of the 27th Dynasty

1. Status: It was the first of two periods of ***Persian rule in Egypt*** (the second being the 31st Dynasty).
2. Government: The Persians generally ruled through a resident governor known as a satrap, though they adopted traditional Egyptian pharaonic titles.
3. Major Rulers: Notable Persian pharaohs of this period included *Darius I*, who improved the legal system and built public works, and *Xerxes I*, during whose reign Egyptian status was downgraded.
4. End of Rule: The dynasty concluded in 404 BC when a native Egyptian revolt led by *Amyrtaeus* (founder of the 28th Dynasty) successfully *overthrew* the Persian masters.

## Library of Alexandria

The timeline of the Ancient Library of Alexandria spans nearly a millennium, from its **founding in the early 3rd c. BC** to its *gradual decline* and **ultimate disappearance by the 7th c. AD**. Rather than a single catastrophic event, it was likely destroyed through a ***series of fires**, ***neglect***, and ***political shifts***. 

Founding and Peak (**Ptolemaic Era**)

1. **c. 305-285 BC**: ***Ptolemy I Soter*** initiates the project, likely on the advice of the exiled Athenian scholar *Demetrius of Phalerum*.
2. **c. 282-246 BC**: The Library is officially built and expanded under ***Ptolemy II Philadelphus***, becoming part of the Mouseion (Temple of the Muses).
3. **246-222 BC**: ***Ptolemy III Euergetes*** founds the *Serapeum*, a *daughter library* to house the growing collection of scrolls, which reached between 400000 and 700000 at its height.
4. **145 BC**: *Ptolemy VIII* expels foreign scholars from Alexandria, marking the **beginning of** the library's intellectual **decline**. 

Gradual Destruction and **Decline**

1. **48 BC**: During ***Julius Caesar's*** siege of Alexandria, a **fire** spread from the docks to a warehouse containing scrolls. While legendary, many scholars believe the *main library survived* this event.
2. **270-275 AD**: The city suffers major damage during the Palmyrene invasion and the subsequent counterattack by Emperor ***Aurelian***, likely **destroying** the Royal Quarter where **the library** was located.
3. **391 AD**: ***Bishop Theophilus***, **destroys the Serapeum** temple. It is debated whether many books remained there by this time.
4. **415 AD**: The murder of *Hypatia*, a leading philosopher and scholar, signals the **end** of Alexandria's status as a **pagan intellectual center**.
5. **642 AD**: The Arab conquest of Egypt takes place. Historical tales of ***Caliph Omar*** **burning the library's remains** for six months are *largely considered discredited legends* by modern historians.

## Aegyptiaca (3rd c. BC)

**Aegyptiaca**, a history of Egypt written by the Greco-Egyptian priest and historian ***Manetho*** in the 3rd c. BC

To reconstruct the history of Ancient Egypt, scholars rely on a combination of monumental archaeology, written records (on papyri and stone), and foreign accounts. 

Primary Written Sources (King Lists)

The most vital documents for establishing chronology are the *King Lists*, which record the names and reign lengths of pharaohs. 

1. **Turin King List (Royal Canon of Turin)**: A 19th c. BC hieratic papyrus containing over 300 royal names. It is considered the *most accurate* indigenous source.
2. **Abydos King List**: Carved on the walls of the Temple of *Seti I*, this list features 76 kings from Narmer to *Seti I* but intentionally omits illegitimate rulers like *Hatshepsut* and *Akhenaten*.
3. **Palermo Stone**: A basalt stele fragment that records the annals of the Old Kingdom, including annual Nile flood heights and religious festivals.
4. **Manetho’s Aegyptiaca**: Written in the 3rd century BC by an Egyptian priest in Greek, this work first divided history into the 30 dynasties we *still use today*. 

Archaeological & Epigraphic Sources

Beyond lists, physical remains provide the cultural and social context of the civilization. 

1. **The Rosetta Stone**: Essential for deciphering hieroglyphs, this decree was written in three scripts: Hieroglyphic, Demotic, and Greek.
2. **Pyramid Texts & Book of the Dead**: Funerary spells inscribed on tomb walls or papyri that detail Egyptian beliefs about the afterlife.
3. **Autobiographies**: *Inscriptions* in non-royal tombs, such as those of the official ***Weni** or the explorer ***Harkhuf***, offer rare insights into military campaigns and trade. 

Classical Foreign Accounts

Early Greek and Roman historians visited Egypt and recorded their observations, though these often mixed fact with myth. 

1. ***Herodotus (The Histories)***: Devoted his second book to Egypt, detailing customs, geography, and legends like the building of the Great Pyramid.
2. ***Strabo & Diodorus Siculus***: Later Greek writers who provided descriptions of Egyptian temples and administrative systems as they appeared in the Greco-Roman period. 

Where to Access Digital Archives

1. *The British Museum Online Collection*: Houses the Rosetta Stone and a vast array of papyri.
2. *Duke Papyrus Archive*: Provides electronic access to images and translations of over 1400 ancient papyri.
3. *UCLA Encyclopedia of Egyptology*: A resource for specific historical topics.

## Manetho (3rd c. BC)

Manetho (/ˈmænɪθoʊ/; Koine Greek: Μανέθων Manéthōn, gen.: Μανέθωνος, fl. 290-260 BC) was an *Egyptian priest* of the *Ptolemaic* Kingdom who lived in the early 3rd c. BC, at the very beginning of the Hellenistic period. 
Little is certain about his life. He is known today as the author of a history of Egypt in Greek called the ***Aegyptiaca*** (History of Egypt), written during the reign of *Ptolemy I Soter* or *Ptolemy II Philadelphus* (285-246 BC). None of Manetho’s original texts have survived; they are lost literary works, **known only from fragments** transmitted by later authors of classical and late antiquity.

Transmission and Surviving Works

No original copies of Manetho's texts survive today. His work is preserved only through fragments and summaries (epitomes) in the writings of later authors: 

1. ***Flavius Josephus***: Quoted Manetho in *Against Apion* to discuss Jewish history and the Exodus.
2. **Christian Chronographers**: Writers like ***Sextus Julius Africanus*** and ***Eusebius of Caesarea*** preserved his king-lists to *align* ***biblical events*** *with* ***Egyptian history***.
3. ***George Syncellus***: A 9th c. Byzantine monk whose work is the **primary source** for the **surviving** versions of ***Manetho’s*** dynastic lists.

## Herodotus (c. 484-425 BC)

Herodotus (c. 484-425 BC) was an ancient Greek writer and geographer widely celebrated as the **Father of History**. He was the first to approach the study of the past as a systematic *inquiry* (historía in Greek), seeking to understand the causes and effects of human events rather than attributing them solely to the gods. 

Because the *original 5th c. BC papyri have not survived*, scholars rely on later copies to reconstruct the text: 

1. **Papyrus Fragments**: The earliest physical remnants are fragments from the *Oxyrhynchus Papyri*, some dating as far back as the **1st-2nd c. AD**.
2. **Byzantine Manuscripts**: The most complete versions are medieval manuscripts, the most famous being the **Codex Laurentianus LXX (Codex A)** from the **9th-10th c. AD**, held in Florence.
3. Other Key Codices: Important secondary manuscripts include the **Codex Angelicanus (11th c.)** and **Vatican graecus 2369 (11th-12th c.)**.

***Rhampsinit*** (*also called Rhampsinitos, Rhampsinitus, Rampsinitus, Rampsinit, derived from Herodotus' Greek Ῥαμψίνιτος Rhampsínitos*) *fictitious* king (pharaoh) from Ancient Egypt. He is named by the ancient Greek historian *Herodotus* as a literary figure in his *Historiae*. There it is told that *Rhampsinit* was the **predecessor** of the legendary king ***Kheops***.

The Greek historian ***Herodotus** instead depicts *Khufu* as a heretic and cruel tyrant. In his literary work ***Historiae, Book II, chapter 124–126***, he writes: 

>"As long as *Rhámpsinîtos* was king, as they told me, there was nothing but orderly rule in Egypt, and the land prospered greatly. But after him *Khéops* became king over them and brought them to every kind of suffering: He closed all the temples; after this he kept the priests from sacrificing there and then he forced all the Egyptians to work for him. So some were ordered to draw stones from the stone quarries in the Arabian mountains to the Nile, and others he forced to receive the stones after they had been carried over the river in boats, and to draw them to those called the Libyan mountains. And they worked by 100,000 men at a time, for each three months continually. Of this oppression there passed ten years while the causeway was made by which they drew the stones, which causeway they built, and it is a work not much less, as it appears to me, than the pyramid. For the length of it is 5 furlongs and the breadth 10 fathoms and the height, where it is highest, 8 fathoms, and it is made of polished stone and with figures carved upon it. For this, they said, 10 years were spent, and for the underground chambers on the hill upon which the pyramids stand, which he caused to be made as sepulchral chambers for himself in an island, having conducted thither a channel from the Nile.  
    For the making of the pyramid itself there passed a period of 20 years; and the pyramid is square, each side measuring 800 feet, and the height of it is the same. It is built of stone smoothed and fitted together in the most perfect manner, not one of the stones being less than 30 feet in length. This pyramid was made after the manner of steps, which some call 'rows' and others 'bases': When they had first made it thus, they raised the remaining stones with *devices made of short pieces of timber*, lifting them first from the ground to the first stage of the steps, and when the stone got up to this it was placed upon another machine standing on the first stage, and so from this it was drawn to the second upon another machine; for as many as were the courses of the steps, so many machines there were also, or perhaps they transferred one and the same machine, made so as easily to be carried, to each stage successively, in order that they might take up the stones; for let it be told in both ways, according as it is reported. However, that may be, the highest parts of it were finished first, and afterwards they proceeded to finish that which came next to them, and lastly they finished the parts of it near the ground and the lowest ranges.

Kheops moreover came to such a pitch of evilness, that being in want of money he sent his own daughter to a brothel and ordered her to obtain from those who came a certain amount of money (how much it was they did not tell me). But she not only obtained the sum that was appointed by her father, but she also formed a design for herself privately to leave behind her a memorial: She requested each man who came in to her to give her one stone for her building project. And of these stones, they told me, the pyramid was built which stands in front of the great pyramid in the middle of the three, each side being 150 feet in length."

***Herodotus*** provided one of the earliest known historical accounts of the Giza pyramids in his work, *The Histories*. **Visiting Egypt roughly 2000 years after the monuments were built**, he relied on *oral* traditions from local guides and priests, which blended factual observation with legendary anecdotes. 

One of his most famous claims concerns the construction method of the Great Pyramid (Khufu's pyramid). He described the use of *machines made of short wooden planks* that lifted the massive stones from one step of the pyramid to the next. 

- **Method**: Stones were raised from the ground to the first *step by one machine*, then another machine on the first tier lifted them to the second, *and so on*.
- **Modern Interpretation**: Engineers have theorized these could have been *rocker devices* or *shaduf-style levers*. While ***Leonardo da Vinci*** even *sketched designs based on this description*, many modern archaeologists believe ramps were the primary method, perhaps supplemented by these machines for final positioning. 

The Pyramids
 
Construction Statistics

Herodotus provided ***specific*** (though often debated) ***figures*** for the scale of the project: 

1. *Workforce*: He claimed 100000 men worked in three-month shifts. Modern estimates by Egyptologists like *Zahi Hawass* suggest a smaller, more permanent force of about 20000 to 36000 skilled laborers.
2. *Timeline*: He recorded that it took 10 years just to build the causeway for transporting stone and another 20 years to build the pyramid itself.
3. *Finishing*: He noted the pyramid was finished from the top down, meaning the final outer casing of polished limestone was applied starting at the summit. 
 
Historical and Mythological Anecdotes

Herodotus' accounts include several colorful stories that modern historians view as folklore: 

1. *Tyrannical Rule*: He portrayed Pharaoh Khufu (Cheops) as a cruel despot who closed temples and forced all of Egypt into labor.
2. *The Prostitution Story*: A particularly famous (and likely fabricated) anecdote claims Khufu, short on funds, forced his own daughter into prostitution to finance the build. She supposedly asked each client for a single block of stone to build her own small pyramid.
3. *Subterranean Chambers*: He described an underground island chamber surrounded by a canal from the Nile where Khufu was supposedly buried. While shafts exist, no evidence of an internal *canal-fed island* has been found. 

The **primary source** of *Herodotus's Histories* (written c. 450–420 BC) is the author's **own extensive investigations**, which he called historie (Greek for *inquiry*). 
Unlike modern history based on archived documents, his work relied heavily on **oral** tradition, personal **observation**, and **interviews**. 

### Herodotus's Methods and Informants

Herodotus **traveled extensively** throughout the Mediterranean, Egypt, and the Near East to gather data. His information came from: 

1. **Oral Testimony**: He interviewed a wide range of people, including priests (in Greece, Egypt, and Babylon), merchants, and veterans of the Greco-Persian Wars.
2. **Autopsy (opsis)**: He prioritized what he *saw personally*, such as monuments, geography, and foreign customs.
3. **Local Legends**: He often recorded multiple, sometimes conflicting, versions of a story, famously stating that his duty was to report what he was told, even if he did not believe it.
4. **Poetic Traditions**: He was influenced by the epic style of *Homer*, though he moved away from divine explanation toward human cause-and-effect.

## Scylax of Caryanda

Scylax of Caryanda (fl. 515 BC) was a *renowned* ***Greek mariner***, *explorer*, and author who **served the Achaemenid Empire**. 

He is best known for his **pioneering exploration of the Indus River** and the surrounding regions *on behalf of the Persian King* ***Darius I***. 


Key Achievements and Voyage

1. **The Expedition**: Commissioned **around 515 BC**, Scylax led a reconnaissance mission to trace the course of the Indus River. Starting from Caspatyrus (near modern Peshawar, Pakistan), he navigated downstream to the Arabian Sea.
2. **The Route**: From the mouth of the Indus, he sailed west across the Indian Ocean, circumnavigated the Arabian Peninsula, and eventually reached the Isthmus of Suez in Egypt.
3. **Duration**: The entire voyage lasted approximately **30 months**.
4. **Outcome**: His reports facilitated *Darius I's* subsequent conquest of the Indus Valley and *established early maritime communication* between *India*, *Persia*, and the *Mediterranean*. 

Literary Contributions

1. **The Original Periplus**: Scylax wrote an account of his travels, often considered the **first Greek geographical work in prose**. While the **original text is lost**, fragments are **cited by** later authors like ***Herodotus, Aristotle, and Strabo***.
2. **Pseudo-Scylax**: A surviving 4th c. BC Mediterranean coastal guide, the ***Periplus*** of *Pseudo-Scylax*, was traditionally attributed to him but is now known to be the work of a later, anonymous author using his famous name for authority.
3. **Biography**: He is also credited with writing a life of his contemporary, *Heraclides of Mylasa*, making him a pioneer in the historical-biographical genre. 

Historical Legacy

Scylax is a pivotal figure who **bridged Eastern and Western knowledge**. His writings were the primary source for the Greek world’s *early understanding of India*, even introducing the name *India* to Western literature through his descriptions of the people near the Indus.

## Hekataios von Milet (c. 560-480 v. Chr.)

Zur Zeit des Ionischen Aufstandes von 500 bis 494 v. Chr.
Die Werke von *Hekataios von Milet* (ca. 560-480 v. Chr.) sind ***nicht im Original erhalten***, sondern ***überliefert durch über 370 Fragmente in den Schriften späterer antiker Autoren***. Er gilt als einer der ersten griechischen Prosaautoren (Logographen) und als ***Vater der Geographie***. 

### Zentrale Überlieferung

Die Fragmente seiner beiden Hauptwerke sind primär durch folgende Quellen bekannt:

1. ***Stephanus von Byzanz***: Der wichtigste Vermittler; sein geographisches Lexikon Ethnika (6. Jh. n. Chr.) enthält über 300 Zitate aus *Hekataios'* geographischem Werk, meist kurze Nennungen von Ortsnamen.
2. ***Herodot***: Er nutzte Hekataios als wichtige Vorlage und kritisierte ihn oft. Herodot zitiert ihn unter anderem in seinen Berichten über Ägypten und die Ionische Revolte.
3. ***Fragmente*** *der griechischen Historiker (FGrH)*: Die wissenschaftliche Standardausgabe der Fragmente wurde von ***Felix Jacoby*** (als Nr. 1) zusammengestellt. 

### Die zwei Hauptwerke

1. **Periegesis, Ges Periodos (Periodos ges / Erdumkreisung, Erdumrundung)**: Eine systematische geographische Beschreibung der damals bekannten Welt in *zwei Büchern* (Europa und Asien/Libyen), das Küstenverläufe, Völker und Entfernungen beschrieb. Es enthielt eine der ersten Weltkarten, welche die Erde als kreisrunde Scheibe darstellte.

2. **Genealogiai (Genealogien)**: Ein *vierbändiges Werk* über die griechische Mythologie und Heroenzeit, über die *Mythenwelt und die Abstammung* heroischer Familien. Hekataios versuchte hier erstmals, Mythen rational zu deuten, mit dem berühmten Einleitungssatz:

> „Ich schreibe dies, wie es mir wahr zu sein scheint; denn die Erzählungen der Griechen sind vielfältig und, wie mir scheint, lächerlich“.

## Miletus

Miletus (Ancient Greek: Μίλητος, romanised: Mílētos) was one of the most powerful and influential ancient Greek cities in *Ionia*, near the mouth of the *Maeander River* located on the western coast of Anatolia in present-day Turkey. ***Renowned as the birthplace of Western philosophy***, it was home to the **Milesian School** of thinkers, including ***Thales, Anaximander,*** and ***Anaximenes***, who were among the first to *seek* ***natural rather than mythological explanations*** *for the* ***universe***. 

Builder:	**Minoans (later Mycenaeans)** and then **Ionians** (the later on a former Anatolian site).

### Historical Significance

1. **Maritime Power**: At its peak in the 7thy-6th c. BC, Miletus was the *greatest Greek metropolis*, founding over 90 colonies across the Mediterranean and Black Sea.
2. **Urban Planning**: The city is famous for its *Hippodamian* plan, a grid-like street layout named after its native architect ***Hippodamus***, which became the *standard* for Hellenistic and Roman urban design.
3. **Cultural Hub**: It served as a strategic trade link between the Aegean and inner Anatolia, influencing the spread of Greek culture and commerce.
4. **Religious Center**: It managed the nearby *Sanctuary of Apollo at Didyma*, connected to the city by a 17-kilometer Sacred Way.

### Kings of Miletus

Miletus, one of the most powerful Ionian Greek cities, transitioned through several forms of leadership from its mythological founding to the historical era, shifting from kings to aristocracies and eventually tyrants. 

#### Mythological & Early Kings

The earliest rulers are often found in myth or the semi-legendary *Dark Ages*:

1. ***Anax***: A giant and son of Earth, said to be the first king when the **city was known as Anactoria**.

#### Famous Tyrants

In the **Archaic period (7th-5th c. BC)**, the city was famously governed by **tyrants**, often acting as **vassals** for empires like **Lydia or Persia**: 

1. ***Thrasybulus*** **(c. 610 BC)**: A powerful ally of *Periander of Corinth* who successfully defended Miletus against the Lydian king *Alyattes II*.

3. ***Aristagoras*** **(c. 500 BC)**: Son-in-law of *Histiaeus*; he instigated the **Ionian Revolt** against Persia, which led to the city's destruction in 494 BC.

#### Historical Governance

1. **Aristocracy/Oligarchy**: Following the fall of the Neleid kings, the city was ruled by an **elite group** for centuries, sometimes led by an aesymnetes (a mediator or elected dictator) like ***Epimenes***.
2. **Democracy**: After its liberation from Persia by ***Alexander the Great*** in **334 BC**, the city frequently adopted **democratic systems** modeled after Athens.
3. **Eponymous Archons**: In later periods, the city was led by an annual magistrate called a stephanephorus. This role became so ceremonial that at times even the god Apollo was *elected* to the office during times of crisis.

## Amphitres (8th-7th c. BC)

Amphitres (also known as Phitres) was a *semi-legendary figure* in the early history of the ancient Greek city-state of Miletus, often cited as its **first tyrant** or one of its **last kings**. 

Historical & Mythological Context

According to ancient accounts, Amphitres lived ***during the transition from monarchy to other forms*** of government in Miletus (roughly the late 8th or early 7th c. BC): 

1. **Rivalry for the Throne**: ***Amphitres and Leodamas*** were cousins and members of the royal *Neleid* family who both claimed the kingship.
2. **The Trial of War**: To resolve their dispute, the Milesians decided that the one who brought the most benefit to the city would rule. ***Leodamas*** led a successful campaign against the *Carystians*, while ***Amphitres*** failed in his expedition against the *Melians*.
3. **Murder and Tyranny**: Discontented with the result, ***Amphitres*** eventually **murdered** ***Leodamas*** during a festival for *Apollo* and seized power as a tyrant.
4. **Fall**: His rule was short-lived; the sons of *Leodamas* eventually sought revenge, **killing Amphitres** and ending the tyranny. This led to the appointment of an aesymnetes (an elected arbitrator) named ***Epimenes*** to restore order.

## Aeinautae (c. 600 BC)

The ***Aeinautae were a mercantile oligarchy*** in archaic Miletus whose name literally translates to **ever-voyagers** (αεί, *always and ναύται, *sailors*). They were known for ***conducting governmental business while aboard their ships*** at sea to remain undisturbed by the populace. Their *rule was eventually overturned by civil strife* (***stasis***), which ***led to the rise of individual tyrants***, most notably ***Thrasybulus***, who was a contemporary and ally of Periander of Corinth. 

Aeinautae  were *magistrates* at Miletus around 600 BC, consisting of the *chief men in the state*, who obtained the *supreme power* on the ***deposition of the tyrants, Thoas and Damasenor***. 


They were known for a ***unique method of political deliberation***: 

1. *The Process*: Whenever ***important state matters*** needed to be decided, the magistrates would ***board a ship*** and sail away from the coast.
2. *The Decision*: They remained at sea, weighing anchor until they had reached a final decision, only returning to land once their business was concluded.
3. *The Context*: This group ***consisted of the city's chief men*** who took power after the deposition of the tyrants Thoas and Damasenor.

Summary of Political Shift

1. *Predecessor*: Neleid Dynasty (Monarchy)
2. *The Aeinautae*: Mercantile Oligarchy
3. *Immediate Successor State*: Civil anarchy/Stasis
4. *Final Resolution*: Government by Landowning Aristocrats (established by Parian arbitrators) 

In Miletus, the **Aeinautae (Aeinaustae) were succeeded in power by tyrants.**

##  Histiaeus of Miletus (6th-5th c. BC)

Histiaeus (died c. 493 BC) was the Greek *tyrant of Miletus* under the Persian King *Darius I*. He is most famously known for instigating the **Ionian Revolt** against Persian rule through a creative and early method of steganography. 

There is ***no specific*** individual named in historical records as the ***immediate predecessor*** to ***Histiaeus*** as the tyrant of Miletus. Historical accounts, primarily from *Herodotus's Histories*, pick up his story when he ***was already established*** as the ruler ***under the Persian King*** Darius I around 513 BC

Key Historical Contributions

1. **The Bridge at the Danube**: During Darius's Scythian campaign (c. 513 BC), Histiaeus persuaded other Greek tyrants not to destroy the bridge over the Danube, ensuring the Persian army's safe return. Darius rewarded him with territory in Thrace.
2. **The Secret Message**: While being held as a *guest* (virtual prisoner) in the Persian capital of Susa, *Histiaeus* plotted a revolt to regain his freedom. He shaved the head of a trusted slave, tattooed a message on the scalp, and waited for the hair to regrow before sending the slave to his son-in-law, *Aristagoras*, in Miletus.
3. **Ionian Revolt**: The *message triggered the Ionian Revolt* (499–494 BC). *Histiaeus* eventually convinced *Darius* to let him return to the coast to *quell* the rebellion, but he was mistrusted by both sides.
4. **Death**: After the *revolt failed*, **Histiaeus turned to piracy**. He was eventually ***captured by the Persian*** *satrap Artaphernes* and ***crucified at Sardis***. His head was sent to *Darius*, who reportedly regretted the execution of his former servant.

##  Aristagoras of Miletus (6th-5th c. BC)

Aristagoras of Miletus (Ancient Greek: Ἀρισταγόρας ὁ Μιλήσιος), d. 497/496 BC, was the tyrant of the Ionian city of Miletus in the late 6th c. BC and early 5th c. BC. He acted as one of the instigators of the **Ionian Revolt** against the Persian Achaemenid Empire, which served as the **critical precursor to the Greco-Persian Wars**. He was the son-in-law of *Histiaeus* and was granted the tyranny of Miletus from him.

Key Historical Role

1. **The Naxos Expedition**: In 500 BC, *Aristagoras* persuaded the Persian satrap *Artaphernes* to fund an invasion of Naxos to restore exiled oligarchs. The four-month siege failed spectacularly, leaving *Aristagoras* deeply in debt and in danger of losing his position under the Persian King *Darius I*.
2. **Inciting Rebellion**: Fearing Persian reprisal, he **incited the Ionian Greek city-states to revolt against Persian rule in 499 BC**. To gain popular support, he formally abdicated his tyranny and established democracies across the region.
3. **Seeking Allies**: He travelled to mainland Greece to seek military aid. While the **Spartans refused him**, he successfully **convinced the Athenians and Eretrians** to send ships.
4. **Death in Thrace**: As the revolt began to falter against the Persian counter-offensive, *Aristagoras* fled Miletus for Thrace. He was eventually killed there by the Thracians while attempting to besiege a local town. 

Historical Reputation

According to *Herodotus*, *Aristagoras* was a self-serving and somewhat cowardly leader who plunged the Greek world into a massive conflict primarily to save his own skin after the Naxos failure. However, modern historians sometimes view him as a more complex figure who recognized the growing instability of Ionian life under the Achaemenid Empire.

## Histiaeus and Aristagoras 

were **the two central figures who incited the Ionian Revolt** (499–494 BC), the **first major uprising of Greek cities against the Persian Empire**. Their partnership combined political desperation with one of history's most famous methods of secret communication. 

Key Figures and Relationship

1. ***Histiaeus***: The Tyrant of Miletus who served King Darius the Great, ruled roughly between 518–514 BC. He served as a loyal *vassal* to the Persian King *Darius I*, famously securing a bridge for the King's retreat during the Scythian campaign. Although he saved Darius during a campaign, the King grew suspicious of his power and kept him in *honorable detention* at the Persian capital, Susa. While held *prisoner* at the Persian court in Susa, *Histiaeus* famously sent a secret message to his son-in-law, *Aristagoras*, by tattooing it on the shaved head of a trusted slave and waiting for the hair to grow back before sending him.
2. ***Aristagoras***: The son-in-law (and cousin) of *Histiaeus*. He was appointed acting tyrant of Miletus while *Histiaeus* was detained in Persia.

## Der Ionische Aufstand (ca. 500–494 v. Chr.)

Der Ionische Aufstand (ca. 500–494 v. Chr.) war eine Rebellion ***griechischer Städte*** in Kleinasien und Zypern ***gegen die persische Herrschaft***, angeführt von Milet unter *Aristagoras*. Er *löste die Perserkriege aus*, da Athen und Eretria die Aufständischen unterstützten. 

Wichtige Fakten zum Ionischen Aufstand:

1. **Auslöser**: Die ***Unzufriedenheit*** mit den von Persien eingesetzten Tyrannen und der gescheiterte Angriff auf Naxos führten dazu, dass *Aristagoras von Milet* den Abfall von der persischen Vorherrschaft forcierte.
2. **Verlauf**: Der Aufstand **breitete sich** ab 500/499 v. Chr. **rasch aus**. Ein Höhepunkt war die Unterstützung durch Athen, die zur Zerstörung der persischen Satrapenstadt Sardeis führte.
3. **Ende**: Die Perser unter *Dareios I.* reagierten mit militärischer Härte. In der entscheidenden **Seeschlacht bei Lade** (494 v. Chr.) wurden die Ionier besiegt, und Milet wurde zerstört.
4. **Folgen**: Die Unterstützung der Aufständischen durch Athen veranlasste den persischen Großkönig *Dareios I.* zu einer *Strafexpedition gegen das griechische Festland*, was den **Beginn der eigentlichen Perserkriege** markiert.

***Epimenides*** (7th-6th c. BC) *Epimenides of Knossos* was a semi-mythical Greek seer, philosopher, and poet from Crete who flourished in the 6th-7th c. BC. He is most famous in modern logic for the *Epimenides Paradox* and in theology for being quoted in the New Testament. 

Key Biographical Legends

1. *The Miraculous Sleep*: Legend says that while looking for a lost sheep, Epimenides fell asleep in a cave sacred to Zeus for 57 years. He allegedly awoke with the gift of prophecy and supernatural wisdom.
2. *Purification of Athens*: He was reportedly invited to Athens (c. 596 BC) to perform religious rites to purify the city from a plague following the Cylonian sacrilege.
3. *Extraordinary Longevity*: Ancient sources, such as Diogenes Laärtius, claimed he lived to be 157 or even 299 years old. 

The Epimenides Paradox

Epimenides is credited with the statement: 

>"All Cretans are liars". 

1. *Logical Problem*: Since Epimenides was himself a Cretan, if the statement is true, then he is a liar, which would mean the statement is false.
2. *Modern Interpretation*: While often grouped with the *Liar Paradox*, logicians note that it is technically not a strict paradox; if at least one Cretan tells the truth, the statement *All Cretans are liars* is simply a false statement made by a liar. 

Biblical References

The ***Apostle Paul*** alludes to *Epimenides* twice in the New Testament:

1. **Titus 1:12**: Paul explicitly quotes the *Cretans are always liars* line, referring to *Epimenides* as *a prophet of their own*.
2. **Acts 17:28**: During his speech at the Areopagus, Paul says, "For in him we live and move and have our being," which is believed to be *a quote* from *Epimenides'* poem *Cretica*.

## Evagoras of Lindos (7th BC)

*Evagoras of Lindos* was a 7th-century BC Greek citizen of Lindos, *Rhodes*, known primarily as the *father* of ***Cleobulus of Lindos***, one of the legendary **Seven Sages** of Greece. ## Golden Age of Greece (c. 480–404 BC)

The Golden Age of Greece, primarily centered in Athens during the 5th c. BC (c. 480–404 BC), was a transformative period of **peace**(!), **prosperity**, and **unparalleled cultural achievement**. Often called the *Age of Pericles* after the statesman who led Athens at its peak, this era *laid the foundations* for *Western civilization* in politics, philosophy, art, and science.

## Peloponnesian War (460-445 / 431-404 BC)

After defeating the Second Persian invasion of Greece in the year 480 BC, Athens led the coalition of Greek city-states that continued the **Greco-Persian Wars** (499-449 BC) with attacks on Persian territories in the Aegean and Ionia. By mid-century, the Persians had been driven out of the Aegean and had ceded control of vast territories to Athens. In the wake of the departure of the Persians from Greece, Sparta sent ambassadors to persuade Athens not to reconstruct their walls, but was rebuffed. Without the walls, Athens would have been defenceless against a land attack and subject to Spartan control.

The **First Peloponnesian War** (460–445 BC) was fought between Sparta as the leaders of the Peloponnesian League and Sparta's other allies, most notably Thebes, and the Delian League led by Athens with support from *Argos*. 

The First Peloponnesian War ended in an arrangement between Sparta and Athens, which was ratified by the **Thirty Years' Peace** (winter of 446–445 BC). According to the provisions of this peace treaty, both sides maintained the main parts of their empires. 

**The Peloponnesian War** (431–404 BC) was a transformative conflict in ancient Greece fought between the **Athenian Empire (Delian League)** and the **Peloponnesian League led by Sparta**. Initially inconclusive, the *intervention* of the **Persian Empire** in *support of* **Sparta** in 413 BC allowed the Spartan coalition to decisively defeat Athens. It *reshaped* the Greek world, ending the *Golden Age of Greece* and establishing Spartan hegemony while leaving the region vulnerable to later conquest by *Macedon*. 

Sources

The primary sources for the *Peloponnesian War* (431–404 BC) are dominated by contemporary accounts from Athenian historians, supplemented by archaeological findings and later classical works. 

Primary Contemporary Sources

1. ***Thucydides***: His *History of the Peloponnesian War* is the **most significant source**. An Athenian general himself, he began writing at the start of the conflict to create a *possession for all time*. His work is famous for its objective tone and detailed speeches (like Pericles’ Funeral Oration), though it ends abruptly in 411 BC.
2. ***Xenophon***: His *Hellenica* serves as the **sequel to Thucydides**, picking up the narrative from 411 BC and covering the final years of the war through the Spartan hegemony.
3. ***Aristophanes***: A comic playwright whose works, such as *Peace*, *The Acharnians*, and *Lysistrata*, provide contemporary—albeit satirical—insight into the social atmosphere, war fatigue, and civilian life in Athens.
4. **Epigraphy and Archaeology**: Physical evidence includes the *Athenian Tribute Lists*, which track the finances of the Delian League. Archaeological remains, such as the *walls of Amphipolis* and the grave of the Spartan general *Brasidas*, provide tangible verification of specific events. 

Later Ancient Sources (Secondary-Primary)

These authors lived centuries later but utilized lost contemporary records (like the universal history by *Ephorus*). 

1. ***Diodorus Siculus***: Books 12-13 of his *Library of World History* provide a chronological account that sometimes offers a different perspective or more detail on non-Athenian theaters than ***Thucydides***.
2. ***Plutarch***: His *Parallel Lives* contains biographies of key figures such as *Pericles*, *Nicias*, *Alcibiades*, and *Lysander*, focusing on character and morality while preserving details not found elsewhere.
3. **The Oxyrhynchus Historian**: Fragments of a lost history (referred to as the *Hellenica Oxyrhynchia*) provide an alternative, highly regarded account of the war's later stages. 

Modern Scholarly Works

1. ***Donald Kagan***: Author of the definitive four-volume series *A New History of the Peloponnesian War*, widely considered the **standard modern reference**.
2. ***Victor Davis Hanson***: Known for *A War Like No Other*, which focuses heavily on the *military-technical* and tactical aspects of the conflict. 

Key Phases of the War

Historians typically divide the **27-year conflict** into three distinct periods: 

1. **Ten Years' War or The Archidamian War (431-421 BC)**: Named after Spartan King Archidamus II, this phase was characterized by annual Spartan invasions of Attica and Athenian naval raids on the Peloponnese. It ended in a stalemate with the Peace of Nicias.
2. **The Sicilian Expedition (415-413 BC)**: Athens launched a massive naval assault against the city of Syracuse in Sicily. The campaign was a total disaster; the entire Athenian force was destroyed or enslaved, severely crippling their military power.
3. **Second War, The Decelean (or Ionian) War (413-404 BC)**: In the final phase, Sparta received financial support from the Achaemenid Empire (Persia) to build a fleet and encourage revolts in Athenian territories. **Sparta, now allied** to the ***Persian Empire***, **broke the peace** in 413 BC and **began the final phase of the war**. While Persia captured the Athenian cities in Asia Minor; Sparta, led by Lysander, built a Persian-financed fleet to break Athens's naval superiority.

Causes and Conflict Dynamics

1. *Imperial Tension*: The primary underlying cause was the growth of Athenian power and the fear it inspired in Sparta (the *Thucydides Trap*).The nearly 50 years before the War had been marked by the development of Athens as a major power in the Mediterranean world.
2. *Immediate Triggers*: Conflicts over city-states like Corcyra, Potidaea, and the Megarian Decree—an Athenian trade embargo—pushed the leagues into open war.
3. *Strategic Contrast*: Athens relied on its superior navy and the Long Walls for protection and supply, while Sparta relied on its professional hoplite army.
4. **The Plague of Athens**: In **430 BC**, a devastating plague struck the overcrowded city of Athens, **killing roughly one-third** of its population, including its leader, *Pericles*. 

Result and Aftermath

The war ended in 404 BC[^3] after the Spartan admiral *Lysander* destroyed the Athenian fleet at the *Battle of Aegospotami* (405 BC). 

Surrender Terms

1. Athens was forced to *dismantle* its Long *Walls*,
2. *reduce its fleet* to just 12 ships
3. *accept* a pro-Spartan oligarchy known as *the Thirty Tyrants*.

Long-term Impact: 

While **Sparta emerged as the leading power**, the war's economic and human costs weakened all Greek city-states, eventually *allowing Philip II of Macedon* to unify them under his rule. 

[^3]: A symbolic peace treaty was signed by the mayors of modern Athens and Sparta on March 12, 1996, officially *ending* the ancient conflict nearly *2400 years later*.

## The Great Plague of Athens (430-426 BC) 

The **Plague of 430 BC** was a devastating, contagious epidemic that struck Athens during the early years of the *Peloponnesian War*, lasting *intermittently* **until 426 BC**. It killed an estimated 75000–100000 people—roughly 25% to 33% of the population—including leader *Pericles*, causing profound social, moral, and military decline. During the Peloponnesian War, this disease decimated the population, caused social chaos, and **contributed to** the **defeat of Athens by Sparta**.

Key Details

1. **Origin and Spread**: The disease is believed to have originated in *sub-Saharan Africa*, traveling through Egypt and Libya before *entering Athens via the port of Piraeus*. Overcrowding within the city walls during the Spartan siege accelerated its spread.
2. **Symptoms**: According to the detailed eyewitness account by historian and survivor *Thucydides*, symptoms included violent fever, red/inflamed eyes, throat/tongue bleeding, sneezing, coughing, and, in advanced stages, severe diarrhea and skin pustules.
3. **Impact**: The plague **severely weakened Athens' military** capabilities, resulting in the loss of thousands of heavy-armed soldiers and cavalry. It severely impacted the city's morale, leading to a breakdown in social order and religious observance.
4. **Scientific Uncertainty**: The exact cause remains debated. While historical theories ranged from bubonic plague to smallpox or measles, modern studies have suggested possibilities including typhoid fever (based on 2006 DNA analysis of, though, this is still debated) or an ancient, extinct strain of a viral infection.
5. **Primary Source**: Our primary understanding of the plague comes from ***Thucydides***, who, as a doctor-like *observer*, provided a *meticulous*, clinical description of the symptoms and the psychological effects on the population.

## The fall of Macedonia (146 BC)

In 146 BC, the **fall of Macedonia and the broader Greek world** was finalised by the *Roman Republic* through two distinct but related events. 

The Fall of Macedonia

Macedonia was officially **annexed and converted into a Roman province** in 146 BC. 

1. **Context**: Following the defeat of the Macedonian pretender *Andriscus* at the second Battle of Pydna (148 BC), the Roman general *Quintus Caecilius Metellus Macedonicus* crushed the last attempt to *restore the monarchy*.
2. **Result**: The former kingdom was formally *reorganised* into the province of Macedonia, ending centuries of Macedonian sovereignty. 

The Fall of Athens and Southern Greece

While Macedonia fell earlier, the rest of mainland Greece (including Athens) was brought under direct Roman hegemony in 146 BC following the *Achaean War*. 

1. **The Battle of Corinth (146 BC)**: The Roman general *Lucius Mummius* defeated the Achaean League and utterly destroyed the city of Corinth as an object lesson to the Greeks.
2. **Status of Athens**: Unlike Corinth, Athens was *not destroyed*. Due to its cultural prestige, it was granted the status of a **free city** (civitas libera) or allied city (civitas foederata). While it lost true political independence, it remained a thriving **cultural and educational hub for the Roman elite**.
3. **Later Revolt (88 BC)**: Athens would later **lose its privileged status after revolting** against Rome during the *Mithridatic Wars*, leading to a **brutal sack** by the Roman general **Sulla in 86 BC**. 

Summary of Outcomes (146 BC)

1. Macedonia: Formally annexed as a Roman province.
2. Achaean League: Dissolved; southern Greece placed under the supervision of the Macedonian governor.
3. Corinth: Razed to the ground; its population enslaved.
4. Athens: Spared destruction but brought under Roman rule as a *free city*. 

The **primary location** associated with ancient Greek **philosophers** is **Athens**, which served as the intellectual heart of the Greek world. However, early thinkers—known as the **Pre-Socratics**—**originated** from various city-states across the Mediterranean, including **Ionia** (modern-day Turkey), southern **Italy**, and **Sicily**.
