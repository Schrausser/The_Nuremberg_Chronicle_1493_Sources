# Sources

---

There are **no original manuscripts** (the physical documents written by the authors themselves) of ancient Greek philosophers that **have survived** the test of time. What remains today are later copies, fragments, and *testimonia* (reports by other ancient authors). 

The sources we rely on for Greek philosophy fall into several distinct categories:

### Intact Works (The Direct Tradition) 

Very few philosophers have works that survived in a *complete* form through **continuous hand-copying** by medieval monks and scribes. 

1. ***Plato***: He is the first philosopher whose **entire body** of work—*primarily his Dialogues*—**has survived** intact.
2. ***Aristotle***: A **large corpus** of his *esoteric* works (lecture notes and treatises) **survived**, though his more polished *exoteric* dialogues were lost.
3. ***Epicurus***: While **most** of his vast output **is lost**, three letters and his *Principal Doctrines* survived because they were preserved in the works of Diogenes Laërtius.
4. **Later Thinkers**: Extensive works exist for ***Plotinus***, ***Sextus Empiricus***, and Roman-era Stoics like ***Marcus Aurelius*** and ***Epictetus***. 

### Doxographies and Biographies

Much of what we know about early philosophers comes from *doxographers*—ancient writers who summarized the *opinions* (doxai) of their predecessors. 

1. ***Diogenes Laërtius***: His 3rd c. AD work, **Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers**, is the **single most important source** for the biographies and doctrines of philosophers **from Thales to Epicurus**.
2. ***Aristotle and Plato***: They are primary sources for the thinkers who came before them (the Pre-Socratics), often quoting them to critique their views.
3. ***Sextus Empiricus***: A major source for Skepticism and summaries of other schools. 

### Fragments and Papyri

For the Pre-Socratics (like ***Heraclitus, Parmenides***, and ***Democritus***), no complete books exist. Their philosophy survives only as: 

1. **Indirect Fragments**: **Quotations embedded** in the works of later authors (like ***Plutarch, Simplicius***, or ***Clement of Alexandria***).
2. **Carbonized Papyri**: The **Herculaneum Papyri**, charred by the eruption of Vesuvius, contain a library of ***Epicurean*** texts.
3. **The Derveni Papyrus**: The **oldest surviving European manuscript** (c. 340 BC), which provides insight into **early Orphic and physical philosophy**.

## Hesiod (8th-7th BC)

Hesiod (altgriechisch ; * vor 700 v. Chr. vermutlich in Askra in Böotien in der Nähe von Thespiai) war ein griechischer Dichter, der als **Ackerbauer und Viehhalter** lebte. 
***Hesiod*** (fl. c. 700 BC) was an ancient Greek poet considered, **alongside Homer**, to be the **foundational figure of Western literature** and Greek religious tradition. Unlike the impersonal epics of Homer, Hesiod's work features a distinct, often curmudgeonly persona acting as the first *individual* author in the Western canon who used his own life experiences to frame his poetry. 

*Hesiods Werke* sind, *neben* der *Ilias* und *Odyssee* von *Homer*, die *Hauptquellen* für unser heutiges Wissen über die griechische Mythologie und Mythographie sowie das Alltagsleben seiner Zeit.

In the context of ancient Greek literature, ***Dius*** is identified as the ***father of the epic poet Hesiod***. 

Key details regarding Dius and his family include:

1. **Origin & Migration**: *Dius* was originally a *seafaring trader* and *farmer* from Cyme in Aeolis (modern-day Turkey). He eventually moved to continental Greece, settling in the village of Ascra near Mount Helicon in Boeotia, which he described as a *miserable hamlet*.
2. **Family**: *Dius* was the ***son of Apelles***. He had two sons: *Hesiod*, the famous poet, and *Perses*, who is the primary subject of Hesiod's instructional poem, Works and Days.
3. **Legacy in Literature**: Hesiod's Works and Days is largely framed as a *series of moral and agricultural instructions to Perses* following a legal dispute over their father's inheritance. *Dius's* struggle with poverty and his relocation provide the biographical backdrop for Hesiod's themes of hard work and justice.
4. **Etymological Note**: Some scholars suggest that the name *Dius* may have originated from a reinterpretation of the Greek phrase Dion genos, meaning *descended from Zeus*, which later came to be treated as a proper name for the poet's father. 

In ancient Greek tradition, several figures were considered the predecessors of Hesiod, depending on whether one looks at historical belief, literary chronology, or cultural lineage. 

Traditional and Mythological Predecessors

According to 5th and early 4th c. BC Greeks, the **oldest poets** were often listed in a specific chronological order: 

1. ***Orpheus***: The legendary musician and prophet of the Orphic cults was widely regarded as the **earliest of all poets**.
2. ***Musaeus***: A semi-mythical polymath and poet, often described as a *disciple or son of Orpheus*, who was believed to have lived before both *Hesiod and Homer*. 

The **Homer vs. Hesiod** Debate

The chronological relationship between Homer and Hesiod was a subject of intense debate in antiquity: 

1. *Homer as Predecessor*: Philosophers like *Xenophanes* and later critics like *Aristarchus of Samothrace* argued that *Homer* was the earlier poet. Modern linguistic analysis often supports this, suggesting Hesiod's Greek is more *evolved* than Homer's.
2. *Hesiod as Predecessor*: Some early historians, such as *Ephorus*, claimed *Hesiod* was actually *a generation older than Homer*. 

Literary and Cultural Influences

1. **Near Eastern Traditions**: Scholars have identified parallels between Hesiod's Theogony and much older Near Eastern myths, such as the *Babylonian En?ma Eli?* and the *Hittite Kingship in Heaven* cycle.
2. **The Muses**: In his own work, *Hesiod* identifies the *Muses* as his direct source of inspiration, claiming they appeared to him while he was shepherding on Mount Helicon and *taught* him his song.
3. ***Dius***: Biographically, Hesiod's father, *Dius*, is his literal predecessor; a *seafaring trader* from Cyme who migrated to the village of Ascra. 

Major Works

Only two complete poems are universally accepted as his authentic work, though others are attributed to the *Hesiodic corpus*: 

1. **Theogony**: A massive synthesis of local *myths* into a single narrative explaining the origins of the universe and the genealogy of the gods. It chronicles the violent *succession myth*," where generations of gods (**Uranus, then Cronus**) are **overthrown** by ***their children***, ending with the permanent **rule of Zeus**.
2. **Works and Days**: A didactic (instructive) poem ostensibly written to ***advise his brother, Perses***, following a legal *dispute over their father's inheritance*. It serves as **a farmer's almanac**, offering practical agricultural advice and moral lessons on the value of hard work and justice. 

Key Themes and Myths

1. **The Five Ages of Man**: Hesiod describes the *decline of humanity* through the Golden, Silver, Bronze, and Heroic ages, ending in the miserable *Iron Age* his own time.
2. **Pandora and Prometheus**: He provides the *earliest* written accounts of *Prometheus stealing fire* and the creation of *Pandora*, who opened a jar releasing all the world's evils.
3. **Didacticism**: Known as the *father of didactic poetry*, his work focuses on teaching morality, farming techniques, and even early economic concepts like resource scarcity. 

Personal Life

Most biographical details are drawn directly from his poetry: 

1. **Origins**: Born in Ascra, a village in Boeotia he famously described as bad in winter, sultry in summer, and good at no time.
2. **Inspiration**: He claimed the *Muses* appeared to him while he was shepherding on Mount Helicon, gifting him a laurel staff and a divine voice.
3. **Competition**: He once won a poetry contest in Chalcis, Euboea, receiving a tripod as a prize. Later legends even imagined a contest between Hesiod and Homer. 

Mythological Succession

 **Succession Myth in the Theogony**, where power passes through three generations of divine rulers: 

1. **Ouranos** (Uranus): The primordial sky father, *overthrown by his son*.
2. **Kronos** (Cronus): The *leader of the Titans*, who castrated Ouranos and was later overthrown.
3. **Zeus**: The final successor and king of the Olympians, who established *permanent order after* the *Titanomachy*. 

Literary Successors

While no single school of *Hesiodic* poets survived him in the way the Homeridae claimed descent from Homer, his influence on later literature was profound: 

1. ***Theognis of Megara***: Often paired with *Hesiod* in classical studies, *Theognis* used the didactic elegiac couplet to provide moral and social advice, mirroring the ethical instruction found in Works and Days.
2. ***Aratus***: His astronomical poem, *Phaenomena*, is considered a direct successor to Hesiod's didactic tradition, focusing on the stars and weather signs.
3. ***Virgil***: In his *Georgics, Virgil* explicitly acknowledges *Hesiod* as his model, aiming to do for Roman agriculture what *Hesiod* did for the Greek farmer.
4. ***Ovid***: In *Metamorphoses, Ovid* adapted and expanded many of *Hesiod's* myths, such as the *Five Ages of Man* and the *origin of the gods*. 

Philosophical Successors

Hesiod's **Theogony laid the groundwork** for **early Greek science and philosophy** by attempting to organize the cosmos into a logical, genealogical structure: 

1. **The Pre-Socratics**: Philosophers like ***Thales, Anaximander, and Anaxagoras*** were influenced by Hesiod's *cosmogony* (the birth of the universe from Chaos) as they sought to explain the world through natural elements rather than divine personifications.
3. **The Peripatetics**: Hellenistic followers of *Aristotle*, such as ***Dicaearchus and Praxiphanes***, integrated *Hesiod's* works into their historical and literary criticisms.

## Homer (8th-7th BC)

(altgriechisch Ὅμηρος Hómēros, Betonung im Deutschen: Homḗr) gilt traditionell als Autor der **Ilias** und der **Odyssee** und damit als **frühester Dichter des Abendlandes**. 
Weder sein Geburtsort noch das Datum seiner Geburt oder das seines Todes sind zweifelsfrei bekannt. Es ist nicht einmal sicher, dass es **Homer überhaupt gab** (siehe auch **Homerische Frage**). 
Kontrovers diskutiert wird die Frage, in welcher Epoche er gelebt haben soll. 

In the context of ancient Greek literature, the identity of a single *predecessor* to Homer is complex because Homer himself is often considered the *first author of the Western tradition*. However, historical and literary perspectives offer several candidates: 

1. ***Hesiod***: Traditionally, Hesiod is the most frequently cited contemporary or predecessor to Homer. 
- The Debate: Ancient Greeks often argued over which poet came first. Historians like Ephorus of Cyme argued that Hesiod was older than Homer.
- Literary Contest: A famous ancient narrative, The Contest of Homer and Hesiod (Certamen), depicts the two competing in a poetic duel, often implying they were at least contemporaries.
- Linguistic Evidence: Modern linguistic analysis sometimes suggests Hesiod's Greek is more *evolved*, potentially making him younger, though this remains a point of scholarly debate. 

2. **Mythological Predecessors**: Ancient traditions often listed legendary bards as preceding both Homer and Hesiod: 
- ***Orpheus*** and ***Musaeus***: Greeks in the late 5th century BC generally considered *Orpheus and Musaeus* to be the **oldest poets**, *followed by Hesiod* and then *Homer*.
- ***Phemius***: In some biographical traditions, Homer is said to have studied under a *bard named Phemius in Cyme*. 

3. **The Oral Tradition**: Modern scholars, following the work of *Milman Parry* (), argue that *Homer* was **not a single individual** starting from scratch **but the culmination** of a centuries-old oral-formulaic tradition. 
- **Dark Age Bards**: This tradition survived the Greek Dark Ages, with unrecorded singers passing down myths and heroic tales through fixed phrases (formulae).
- **Mycenaean Roots**: Linguistic clues, such as the use of the vanished letter digamma, suggest that *parts* of the *Homeric epics* date back to the *Mycenaean era* (c. 1600-1100 BC), long before the poems were written down. 
- ***Herodot*** schätzte, dass Homer 400 Jahre vor ihm gelebt haben müsse, dies entspräche in etwa der Zeit um 850 v. Chr. 
- Andere historische Quellen legen das Wirken Homers in die Zeit des Trojanischen Krieges, der traditionell etwa um 1200 v. Chr. datiert wird. 
- Heutzutage stimmt die Forschung weitestgehend darin überein, dass Homer, **wenn es ihn gab**, etwa in der zweiten Hälfte des 8. Jahrhunderts und/oder in der ersten Hälfte des 7. Jahrhunderts v. Chr. gelebt hat.

Key Literary Successors

Scholars, such as *William Cranston Lawton* in his comprehensive study **The Successors of Homer**, identify several groups and individuals as the primary successors: 

1. ***Hesiod***: Often considered a contemporary or immediate successor, his works like Theogony and Works and Days shifted the focus from heroic epics to theology and didactic (instructive) poetry.
2. **The Cyclic Poets**: Authors of the *Epic Cycle*, a collection of lost epics that filled the narrative gaps of the Trojan War not covered by *Homer* (e.g., the *Cypria and Aethiopis*).
3. ***Authors of the Homeric Hymns***: A collection of 33 *anonymous* ancient Greek hymns celebrating individual gods, written in the same dialect and dactylic hexameter as the Homeric epics.
4. ***Pindar***: A *lyric poet* who adapted Homeric myths into choral odes, often focusing on the divine origins of victors in the *Panhellenic* games.
5. **The Great Dramatists**: Writers such as ***Aeschylus***, ***Sophocles***, and ***Euripides*** are viewed as successors because their tragedies frequently used *Homeric themes* and characters as their primary source material. 

Influence on Later Western Literature

Beyond ancient Greece, many later authors are described as *Homer's* spiritual or stylistic successors due to their work in the epic genre: 

1. ***Virgil***: His Aeneid was specifically designed as a Roman successor to the Iliad and Odyssey.
2. ***John Milton***: Attempted to achieve *Homeric* scale in English with Paradise Lost.
3. ***James Joyce***: His modern masterpiece Ulysses is a direct, structural successor to the Odyssey

## Xenophanes of Colophon (c. 570-478 BC)

Xenophanes of Colophon (/zəˈnɒfəniːz/ zə-NOF-ə-neez; Ancient Greek: Ξενοφάνης ὁ Κολοφώνιος [ksenopʰánɛːs ho kolopʰɔ̌ːnios]; c. 570-478 BC) was a Greek philosopher, theologian, poet, and *critic of Homer*. 
He was born in Ionia and **travelled throughout the Greek-speaking world** in early classical antiquity.

Core Philosophical Contributions

1. **Critique of Anthropomorphism**: He famously argued that humans create gods in their own image, noting that Ethiopians depict gods as black and snub-nosed, while Thracians see them as blue-eyed and red-haired. He satirically added that if horses or oxen could draw, they would depict their gods as horses or oxen.
2. **Theological Rationalism**: Xenophanes proposed a *one greatest god* who is unlike mortals in body or mind, remains motionless, and *shakes all things by the thought of his mind*. This is often seen as a **precursor to monotheism** or the **Unmoved Mover of Aristotle**.
3. **Epistemology & Skepticism**: He was one of the first to distinguish between certain *knowledge* (saphes) and mere *opinion* (dokos). He argued that while humans can never know the *clear truth* about the gods or the universe, they can gradually discover *better* beliefs through inquiry.
4. **Natural Philosophy**: He suggested that all things are composed of **earth and water**. Notably, he used the *discovery of fossilised sea creatures* in mountains as empirical evidence to *conclude* that the *earth was once covered by the sea*. 

Life & Legacy

Born in Colophon (modern-day Turkey), he spent over 60 years as an itinerant rhapsode, wandering through Greece and Sicily after *fleeing the Persian invasion*. 
He was a sharp social critic who prioritised wisdom over athletic strength, famously disparaging the excessive rewards given to Olympic victors. 
While *often linked to the Eleatic School* as a teacher of *Parmenides*, modern scholars often view him as a distinct thinker whose influence reached *Plato* and the later Skeptics.

## Thales (c. 624-548)

the ***earliest*** *Western philosopher*, before him, the Greeks explained the origin and nature of the world through *myths* of anthropomorphic *gods* and *heroes*.

*Thales of Miletus* (c. 624-545 BC) is traditionally regarded as the founder of the **Milesian school** and was succeeded in his philosophical lineage by *Anaximander* (c. 610-546 BC) and *Anaximenes* (c. 586-526 BC)

The Milesian Succession

The *Succession of Philosophers*  is a historiographical tradition that tracks the master-pupil relationships of ancient Greek thinkers. Thales' primary succession within the Ionian tradition is as follows: 

1. ***Thales (Founder)***: Proposed water as the primary substance (arche).
2. ***Anaximander (Successor/Pupil)***: Succeeded Thales and proposed the apeiron (the indefinite or infinite) as the fundamental principle.
3. ***Anaximenes (Pupil of Anaximander)***: Succeeded Anaximander and identified air as the primary substance. 

Lineage of Influence

This chain, often referred to as the Ionian Succession, continued through several generations: 

1. ***Anaxagoras***: **Transferred the Milesian school from Ionia to Athens**.
2. ***Archelaus*** (5th c. BC): Succeeded *Anaxagoras* and was reportedly the teacher of *Socrates*. 

Cosmological model

Worldview of *Thales* and pupil *Anaximander*.
*Thales* thought the Earth must be a *flat disk* or mound of land and dirt which is *floating* in an expanse of water.
*Heraclitus Homericus* states that *Thales* drew his conclusion from seeing moist substance turn into air, slime and earth. It seems likely that *Thales* viewed the land as coming from the water on which it floated and the oceans that surround it, perhaps inspired by observing silt deposits.

He thought the *stars* were *balls of earth on fire*. He seemed to correctly gather that the moon reflects the Sun's light. A crater on the Moon is named in his honor.

The *Pythagorean Succession* refers to the line of followers and schools of thought that carried on the teachings of Pythagoras after his death in approximately 495 BC. 

Key Successors and Influencers

Following *Pythagoras*, the school's leadership and intellectual development were maintained by several key figures:

1. ***Theano of Croton***: Tradition identifies her as *Pythagoras' wife*. She, along with her two daughters, is often credited with managing the Pythagorean School after his death and writing treatises on mathematics and medicine.
2. ***Philolaus of Croton*** (c. 470-390 BC): Regarded as the *primary* figure who developed *Pythagorean cosmology* into a more *mathematical* direction. He is often cited as the first to write down the secret Pythagorean teachings.
3. ***Archytas of Tarentum*** (fl. 400-350 BC): A statesman and mathematician who furthered the scientific rigor of the tradition and was a friend of *Plato*.
4. ***Telauges***: Alleged to be the *son* of Pythagoras, he is sometimes listed as a direct successor in various historical accounts of the *Italian School*. 

The Intellectual *Italian* Lineage

Ancient biographers like *Diogenes Laertius* traced a specific *Italian* succession that linked Pythagoras to later major philosophical movements: 

1. ***Pherekydes*** (*Pythagoras'* teacher)
2. ***Pythagoras***
3. ***Telauges***
4. ***Xenophanes***, ***Parmenides***, and ***Zeno of Elea*** (The *Eleatic School*)
5. ***Leucippus*** and ***Democritus*** (The *Atomists*)
6. ***Epicurus***

Impact on Later Philosophy

The *succession* eventually moved underground as the original schools were suppressed during political upheavals in Italy. However, its influence was immortalised through: 

1. ***Plato***: Who integrated many Pythagorean concepts such as the *immortality of the soul* and the *mathematical nature of the universe* into his own philosophy.
2. **Neopythagoreanism**: A revival of these ideas in the 1st c. BC that influenced early Christian and Renaissance thought.

## Anaxagoras (c. 499-428 BC)

Anaxagoras (; * um 499 v. Chr., wahrscheinlich in Klazomenai; + 428 v. Chr., wahrscheinlich in Lampsakos) war ein griechischer Philosoph (Vorsokratiker) aus Klazomenai in Kleinasien. Sein nur in *Fragmenten* und **hauptsächlich von Aristoteles überliefertes** philosophisches Denken wird als Zusammenführung der Ansätze *Heraklits* und der *Eleaten* gedeutet. Mit *Anaxagoras* gelangte die *ionische Aufklärung* nach Athen, denn dort verbrachte er die wichtigsten Jahrzehnte seines Lebens und stand dem leitenden Staatsmann *Perikles* als philosophischer Lehrer und Berater nahe. Auch der Tragödiendichter *Euripides* () ließ sich von ihm in das philosophische Denken und Forschen einführen. Als Mathematiker beschäftigte er sich hauptsächlich mit der *Quadratur des Kreises*.

The relationship between the playwright *Euripides* and the philosopher *Anaxagoras* is one of the most famous intellectual pairings of 5th c. BC Athens. *Euripides* is traditionally described as a *pupil* or *nursling* of *Anaxagoras*, and his tragedies are often seen as a *poetic vehicle* for the philosopher's radical *scientific* and *rationalist* ideas. 

The Intellectual Connection

*Euripides'* work frequently *mirrors* *Anaxagoras’* theories on the nature of the universe:

1. **Scientific Rationalism**: *Anaxagoras* was notorious for claiming the sun was a *red-hot mass of stone* rather than a god. *Euripides* *echoed* this in his plays, once calling the sun a *gold clod of earth*.
2. **Cosmogony**: *Anaxagoras* taught that the world began as a mixture of all things until *Mind* (*Nous*) set it in motion. This influence is visible in *Euripidean* fragments (such as those from ***Melanippe the Wise***) that *describe* the original *unity of heaven and earth* before they were separated.
3. **Skepticism and Humanism**: Both men were viewed as subversives who challenged traditional Olympian religion. This led to a shared reputation for *impiety*—*Anaxagoras* was eventually exiled from Athens for his views, and *Euripides’* plays often portrayed the gods as cruel or arbitrary.

## Euripides (c. 484-406 BC)

 was the last of the **three great tragic dramatists** of classical Athens, following **Aeschylus** and **Sophocles**. He is celebrated for his *psychological realism*, complex female characters, and a skeptical approach to traditional Greek mythology that made his work feel more *modern* than that of his predecessors. 

Key Characteristics & Innovations

1. *Psychological Realism*: Unlike the idealized heroes of earlier tragedy, Euripides portrayed mythical figures as ordinary people in extraordinary circumstances, focusing on their inner motives and flaws.
2. *Portrayal of Women*: He gave unprecedented prominence and complexity to female characters, leading some modern scholars to view him as a *prototype feminist*, though this remains a subject of academic debate.
3. *Deus ex Machina*: He frequently used the *god from the machine* (a god appearing at the end of a play to resolve the plot) to provide closure to messy human conflicts.
4. *Intellectual Skepticism*: His plays often questioned the morality of the gods and reflected the influence of contemporary thinkers like *Protagoras* and *Socrates*. 

Notable Extant Works

Of his estimated 92 plays, 18 or 19 have survived more or less complete—more than those of Aeschylus and Sophocles combined. 

1. **Medea** (431 BC): A powerful study of a woman's ruthless revenge after being abandoned by her husband, Jason.
2. **Hippolytus** (428 BC): A tragedy of fatal passion and divine vengeance involving *Phaedra* and her stepson.
3. **The Trojan Women** (415 BC): A bleak and powerful indictment of the cruelties of war.
4. **Cyclops** (408 BC): The only fully extant example of a satyr play, a comic and grotesque genre performed alongside tragedies,  retelling of Odysseus’s famous encounter with the one-eyed giant *Polyphemus* from Book 9 of *Homer’s Odyssey*.
5. **The Bacchae** (405 BC): Regarded as his *masterpiece*, it explores the conflict between rational order and the primal, irrational power of the god *Dionysus*.

Life and Legacy

Euripides was born on the island of Salamis. Although he was less successful than his rivals during his lifetime—winning only five first prizes in competitions—his popularity surged after his death. He spent his final years in the court of King *Archelaus of Macedonia*, where he died in 406 BC. His influence is seen throughout Western literature, from the New Comedy of the Hellenistic period to the works of *Shakespeare*, *Ibsen*, and *Shaw*.

## Zeno of Elea (c. 490-430 BC)

Zeno of Elea (c. 490–430 BC) was a pre-Socratic Greek philosopher from the colony of Elea in southern Italy. A prominent member of the **Eleatic School**, he was the **primary student of Parmenides** and is best known for his series of ingenious *paradoxes* designed to support his teacher's theory of monism—the belief that reality is a single, unchanging entity and that motion and plurality are merely illusions. 

Key Paradoxes

*Zeno* is credited with devising roughly 40 paradoxes, though only a handful **survive through the writings of Aristotle and Simplicius**. His most famous arguments target the *possibility of motion* and the existence of *the many*: 

1. *The Achilles and the Tortoise*: In a race, a faster runner (Achilles) can never overtake a slower one (the tortoise) if the tortoise has a head start. Achilles must first reach the point where the tortoise began, by which time the tortoise has moved further ahead, ad infinitum.
2. *The Dichotomy Paradox*: To travel any distance, one must first travel half that distance, then half of the remaining distance, and so on. Because this sequence of tasks is infinite, Zeno argued it is impossible to ever begin or finish the journey.
3. *The Arrow Paradox*: At any specific instant, an arrow in flight occupies a space exactly equal to its size. Since it is stationary at every instant, and time is made of instants, the arrow is never actually in motion.
4. *The Moving Rows (The Stadium)*: This paradox argues that the same distance can be traversed in different amounts of time depending on the relative motion of observers, leading Zeno to conclude that time and motion lead to logical contradictions. 

Intellectual Legacy

1. *Inventor of Dialectic*: Aristotle famously called Zeno the "inventor of the dialectic," a method of argument that seeks truth through logical discussion and the uncovering of contradictions.
2. *Mathematical Rigour*: His puzzles regarding infinite divisibility and the nature of the continuum prefigured concepts in calculus and quantum mechanics, challenging mathematicians for over two millennia.
3. *Influence*: Zeno's work forced later philosophers, including Leucippus and Democritus (the Atomists), to refine their theories of matter and space to account for his logical traps. 

**Note:** He is often confused with ***Zeno of Citium***, the later **founder of Stoicism**.

## Socrates (c. 470-399 BC)

Socrates (c. 470–399 BC) was a **foundational figure** in Western philosophy, renowned for his commitment to the *examined life* and his influence on ***his student Plato***. Despite his immense legacy, he wrote no texts; our *knowledge of him comes* ***primarily from the dialogues of Plato*** and the ***writings of the historian Xenophon***. 

Core Philosophical Ideas

1. *The Socratic Method (Elenchus)*: A form of cooperative argumentative dialogue based on asking and answering questions to stimulate critical thinking and illuminate ideas.
2. *Socratic Ignorance*: He famously claimed that his only wisdom was knowing that he knew nothing, a concept often linked to the Oracle at Delphi declaring him the wisest man in Athens.
3. *The Unexamined Life*: At his trial, he asserted that *the unexamined life is not worth living*, prioritizing the care of the soul over material wealth or physical comfort.
4. *Virtue as Knowledge*: He believed that all wrongdoing stems from ignorance and that if one truly knows what is right, they will act accordingly. 

Trial and Death

In 399 BC, at the age of 70, *Socrates* was brought to trial by the Athenian democracy on two charges: impiety (not worshipping the city's gods) and corrupting the youth. 

1. *The Verdict*: He was found guilty by a jury of his peers and sentenced to death.
2. *Execution*: Refusing to escape when offered the chance, he died by drinking a cup of poisonous hemlock, maintaining his commitment to the law and his principles until the end. 

Key Figures & Works

1. ***Plato***: His most famous student whose *dialogues* (like the **Apology, Crito**, and **Phaedo**) are the **primary sources** for Socratic thought.
2. ***Xenophon***: A contemporary and follower who provided more practical, biographical accounts in works like Memorabilia.
3. ***Aristophanes***: A comic playwright who satirized *Socrates* as a *con man* in his play *The Clouds*.

## Euclid of Megara (c. 435-365 BC)

Euclid of Megara (/ˈjuːklɪd/; Ancient Greek: Εὐκλείδης Eucleides; c. 435-365 BC) was a Greek Socratic philosopher who **founded the Megarian school** of philosophy. He was a pupil of *Socrates* in the late 5th c. BC, and was present at his death. 

Key Life Events

1. *Dedication to Socrates*: When Athens banned citizens of Megara from entering the city, *Euclid* reportedly disguised himself as a woman to sneak into Athens at night and hear *Socrates* speak.
2. *Presence at Socrates' Death*: He was among the intimate circle of friends present at the execution of *Socrates* in 399 BC.
3. *Refuge for Plato*: Following *Socrates'* death, *Euclid* provided a safe haven in Megara for *Plato* and other frightened disciples. 

Philosophical Teachings

1. **The Supreme Good**: Euclid taught that *The Good* is one, eternal, and unchangeable. He argued that although it may be called by many names—such as Wisdom, God, or Reason—it remains a single entity, and its opposite does not exist.
2. **Eristic Method**: He was known for his mastery of *eristic* (contentious) dialectic. Instead of attacking an opponent's premises, he would attack their conclusion by showing it led to absurd or impossible results (a technique related to reductio ad absurdum).
3. **Influence**: His school flourished for about a century and significantly influenced the development of Stoicism through his successors like Stilpo and Zeno of Citium. 

Historical Confusion

A common historical error—prevalent ***from the Middle Ages through the early Renaissance***—***confused*** *Euclid of* ***Megara*** with *Euclid of* ***Alexandria***, the *Father of Geometry* and author of the *Elements*. It was not until the 16th century that scholars definitively distinguished the two figures.

## Plato (c. 427-347 BC)

Plato’s philosophy was not a solitary invention but a synthesis of several major Greek intellectual traditions. He systematically integrated the ethical focus of his teacher, *Socrates*, with the *metaphysical theories* of the *Pre-Socratics*. 

Core Predecessors and Their Influence

Plato's work is famously described as a *series of footnotes* to his own genius, yet he borrowed heavily from these specific figures: 

1. ***Socrates*** (The Ethical Foundation): Plato’s most direct influence and primary character in his dialogues. He inherited Socrates' relentless questioning (the Socratic method) and his focus on ethics and the soul over natural science.
2. ***Heraclitus*** (The World of Flux): From Heraclitus, Plato adopted the view that the physical, sensible world is in a state of constant change (*flux*), making it impossible to gain permanent knowledge from it alone.
3. ***Parmenides & Zeno*** (The World of Being): In contrast to flux, the Eleatic school taught that true reality is unchanging and unified. Plato used this to develop his Theory of Forms, arguing that while physical things change, the "Forms" (like Justice or Beauty) are eternal and stable.
4. ***Pythagoras*** (Mathematics & The Soul): Plato was deeply influenced by the Pythagorean belief in the immortality and transmigration of souls. He also shared their fascination with mathematical order as a key to understanding the universe.
5 . **The Sophists** (The Rivals): Figures like Protagoras and Gorgias served as Plato's intellectual *predecessors* by opposition. He rejected their relativism (the idea that *man is the measure of all things*) and developed his own objective philosophy in response. 

Origin and Historical Context

Plato (c. 427-347 BC) was born into an aristocratic Athenian family during the decline of Athens’ *Golden Age*. 

1. *Lineage*: His family claimed **descent from the god Poseidon** *and* the famous lawgiver ***Solon***.
2. *Political Catalyst*: Originally intending for a political career, he was disillusioned by the *Thirty Tyrants* (which included his own relatives) and the subsequent democratic government’s execution of Socrates.
3. *The Academy*: Following *Socrates'* death, *Plato* traveled for 12 years to Egypt, Italy, and Sicily to study with other thinkers before returning to Athens **to found the Academy** in 387 BC., the **first institution of higher learning in the West**.

## Aristotle (384-322 BC)

Aristotle (384-322 BC) was a **foundational** ancient Greek philosopher and polymath whose work established the intellectual framework for much of Western science, logic, and philosophy.

Aristotle is widely considered the **father of psychology** among Greek philosophers. 

While **modern psychology** as a scientific discipline was formally established in the 19th century by **Wilhelm Wundt**, *Aristotle* is credited with laying its philosophical and theoretical foundations nearly 2400 years ago. 


Why Aristotle is Given This Title

1. **The First Systematic Text**: His treatise ***De Anima*** (On the Soul) is often cited as the first formal book on psychology. In it, he **explored the nature of the mind** and its relationship to the body.
2. **Empirical Approach**: Unlike his teacher Plato, who emphasized abstract *Forms,* Aristotle favored observation and experience (**empiricism**) to understand human behavior and mental processes.
3. **Comprehensive Study**: He systematically analyzed topics that remain central to psychology today, including:

- Perception and Sensation: How the *five senses gather information*.
- Memory and Learning: The *concept of the tabula rasa* (blank slate), suggesting *we are shaped by our environment* and *experiences*.
- Motivation and Emotion: The drives and desires that govern human action. 

Other Greek Influences

While **Aristotle is the primary figure**, other Greek thinkers contributed significantly: 

1. ***Socrates***: Introduced **introspection**—looking inward to gain *self-knowledge and awareness*.
2. ***Plato***: Proposed a **tripartite model of the soul***, suggesting the mind is divided into **reason, spirit, and appetite**—a precursor to later theories like ***Freud's*** **id, ego, and superego**.
3. ***Hippocrates***: Known as the **father of medicine**, he was one of the first to suggest that *mental illnesses* had natural, **physical causes** rather than being divine punishments.

Core Life and Career

1. *Early Years*: Born in Stagira, his *father was* the court *physician to the King of Macedon*.
2. *The Academy*: At 17, he moved to Athens to study **at Plato's Academy**, where he remained for 20 years as a student and teacher.
3. *Tutor to Alexander*: In 343 BC, he was summoned by *Philip II of Macedon* to tutor his young son, *Alexander the Great*.
4. *The Lyceum*: He returned to Athens in 335 BC to **found his own school, the Lyceum**, where he conducted much of his research and teaching. 

Key Philosophical Contributions

1. **Formal Logic**: He invented the field of formal logic, specifically the syllogism (e.g., *All men are mortal; Socrates is a man; therefore, Socrates is mortal*).
2. **Empiricism**: Unlike *Plato*, who focused on *abstract forms*, *Aristotle* emphasized *empirical observation* and the study of *physical objects* through the senses.
3. *Ethics*: In his **Nicomachean Ethics**, he argued that the ultimate goal of human life is *Eudaimonia (flourishing or happiness)* achieved through the cultivation of virtues and the *Golden Mean* (moderation).
4. *Natural Sciences*: Often called the *Father of Zoology*, he was the first to systematically classify animals based on their physiology and behavior in works like **History of Animals**.
5. *Politics*: He famously defined man as a *political animal* and analyzed various forms of government to determine how communities could best live together. 

Legacy

Aristotle's influence was so absolute that medieval scholars like *Thomas Aquinas* referred to him simply as **The Philosopher**. His systems of logic and science **remained dominant in both the Western and Islamic worlds until the Enlightenment**.

## Euclid of Alexandria (4th -3rd. c. BC)

Euclid of Alexandria was an ancient Greek mathematician active around 300 BC, widely referred to as the **father of geometry**. He is best known for his influential 13-volume treatise, **Elements**, which served as **the primary textbook for geometry for over 2000 years**. 

*Euclid of Alexandria* (c. 300 BC) did not invent geometry from scratch; instead, he was a master systematiser who compiled, organized, and perfected the work of his predecessors into a single logical framework known as the Elements. 

Key Predecessors and Their Contributions

According to ancient historians like *Proclus*, *Euclid perfected* and *brought to irrefutable proof* ideas that were previously loosely demonstrated. 

1. ***Thales of Miletus*** (c. 624–548 BC): Recognized as one of the first to apply mathematical principles to solve physical problems, such as calculating the height of pyramids.
2. ***Pythagoras*** (c. 570–495 BC): Much of the material in Books I and II of the Elements—including the famous Pythagorean Theorem—is attributed to Pythagoras and his followers.
3. ***Hippocrates of Chios*** (c. 470–410 BC): He is believed to have **written the first known Elements** (**now lost**) and likely provided the basis for *Euclid's* Book III.
4. ***Theaetetus*** (c. 417–369 BC): A student of *Plato* who developed the theory of irrational lengths (Book X) and the study of the five regular solids (Book XIII).
5. ***Eudoxus of Cnidus*** (c. 408–355 BC): He developed the theory of proportion (Book V) and the method of exhaustion (Book XII), which allowed for calculating areas and volumes of curved figures.
6. ***Theudius of Magnesia & Leon***: Wrote earlier versions of geometry textbooks that *Euclid* likely used as references for his own structure. 

Broad Historical Context

Beyond individual mathematicians, Euclid’s work drew from centuries of practical knowledge from *Ancient Egypt* and *Babylonia*, where geometric rules were used for land surveying and construction long before they were formalized into a deductive system. 

Major Contributions

1. **The Elements**: This monumental work systematically compiled the mathematical knowledge of his time, deriving theorems from a small set of axioms and postulates using rigorous logical proofs. It covered plane geometry, number theory (including the infinitude of prime numbers and the Euclidean algorithm), the theory of irrational numbers, and solid geometry, including the construction of the Platonic solids.
2. *Axiomatic Method and Logical Rigor*: *Euclid's* **greatest legacy is his pioneering use of the axiomatic method**, starting from a few self-evident statements (axioms and common notions) to logically prove complex propositions. This approach *established the standard for mathematical rigor and deductive reasoning* that influenced not only mathematics but also philosophy and science for millennia.
3. *Euclidean Geometry*: The system of geometry presented in his main work is now known as *Euclidean geometry*. His postulates, particularly the fifth (the parallel postulate), defined the plane geometry taught in schools to this day and **provided the foundation upon which later non-Euclidean geometries were developed** in the 19th century.
4. *Other Works*: In addition to Elements, Euclid wrote other significant works, including **Optics** (the earliest surviving Greek text on perspective), **Data**, **Phaenomena** (on spherical astronomy), and the likely lost *Conics and Porisms*, all following the same logical structure of definitions and proved propositions. 

Life and Historical Context

Very little is known for certain about Euclid's personal life. It is generally accepted that he spent his career and **founded a mathematical tradition in Alexandria**, Egypt, during the reign of *Ptolemy I Soter* (323-285 BC). While details are scarce, the impact of his work on figures like *Sir Isaac Newton* and *Albert Einstein* highlights his profound and lasting influence on Western thought. 

While *Euclid of Alexandria* (c. 300 BC) did not have a single *successor* in the sense of a royal heir, his legacy at the Library of Alexandria was carried forward by a lineage of brilliant mathematicians and commentators who expanded upon his work for over seven centuries. 

Immediate and Influential Successors

1. ***Archimedes of Syracuse*** (c. 287–212 BC): Often **considered the greatest mathematician of antiquity**, *Archimedes* used *Euclidean methods* to develop early forms of calculus and precisely measure the circle and sphere.
2. ***Eratosthenes of Cyrene*** (c. 276–194 BC): A contemporary who used Euclidean principles to calculate the Earth's circumference with remarkable accuracy. 
3. ***Apollonius of Perga*** (c. 262–190 BC): Known as *The Great Geometer,* he extended Euclid’s work into the study of conic sections, **introducing** the terms **ellipse, parabola, and hyperbola**.


Later Alexandrian Successors & Commentators

As Alexandria remained an intellectual hub, several figures were specifically noted for **preserving or refining Euclid's Elements**: 

1. ***Pappus of Alexandria*** (c. 320): Frequently *described as the* ***true successor*** to *Euclid* and *Apollonius*, he revived classical geometry and introduced flexible problem-solving techniques like *analysis and synthesis*.
2. ***Theon of Alexandria*** (c. 335–405): He produced a definitive **edition of the Elements** that served as the **primary source** for most translations until the 19th century.
3. ***Hypatia of Alexandria*** (d. 415): The daughter of *Theon* and the first notable female mathematician, she was the principal teacher at the *Neoplatonic School* and is often cited as the last major mathematical figure of ancient Alexandria.
4. ***Proclus*** (c. 412–485): Wrote one of the most significant *commentaries* on Book I of the Elements, providing vital historical context on Euclid's predecessors.

## Epicurus (341-270 BC)

Epicurus (341–270 BC) was an ancient Greek philosopher who *founded* ***Epicureanism***, a school of thought that famously identified pleasure as the highest good. Unlike modern connotations of *epicurean* luxury, Epicurus taught that true pleasure is the *absence* of pain (aponia) and mental disturbance (ataraxia), achievable only through simple living and the cultivation of friendship. 


Core Philosophical Pillars

1. *Ethics* (**Hedonism through Moderation**): Epicurus divided desires into three categories: 
natural and necessary (food, safety, friends)  
natural but unnecessary (extravagant food)  
vain/empty (wealth, fame). He advised focusing only on the first to achieve lasting tranquility.
2. *Physics* (**Atomism**): He proposed a *materialistic* universe composed of *invisible, indivisible* atoms moving through a *void*. Notably, he *introduced* the concept of the atomic *swerve*—*random shifts* in atomic motion—to explain how humans possess *free will* in a physical world.
3. *Theology* (Uninvolved Gods): While he believed gods existed as immortal, blessed beings, he argued they have *no interest* in human affairs and do not reward or punish us. This removed the need for religious fear, a major source of anxiety.
4. *Epistemology* (**Empiricism**): He held that all knowledge originates in *sensory perception*, which is always true; errors only arise in how we judge or interpret those sensations. 

The Tetrapharmakos (The Fourfold Cure)

Epicureans lived by a basic guideline to cure the *diseases of the soul* and attain a happy life: 

1. Don't fear god (They are too happy to care about us).
2. Don't worry about death (*When we exist, death is not; and when death exists, we are not*).
3. What is good is easy to get (Basic needs are simple to satisfy).
4. What is terrible is easy to endure (Pain is either brief or manageable). 

Life and Legacy

Epicurus established his **school**, ***The Garden*** (***Ho Kepos***), in Athens around 306 BC. It was revolutionary for its time, welcoming women and slaves as equal members of the community. Although a prolific writer of over 300 works, only a few letters and collections of aphorisms (like the Principal Doctrines) survived Christian suppression during the Middle Ages. 
His ideas were *rediscovered* in the 15th century and profoundly influenced Enlightenment thinkers like *Thomas Jefferson*, who famously identified as an *Epicurean*. 

Epicurus (341–270 BC) established a lineage of leaders, known as scholarchs, to head his philosophical school, *The Garden*, in Athens. He formally named his first successor in his will, ensuring the school's continuity for several centuries. 

The Four Founders

Before the formal line of succession, Epicurus was supported by three close companions, collectively known with him as the "Four Men" or "Founders" of the school: 

1. ***Epicurus***: The Hegemon (Leader) and founder.
2. ***Metrodorus of Lampsacus***: Referred to as "almost a second Epicurus," he was the intended successor but died seven years before Epicurus.
3. ***Polyaenus of Lampsacus***: A distinguished mathematician who also predeceased Epicurus.
4. ***Hermarchus of Mytilene***: The first official successor to Epicurus. 

Chronological List of Scholarchs 

The leadership of the *Athenian Garden* followed this general succession: 

1. ***Hermarchus of Mytilene*** **(c. 270–250 BC)**: The first to lead after *Epicurus*; he focused on the origin of law and rhetoric.
2. ***Polystratus*** **(c. 250–219 BC)**: The first scholarch who was not a founding member.
3. ***Dionysius of Lamptrai*** **(c. 219–201 BC)**: Succeeded *Polystratus* after a contested election.
4. ***Basilides of Tyre*** **(c. 201–175 BC)**: A pupil of *Artemon* who taught in Syria before leading in Athens.
5. ***Apollodorus*** (!!) *the Tyrant* **(c. 150–110 BC)**: Nicknamed for his strict discipline, he wrote over 400 books, though they are now lost to history. *Apollodorus the Epicurean* (fl. 2nd century BC) was an influential Greek philosopher and the head (scholarch) of the Epicurean school in Athens. He is most famous for his nickname, the **Tyrant of the Garden** (*Kepotyrannos*), a title earned for his strict and dominant leadership of the school founded by *Epicurus*. [Hesiod]
6. ***Zeno of Sidon*** **(c. 100–75 BC)**: A prolific writer who revitalised the school's intellectual life and influenced *Cicero*.
7. ***Phaedrus*** **(c. 75–70 BC)**: A wealthy Athenian orator who succeeded *Zeno*.
8. ***Patro*** **(c. 70–51 BC)**: The last major head of the Athenian school; under his leadership, the school's central supremacy began to fade. 

Notable Later Epicureans

While the formal scholarchate declined, Epicureanism continued through influential figures elsewhere: 

1. ***Philodemus of Gadara*** **(c. 110-35 BC)**: A student of *Zeno of Sidon* whose library was preserved at Herculaneum.
2. ***Lucretius*** **(c. 99-55 BC)**: The Roman poet whose work De Rerum Natura is the most complete surviving exposition of the doctrine. 
3. **Roman Period and Decline** **(1st c. BC - 4th c. AD)**: Epicureanism gained a following in Rome, famously inspiring the poet *Lucretius*. However, the organized school in Athens eventually faced decline with the rise of Christianity.
4. ***Diogenes of Oenoanda*** **(2nd c. AD)**: Famous for carving the school’s teachings onto a massive stone wall in modern-day Turkey. 

The *Epicurean paradox* is a version of the problem of evil. Lactantius attributes this trilemma to *Epicurus in De Ira Dei, 13, 20-21*.

The **Garden of Epicurus** (Ho Kêpos) was a philosophical school and commune founded by *Epicurus* in Athens around 306 BC. *Unlike the public spaces* used by other schools—such as **Plato’s Academy** or **Aristotle’s Lyceum**—the Garden was a *private* residence and retreat that became the heart of *Epicurean* life and thought.

## Aristarch (c. 310-230 BC)

Successors of Aristarchus of Samos (Astronomer) 

Aristarchus of Samos (c. 310-230 BC) is famous for proposing the *first known heliocentric model*, though it was largely rejected in antiquity in favour of geocentrism. 

1. **Seleucus of Seleucia**: The only known astronomer from antiquity to explicitly support and *attempt* to prove *Aristarchus'* heliocentric theory.
2. **Hipparchus of Nicaea**: Often considered the greatest astronomer of antiquity, he used *Aristarchus'* quantitative methods to improve models of the Sun and Moon, though he returned to a geocentric framework.
3. **Nicolaus Copernicus**: While living 1800 years later, *Copernicus* is often called the *modern Aristarchus* because he revived the heliocentric theory, citing *Aristarchus* as an inspiration.

## Eratosthenes (c. 276-194 BC)

The *primary predecessor of Eratosthenes* as the head of the Library of Alexandria was *Apollonius Rhodius*. 
In his scientific and geographical work, Eratosthenes built upon the theories and observations of several notable figures:

1. *Geography and Mapping*: He drew heavily from Hecataeus of Miletus, Anaximander, and Pytheas, who provided early data on landmasses and tides.
2. *Astronomy and Mathematics*: His predecessors included Aristarchus of Samos, who developed the heliocentric hypothesis, and Eudoxus of Cnidus, who influenced his work on Earth's climate zones and spherical geometry.
3. *Mentorship*: In Athens, he was a pupil of the philosopher *Callimachus* and the Stoic *Ariston* of *Chios*. 

*Eratosthenes'* most immediate and notable successor was *Aristophanes of Byzantium*, who followed him as the Chief Librarian of the Great Library of Alexandria. 

While Aristophanes took over his professional role, several other ancient scholars are recognized as his students or intellectual successors who built upon his work in geography, mathematics, and history: 

1. ***Aristophanes of Byzantium***: A renowned grammarian and critic who succeeded *Eratosthenes* as the head of the Library of Alexandria.
2. ***Mnaseas of Patara***: A geographer and student of *Eratosthenes* known for his periegesis (descriptions of places).
3. ***Hipparchus of Nicaea***: Often considered *Eratosthenes'* successor in scientific geography and astronomy, he famously critiqued and refined *Eratosthenes'* calculations for the Earth's circumference and his geographical maps.
4. ***Strabo***: A later geographer whose work Geography is the *primary source* for much of *Eratosthenes'* lost writings. He heavily referenced, and at times refuted, *Eratosthenes'* theories.
5. ***Menander of Ephesus***: An ancient historian and student of *Eratosthenes*.
6. ***Aristis***: Recorded as a student of *Eratosthenes*, though little is known about his specific works.

## Hipparchus of Nicaea (Astronomer) (c. 190-120 BC)

Hipparchus of Nicaea (c. 190-120 BC) built his landmark *astronomical* and *mathematical* work by synthesizing centuries of data and techniques from both *Greek and Babylonian predecessors*. 

He relied on the observations of earlier astronomers to discover the precession of the equinoxes and utilized theoretical geometric models that had not yet been applied in practice. 

Key Greek Predecessors, Babylonian Influences

*Hipparchus* was the *first Greek* astronomer to systematically exploit the mathematical techniques and observations accumulated over centuries by the *Chaldeans* (*Babylonians*). 
*Hipparchus* frequently compared his own observations with those of his predecessors to identify long-term celestial changes. 

1. ***Meton of Athens*** **(5th c. BC)**: *Hipparchus* used *Meton's* solstice observations (specifically from 432 BC) to help calculate the length of the tropical year. 
2. ***Kidinnu*** **(c. 4th c. BC)**: Some scholars attribute the Babylonian *System B* ephemerides to *Kidinnu*. Hipparchus adopted several parameters from this system, including the mean synodic month (calculated at approximately 29.530594 days).
3. ***Eudoxus of Cnidus*** **(4th c. BC)**: *Eudoxus* described stars and constellations in his works *Phaenomena and Entropon*. Hipparchus's only surviving work is a commentary criticizing the imprecise stellar placements found in the works of *Eudoxus* and the poet *Aratus*.
4. ***Aristarchus of Samos*** **(c. 310-230 BC)**: Famous for proposing the first heliocentric (Sun-centered) model, *Aristarchus* also wrote On the Sizes and Distances of the Sun and Moon. *Hipparchus* used Aristarchus's observations, such as a solstice timing from 280 BC, and wrote his own treatise on the same topic.
5. ***Timocharis*** and ***Aristyllus*** **(3rd c. BC)**: *Hipparchus* used their earlier measurements of star positions, specifically the longitudes of bright stars like *Spica* and *Regulus*, to discover the precession of the equinoxes. By comparing their data to his own, he realized these stars had shifted roughly 2? relative to the equinoxes.
6. ***Eratosthenes of Cyrene*** **(c. 276-194 BC)**: Best known for calculating the *Earth's circumference*, *Eratosthenes* was a major influence on *Hipparchus's* geographical work. *Hipparchus* wrote a three-book critique titled Against the *Geography of Eratosthenes*, where he corrected Eratosthenes's coordinate data and distances.
7. ***Apollonius of Perga*** **(c. 262-190 BC)**: *Apollonius* developed the mathematical models of eccentrics and epicycles to explain the non-uniform motion of the Sun and Moon. While Apollonius created these as theoretical models, Hipparchus was the first to apply them to actual observations and determine their specific parameters.


Data and Units:

1. *Eclipse Records*: Hipparchus likely compiled a list of Babylonian eclipse observations stretching back centuries (e.g., records from 721 BC). He used these to verify the accuracy of his lunar theories.
2. *Measurement Units*: He adopted the Babylonian cubit (pechus) for measuring angular distances (approx. 2? or 2.5?) and used their division of the circle into 360 degrees and 60 arcminutes.
3. *Arithmetic Techniques*: He utilized Babylonian arithmetical methods alongside Greek geometry, bridging two separate traditions to create a predictive *numerical-geometrical* astronomy. 

In the scientific lineage, *Hipparchus* had no *immediate successor* who matched his caliber for centuries. However, his most significant intellectual successor was *Claudius Ptolemy*, who lived roughly 300 years later. Ptolemy's work, the *Almagest*, preserved and built upon *Hipparchus's* star catalogues and theories on planetary motion.

## Archē

In ancient Greek philosophy, the *archē* (ἀρχή) is the *first principle*, *origin*, or *primary substance* from which the *entire universe* is derived. *The search for this single, unifying essence marked the transition from mythological explanations to rational, scientific inquiry*. 

**The Mileisan Quest for Archē**

The earliest Western philosophers, known as the *Milesian School*, each proposed a different material substance as the ultimate foundation of reality: 

1. ***Thales***: **Water**
As the first philosopher, Thales argued that water is the *archē*. He likely chose it because it is essential for life, found in all things, and can transform into different states—solid, liquid, and gas.
2. ***Anaximander***: **Apeiron**
A student of *Thales, Anaximander* rejected specific elements like water because they have opposites (e.g., fire) that would cancel them out. Instead, he proposed the *Apeiron*—an indefinite, boundless, and eternal substance from which all things originate and eventually return.
3.  ***Anaximenes***: **Air**
*Anaximenes* returned to a tangible element, positing air as the *archē*. He introduced a *mechanism of change*: through **rarefaction (thinning), air becomes fire**; through **condensation (thickening), it becomes wind**, **clouds**, **water**, **earth**, and finally **stone**. 

**Later Philosophical Interpretations**

As philosophy evolved beyond Miletus, the concept of archē expanded: 

1. ***Heraclitus***: **Fire**
For Heraclitus, the primary principle was fire, symbolizing a universe in a state of constant flux and transformation, governed by the Logos (rational order).
2. ***Pythagoras***: **Numbers**
The Pythagoreans shifted from physical matter to *abstract principles*, suggesting that *numbers and mathematical ratios* were the true *archē of the cosmos*.
3. ***Empedocles***: **The Four Roots**
Empedocles proposed a *pluralistic archē*, arguing the universe is made of four eternal *roots*—*Earth, Air, Fire, and Water*—which are *mixed* and separated by the forces of Love and Strife.

## Arche vs. Yle

In ancient Greek philosophy, *Arche (or archē)* refers to the fundamental substance or *first principle* of the universe, while *Hyle (or hyle)* refers to the *matter* or *material* that makes up physical objects. 
The distinction between these two concepts is central to how early thinkers, **particularly Aristotle**, explained the structure of reality. 

Key Conceptual Differences

**Table 3**. Arche vs. Yle.

Concept 	Translation						Role in Philosophy
~~~
Arche		Beginning, Origin, Principle	The foundational source or cause from which everything originates and to which it returns.
Hyle		Matter, Wood/Timber				The underlying material that receives form to become a specific object.
~~~

### Arche: The Originating Principle

The concept of arche was the primary focus of the Pre-Socratic philosophers (the Milesians). They sought a single, eternal substance that explained the diversity of the world. 

1. ***Thales***: Identified the *arche* as **Water**, believing it was the source of all life and change.
2. ***Anaximander***: Proposed the *Apeiron* (the Boundless), an indeterminate and infinite *substance* that *precedes* all specific elements.
3. ***Heraclitus***: Viewed **Fire** as the *arche*, symbolising constant change and the Logos (universal reason). 

### Hyle: The Material Basis

Hyle (literally *timber* or *raw material*) became a technical term later, most notably in ***Aristotelian*** philosophy. 

1. **Material Cause**: *Aristotle* used *hyle* to describe one of his Four Causes. It is the *stuff* out of which something is made (e.g., bronze is the *hyle* of a statue).
2. **Potentiality**: In his *metaphysics*, *hyle* represents pure potentiality. Matter has no definite shape or identity until it is acted upon by Morphe (Form).
3. **Relation to Arche**: While the Pre-Socratics' arche was often a *specific type* of matter (like water or air), *Aristotle's* hyle is a more *abstract concept* used to explain the **metaphysical structure** of individual objects. 

Summary of the Relationship

In the earliest philosophy, the two concepts were often blurred: the *arche* was simply the ***first*** *matter*. 

As philosophy matured, Arche became the logical or divine *starting point* of the cosmos, whereas *Hyle became* the *physical substrate* that undergoes change and takes on different *forms*.

## Archimedes (c. 287-212 BC)

*Archimedes of Syracuse* (c. 287-212 BC) was an Ancient Greek polymath—a mathematician, physicist, engineer, and inventor—widely regarded as **one of the greatest scientists in history**. Living in the Sicilian city of Syracuse, his work **laid the foundations for hydrostatics, statics**, and the explanation of the **principle of the lever**. 

Key Scientific Contributions

1. **Archimedes' Principle**: He discovered the law of buoyancy, stating that an object submerged in a fluid is buoyed up by a force equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces. Legend says he realized this in a bathtub and ran naked through the streets shouting *Eureka!* (*I have found it!*).
2. **Mathematical Breakthroughs**: He was the first to rigorously approximate the value of **pi** and proved that the volume of a sphere is exactly two-thirds the volume of a cylinder that encloses it. He also pioneered methods that *anticipated modern calculus*.
3. **The Lever**: While he did not invent the lever, he famously proved its mathematical principles, leading to his quote: *Give me a place to stand, and I will move the world*. 

Famous Inventions

1. **Archimedes' Screw**: A device for raising water that is still used today for irrigation and wastewater management.
2. **Defensive War Machines**: During the Roman siege of Syracuse, he designed the Claw of Archimedes to capsize ships and is legendarily attributed with using mirrors (the *Death Ray*) to focus sunlight and ignite enemy vessels. 

Death and Legacy

Archimedes was killed by a Roman soldier during the fall of Syracuse. According to historians, he was so engrossed in a mathematical diagram drawn in the sand that his final words were a plea for the soldier not to disturb his circles (**Noli turbare circulos meos**). His tomb was marked with his favorite discovery: a sphere inscribed within a cylinder.
