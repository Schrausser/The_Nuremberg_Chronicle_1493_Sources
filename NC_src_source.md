# Diels-Kranz

---

The term ***Diels-Kranz*** (often abbreviated as ***DK***) refers to the definitive collection of source materials for Presocratic philosophy, titled **Die Fragmente der Vorsokratiker** (The Fragments of the Presocratics). First published by ***Hermann Diels*** in 1903 and later expanded by ***Walther Kranz***, it is the **standard reference** for scholars studying early Greek thinkers (c.f. Diels, [1906](https://archive.org/details/diefragmentederv01diel)). 

Core Structure of Sources

The collection organizes sources into three distinct categories for each philosopher, known as the **Diels-Kranz numbering system**: 

1. **A-fragments (Testimonia)**: These are secondary reports or *testimonies* from later ancient authors (like *Aristotle* or *Simplicius*) about the life, doctrines, and influence of a philosopher.
2. **B-fragments (Ipsissima Verba)**: These are considered *actual fragments*—the verbatim words or *direct quotations* of the original thinker as preserved in later works.
3. **C-fragments (Imitations)**: These are later works that imitate or are modeled after the philosopher's style or ideas, used to show their lasting impact. 

Key Editions and Access

**Standard Edition: The 6th edition** (1951–1952) remains the most widely cited scholarly version.

Volume Structure:

1. Volume I & II: Contain the fragments and testimonies of philosophers ranging from Thales to the Sophists.
2. Volume III: Features a comprehensive index (Wortindex) of the Greek terms used in the fragments, compiled by Walther Kranz.
3. Digital Archives: Early editions of the work are available through the Internet Archive for public research. 

Examples of Citations

A typical citation looks like **DK 28 B 1**, where:

- 28 identifies the philosopher (in this case, Parmenides).
- B indicates it is a direct fragment.
- 1 refers to the specific fragment number. 

1. Die Fragmente der Vorsokratiker (3-Volume Set): This is the complete standard edition. It features Greek texts with German translations.
2. Die Fragmente der Vorsokratiker Band 1 (6th Edition): Edited by Walther Kranz and published by Weidmannsche Buchhandlung; this edition is widely cited in academic literature.
3. Die Fragmente der Vorsokratiker Band 2: Often includes supplements and "Nachträge" (addenda) provided by Walther Kranz to update Diels' original findings.
4. Die Fragmente der Vorsokratiker Band 3 (Wortindex): A comprehensive word index compiled by Walther Kranz to assist in philological research. 

Related Philosophical Works

Beyond the main collection, Walther Kranz published other influential works on Greek philosophy that are sometimes grouped with the "Diels-Kranz" scholarly legacy. 

1. Antik Felsefe: Metinler ve Açıklamalar (Walther Kranz): A collection of ancient philosophical texts and explanations, often used in translated academic settings.
2. Die Griechische Philosophie (Walther Kranz): A standalone study of Greek philosophy published in several editions.
3. The Older Sophists (Rosamond Kent Sprague and Diels-Kranz): An English translation specifically focusing on the sophists included in the Diels-Kranz collection. 

Originally published by Hermann Diels in 1903, it was later significantly expanded and revised by Walther Kranz. 

Historical Editions

The work evolved through several major revisions, with the 6th edition becoming the definitive standard for scholarly reference. 


1. 1st Edition (1903): A single-volume collection by Hermann Diels that established the initial framework.
2. 2nd–4th Editions (1906–1922): Expanded and revised by Diels during his lifetime.
3. 5th Edition (1934–1937): The first major revision by Walther Kranz, which introduced the current three-volume format and finalized the numbering system.
4. 6th Edition (1951–1952): Revised by Kranz and considered the "standard" version used by modern scholars.
5. Subsequent Reprints: Editions after the 6th (such as the 10th or 12th) are primarily photographic reprints with minor corrections or Nachträge (supplements).

# Papyri

## The Derveni papyrus

The Derveni papyrus is an Ancient Greek papyrus roll that was discovered in 1962 at the archaeological site of Derveni, near Thessaloniki, in Central Macedonia. A philosophical treatise, the text is **an allegorical commentary on an Orphic poem, a theogony** concerning the birth of the gods, *produced in the circle of* the philosopher ***Anaxagoras***. The roll dates to around 340 BC, during the reign of Philip II of Macedon, making it **Europe's oldest surviving manuscript**.

>"in the fields of Greek religion, the sophistic movement, early philosophy, and the origins of literary criticism it is unquestionably the most important textual discovery of the 20th century.", (Janko, [2002](http://www.jstor.org/stable/20191519), p. 1)

## The Villa of the Papyri scrolls

The Villa of the Papyri scrolls were carbonised during the eruption of Mount Vesuvius in **AD 79**. While the scrolls were buried for nearly 1700 years, the majority of the texts themselves **date back** to the **1st c. BC**. 

Key Historical Dates

1. **Initial Construction (c. 40 BC)**: Recent archaeological findings suggest the villa was built around this time.
2. **Writing of the Scrolls (1st Century BC)**: Most scrolls contain the works of the *Epicurean philosopher* ***Philodemus of Gadara*** (c. 110–30 BC), meaning they were likely written and collected during the mid-to-late 1st c. BC.
3. **Seaside Pavilion Addition (c. AD 20)**: A later extension was added to the villa roughly 50 years before the eruption.
4. **Volcanic Eruption (AD 79)**: The villa and its library were buried under 30 metres of volcanic material, which carbonised the papyri and preserved them from total decay.
5. **Rediscovery (1750–1754)**: The villa and its collection of over **1800 scrolls** were accidentally found by workers digging a well. 

Recent Breakthroughs

1. 2023–2024: The Vesuvius Challenge successfully used AI and X-ray phase-contrast tomography to *read the first full words* and passages from the *unopened scrolls*.
2. April 2024: Researchers used these techniques to identify a scroll detailing the exact burial location of *Plato* at his Academy in Athens.

## The Oxyrhynchus Papyri

The Oxyrhynchus Papyri comprise the **world's largest collection of ancient manuscripts**, discovered in an ancient rubbish dump at Oxyrhynchus (modern Al-Bahnasa), Egypt. Excavated primarily between 1896 and 1907 by Oxford archaeologists *Bernard Grenfell* and *Arthur Hunt*, the collection contains over 500000 fragments dating from the 3rd c. BC to the 7th c. AD. 


Overview of the Collection

1. **Massive Scale**: The site yielded an unequalled archive of the ancient world; scholars estimate that only 1–2% of the recovered fragments have been transcribed and published so far.
2. **Diverse Content**: Approximately 10% of the collection consists of literary, philosophical, and religious works, while the remaining 90% is *everyday* documentation such as tax assessments, private letters, and wills.
3. **Languages**: While predominantly in Greek, the texts also include Latin, Coptic, Arabic, Hebrew, and various forms of ancient Egyptian. 

Key Archaeological & Literary Highlights

1. **Lost Literature**: The finds restored major works previously thought lost, including poems by *Sappho*, comedies by *Menander*, and fragments of *Sophocles'* satyr plays.
2. **Biblical Significance**: The collection includes some of the earliest known fragments of the *New Testament* and the *Septuagint*. Notable finds include the *Sayings of Jesus* (Logia), now identified as part of the *Gospel of Thomas*.
3. **Scientific Texts**: It contains one of the oldest extant diagrams from *Euclid's Elements*.
4. **Everyday Life**: Documents like school writing exercises about mythology or a man's lament for a lost fighting cock provide rare insights into the lives of ordinary citizens. 

Current Status & Access

The ***Egypt Exploration Society*** acts as the custodian of the collection, which is primarily housed in the Sackler Library at the University of Oxford (c.f. Benaissa et al., [2025](https://oxyrhynchus.web.ox.ac.uk/publications)).

1. **Publication**: Systematic publication began in 1898; as of 2025, 88 volumes have been released in the ***Graeco-Roman Memoirs series***.
2. **Digital Access**: Many images and transcripts are available via the Sustainable Digital Scholarship (SDS) platform and Oxyrhynchus Online. 


The **primary publication** for these ancient manuscripts is the ongoing series titled **The Oxyrhynchus Papyri, published by the Egypt Exploration Society (EES)** within its Graeco-Roman Memoirs series. Since the first volume appeared in 1898, academics have transcribed and published over 6000 texts, yet this represents only about 1–2% of the estimated half-million fragments found at the site. 


Key Publication Series

The Oxyrhynchus Papyri (Volumes I–LXXXVIII): This is the definitive academic series. It includes high-quality transcriptions, English translations, and scholarly commentaries for each text.

1. **Volume I (1898)**: Edited by Bernard P. Grenfell and Arthur S. Hunt, this inaugural volume introduced major finds like the *Sayings of Jesus* (Logia) and poems by Sappho.
2. **Latest Volumes**: Volume LXXXVII was released in August 2023, and Volume LXXXVIII was published on 1 May 2025.
3. **Graeco-Roman Memoirs**: The overarching series from the EES that houses *The Oxyrhynchus Papyri* volumes. It **also** covers **other** Graeco-Roman **materials and papyri** held by the Society. 


Notable Individual Discoveries & Early Pamphlets
Before full volumes were released, some sensational finds were published in standalone formats:

1. Sayings of Our Lord (1897): A small booklet published by Grenfell and Hunt to quickly share the **Sayings of Jesus** (P. Oxy. 1) found on their second day of excavation.
2. Hellenica Oxyrhynchia: A major historical work discovered at the site, providing new details on classical antiquity, published in Volume V (1908).
3. The *New* Sappho and Menander: Extensive fragments of Sappho's poetry and Menander's comedies (such as Misoumenos) have been featured across multiple volumes (e.g., Volumes XV, XVII, and XLVIII). 


Digital and Archival Access

1. HathiTrust & Internet Archive: Provide free digital access to public domain volumes (typically Volumes 1–17).
2. Papyri.info: An online database offering searchable Greek texts for many of the published documentary papyri.
3. Imaging Papyri Project: A digital repository hosted by the University of Oxford providing high-resolution images of the papyri.

## Western antiquity

In the context of Western Antiquity (the Greco-Roman world), original sources can be defined either as the earliest recorded texts (whose original physical copies are lost) or the oldest extant physical manuscripts that survive today. 

Earliest Recorded Texts (8th Century BC)

The **beginning of Western antiquity** is traditionally dated to the **emergence of Greek alphabetic writing and epic poetry**. 


1. **Homer's Epics (Iliad and Odyssey)**: Composed in the 8th-7th c. BC, these are the **foundational works of Western literature**. While **no original 8th c. BC manuscripts exist**, they are the oldest major literary sources of the Western tradition.
2. **Greek Alphabetic Inscriptions**: The earliest physical evidence of Greek writing appears in the first half of the **8th c. BC** as short inscriptions on **pottery**. 

Oldest Extant Physical Manuscripts

Most ancient Western literature survived through centuries of recopying; however, a **few rare physical originals** from antiquity **still exist**: 

1. **The Dispilio Tablet** (c. 5260 BC): Discovered in Greece, this wooden tablet bears symbols that may represent a form of **proto-writing**. While it predates *Western Antiquity* (*Greco-Roman era*) and is undeciphered, it is the **oldest physical textual artifact** found **in the European region**.
2. **The Pyrgi Gold Tablets** (c. 500 BC): Three gold plates found in Italy containing bilingual **inscriptions in Etruscan and Phoenician**. These are among the oldest physical Western *books* or multi-page documents.
3. **The Dead Sea Scrolls** (1st-4th c. AD): Though Near Eastern in origin, these are critical to Western antiquity and include the **oldest surviving fragments** of the Hebrew **Bible** and some Greek texts.
4. **Vindolanda Tablets** (1st-2nd c. AD): Hand-written wooden leaf tablets from a Roman fort in Britain, providing a rare first-hand look at daily life in the Roman Empire.
5. **Villa of the Papyri Scrolls** (1st c. AD): A library of approximately 1800 carbonized papyrus scrolls buried by the eruption of Mt. Vesuvius in 79 AD. It is **the only library of Western antiquity** to have its physical collection **survive**.

The **Vergilius Vaticanus** (c. 400 AD) is widely considered **one of the oldest surviving manuscripts** from Western antiquity, representing the critical transition from the classical world to the medieval period. It is **one of** only **three ancient manuscripts** of **Virgil** to survive into the modern era, alongside the **Vergilius Augusteus** (4th c. AD fragment) and the **Vergilius Romanus** (c. 500 AD). 

Key Manuscripts of Western Antiquity

These documents are primary witnesses to the evolution of the Western book (codex) and manuscript tradition:

1. **Vergilius Vaticanus** (c. 400 AD): Housed in the Vatican Library, this is the oldest known illustrated manuscript of Virgil’s Aeneid and Georgics.
2. **Codex Sinaiticus** (mid-4th c. AD): One of the most important early Christian Greek Bibles, containing the **oldest complete copy of the New Testament**.
3. **Vergilius Romanus** (c. 500 AD): A lavishly illustrated manuscript of *Virgil's* works, notable for its naturalistic human and animal forms that characterize the art of Late Antiquity.
4. **Vienna Dioscorides** (c. 512 AD): Created in Constantinople, this is the **oldest surviving Byzantine manuscript** and a landmark in the history of medical science.
5. **Cathach of St. Columba** (c. 560 AD): Often cited as **Ireland's oldest surviving manuscript**, this Psalter predates more famous works like the Book of Kells by over two centuries.
6. **St. Cuthbert Gospel** (c. 698 AD): Regarded by the British Library as Europe's **oldest intact book**, it was buried with St. Cuthbert and remains in its original red leather binding. 

Transition to the Medieval Era

While the classical era used papyrus scrolls, the early medieval period saw the rise of parchment and the codex format. 

1. *Material*: Fragile papyrus was replaced by durable parchment (animal skin) around the 4th c.
2. *Format*: The scroll was phased out in favor of the codex—the ancestor of the modern bound book—which allowed for easier navigation and more elaborate illumination.
3. *Illumination*: The practice of decorating manuscripts with gold, silver, and vibrant pigments reached its peak in the Insular art of the 7th-8th c., seen in works like the Lindisfarne Gospels and the Book of Durrow.

## Hesiod

The oldest original source of Hesiod’s work does not exist as a single physical manuscript from his own time (c. 750-650 BC). Because his poems like the Theogony and Works and Days **originated in an oral tradition**, the earliest physical records we have are **fragments** and much **later medieval copies**. 

Major Extant Works

Only three works attributed to Hesiod have survived in their entirety or near-entirety: 

1. **Theogony**: A 1022-line poem describing the origins and genealogy of the Greek gods, from the first primordial deities like Chaos to the rise of Zeus and the Olympians.
2. **Works and Days**: A didactic poem of over 800 lines that offers practical advice on farming and seafaring, while addressing themes of justice and morality through myths like Pandora and the Five Ages of Man.
3. **Shield of Heracles**: A shorter narrative poem depicting the hero Heracles preparing for battle; however, most modern scholars believe it was composed by a later poet in the "Hesiodic style" rather than by Hesiod himself. 

The Hesiodic Corpus (Lost and Fragmentary Works)

Many other poems were attributed to Hesiod in antiquity, but today they **only exist as fragments** quoted **in other works** or found in **papyri**:

1. **Catalogue of Women (or Ehoiai)**: A genealogical poem tracing the mortal descendants of unions between gods and women.
2. **Astronomy**: A didactic work on the stars and constellations.
3. **Melampodia**: A poem about the families and myths of famous seers.
4. **Precepts of Chiron**: Educational advice supposedly given by the centaur Chiron to Achilles. 

Early Testimonia (Ancient Mentions, Historical Citations)

While physical copies are late, Hesiod's work was widely cited by other ancient authors much closer to his lifetime.
Our knowledge of Hesiod's life and the *Hesiodic tradition* comes from later ancient authors who quoted or referenced him: 

1. ***Xenophanes*** (**6th c. BC**): One of the earliest critics to mention *Homer and Hesiod* together. The written form of the *Theogony was likely established* during this period.
2. ***Herodotus***, ***Plato*** (**5th c. BC**): Stated that *Homer and Hesiod* were the first to give the Greeks their gods. Authors like *Herodotus* and *Plato* referred to *Hesiod* as a foundational *authority on the gods*. Hegodot mentions *Hesiod and Homer* as the **creators** of the Greek **divine hierarchy**.
3. ***Thucydides*** (**5th c. BC**): *Recorded a tradition regarding Hesiod's* death at the shrine of Nemean Zeus.


Earliest Physical Fragments

The origin of **Alexandrian papyri** of Hesiod is rooted in the **systematic collection** and scholarly editing performed at the **Library of Alexandria** during the **Ptolemaic period** (c. 3rd c. BC onwards). While the original poems date back to approximately 700 BC in Boeotia, their **survival** as standardized texts is **due to Alexandrian philology**. 

Key Origins and Transmission

1. **Standardization**: **Alexandrian scholars**, such as ***Aristophanes of Byzantium***, were responsible for the critical editing and standardization of *Hesiodic texts* like **Theogony and Works and Days**.
2. **Physical Preservation**: Although the Library of Alexandria was the intellectual center, **most actual surviving papyrus** fragments were **found** in the dry sands of the **Egyptian** desert, which preserved the material better than the damp coastal environment of Alexandria itself.
3. **Scholarly Commentary**: These papyri often include scholia (marginal notes) that preserve the exegetical traditions and philological techniques developed by Alexandrian librarians to interpret *the Other Poet* (Hesiod), who was considered the second pillar of the Hellenic canon after Homer.
4. **Timeline**: Some of the earliest known Hesiodic papyri date from the 1st century BC, reflecting a long-standing tradition of circulation and interest in his work throughout Greco-Roman Egypt. 

Alexandrian Papyri

1. **MS 5068 (3rd c. BC)**: Currently in the Schøyen Collection, these are the **earliest known fragments of Works and Days**, predating medieval manuscripts by over a millennium.
2. **Egyptian Papyrus Fragments (100s–200s AD)**: About sixty fragments from this era exist, helping modern editors reconstruct the original *classical* version of the text.
3. **The Catalogue of Women (Ehoiai)**: This work, which is lost in its complete form, survives primarily through numerous papyrus fragments found in Egypt, which have allowed modern scholars to reconstruct large portions of the text.
4. **The Certamen (The Contest of Homer and Hesiod)**: A popular narrative about a poetic duel between the two. While the surviving version is from the **2nd c. AD**, fragments of the story have been found dating back to the 3rd c. BC. A legendary account of a poetic **Contest of Homer and Hesiod**.


Key Byzantine Preservers

**Byzantine scholars** were instrumental in transmitting Hesiod's text and providing the *scholia* (marginal commentaries) that helped later generations understand it. 

1. **The Suda (10th c.)**: This massive Byzantine encyclopedia contains an entry on Hesiod that provides **one of our oldest medieval accounts** of his life and works.
2. ***Eustathius of Thessalonica (12th c.)***: A scholar who preserved remarks on the legendary contest between Homer and Hesiod (the *Certamen*). 
3. ***John Tzetzes (12th c.)***: A Byzantine grammarian whose Vita of Hesiod and extensive scholia on Works and Days are preserved in later 13th-century manuscripts.

Oldest Complete medieval Manuscripts

The survival of Hesiod’s works—primarily the **Works and Days** and the **Theogony**—relies on a long tradition of Byzantine and medieval copying.  While ancient papyrus fragments date back as far as the 3rd century BC, the earliest nearly complete medieval manuscripts **emerged** between the **10th-11th c.**

The *full texts* we read *today come* from the medieval manuscript tradition: 

1. **Parisinus graecus 2771**(**10th c.**): This is widely considered the **oldest complete copy of Works and Days**. It is held at the Bibliothèque nationale de France in Paris.
2. **Parisinus suppl. graecus 663** (**11th-12th c.**): Designated as Manuscript B (or Rzach’s C), this is a critical early witness for the Theogony. Written on Mount Athos, it contains **fragments of the Theogony** (lines 72–145 and 450–504) and the **Shield of Heracles**.
3. **Trinity College, MS O.9.27**: A notable manuscript from the late 13th-14th c. containing **Works and Days**. It is famous for its schematic illustrations of Byzantine agricultural tools and a wagon, providing a rare visual link to medieval farming.
4. **Vaticanus graecus 1825**: Dated to approximately **1310** based on watermarks, this is one of the earliest existing manuscripts of the ***Theogony***.
5. **Marcianus graecus 464** (**14th c.**): A **significant** manuscript because it is the autograph copy of the renowned Byzantine scholar ***Demetrius Triclinius***, containing the **complete works of Hesiod** with his personal commentaries. 
6. **Palimpsest Folios**: Some early medieval fragments of *Hesiod* exist as palimpsests, where **original Hesiodic text was partially erased** to reuse the parchment for later religious texts, such as Gospel lectionaries.

## Homer

There is **no single original manuscript** of the **Iliad** or the **Odyssey** written by *Homer* himself, as both epics originated in an oral tradition long before being committed to writing. 
The oldest physical sources we have today are **fragmentary papyri** and medieval manuscripts that are **copies of much older versions**. 

Historical Context

1. **Oral Composition**: Scholars believe the poems were composed in the late **8th-7th c. BC** by bards who performed them from memory.
2. The **Peisistratean Recension**: *Ancient tradition suggests* the **first official written versions** were commissioned in **6th c. BC** Athens by the tyrant *Pisistratus* to standardize the poems for public recitation.
3. **Alexandrian Scholarship**: The **version** we read **today** was largely stabilized by scholars at the **Library of Alexandria** (e.g., ***Aristarchus of Samothrace***) between **285-150 BC**. 

Earliest Fragments

1. **Olbia Pottery** (Ukraine): A broken **pottery piece** (ostracon) from the **5th c. BC** contains a single line from the **Odyssey (9.39)** and is **one of the earliest direct quotes** found in the archaeological record.
2. **Ptolemaic Papyri**: Some of the oldest known manuscript fragments of the Odyssey were produced in Ptolemaic Egypt during the **3rd c. BC**.
3. **Milan Papyrus**: A fragment containing portions of the **Iliad** that dates to the **2nd c. AD**. 
4. **The Odyssey Clay Tablet** (Olympia, Greece): Discovered in 2018, this Roman-era tablet contains 13 verses of Book 14 and is considered the **oldest written extract of the Odyssey** found on Greek soil, dating possibly to the **3rd c. AD**.


Oldest Complete Manuscripts

Because ancient papyrus is fragile, the earliest complete versions of the epics come from the medieval period: 

1. **Venetus A (Marcianus Graecus Z. 454)**: Housed in the Marciana Library in Venice, this **10th c. AD** manuscript is the **oldest complete text** of the **Iliad**.
2. **Medieval Odyssey Copies**: The oldest complete copies of the **Odyssey** generally date to the **10th-13th c. AD**.

## Thales

**No original manuscripts written by Thales of Miletus (c. 624–546 BCE) survive today**, and it is uncertain whether he ever produced any written works. Instead, his teachings and life are known through the writings of later authors, **preserved in medieval copies**. 

There are **no surviving original writings** authored by Thales of Miletus. Everything known about his life and philosophy is derived from later *doxographical* accounts—compilations of his opinions and achievements *written by subsequent thinkers*. 

The **oldest and most significant** sources for Thales include:

1. ***Herodotus (5th c. BC)***: Writing roughly 100 years after Thales, the historian provides the **earliest extant account** of Thales' **prediction of a solar eclipse in 585 BC**. He also describes Thales' political advice to the Ionian cities.
2. ***Aristotle (4th c. BC)***: Widely considered the **most authoritative source**, *Aristotle* identifies *Thales* as the *founder of natural philosophy* in his work *Metaphysics*. **He credits Thales with the theory that** ***water is the primary principle*** **and that** ***all things are full of gods***.
3. ***Diogenes Laertius (3rd c. AD)***: His work *Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers* is the **most comprehensive biographical** source. Although writing eight centuries later, **he quotes** earlier, now-lost sources like **Apollodorus of Athens** (2nd c. BC) and **Eudemus of Rhodes** (4th c. BC).
4. ***Proclus (5th c. AD)***: In his Commentary on *Euclid*, *Proclus* provides critical information on *Thales' mathematical discoveries*, likely drawing from *Eudemus's* lost *History of Geometry*. 

While some **ancient reports** mention specific titles such as ***On the Solstice*** or the ***Nautical Star-guide***, their authenticity was **questioned** even in antiquity, and no fragments of these works exist today. While works like the **Nautical Star-guide** or **On the Solstice** were **attributed to Thales in antiquity**, they were **lost before the medieval** period began. 


The primary **manuscript sources** for Thales are actually **medieval codices** of other ancient authors:


1. **Codex Laurentianus 70.3** (MS A), dating to the **10th c.**, is the **oldest surviving complete manuscript** of *Herodotus'* work. ***Herodotus, Histories***: *Herodotus* recorded *Thales'* prediction of **the 585 BC solar eclipse**.
2. **Codex Parisinus Graecus 1853** (MS E), dating to the **10th c.**, is **one of the oldest** and **most reliable** manuscripts containing *Aristotle’s* ***Metaphysics***. ***Aristotle, Metaphysics***: Aristotle is the **earliest major source** to describe *Thales'* philosophical **theories** (e.g., that *all things are water*).
3. **Codex Borbonicus** (Naples, Biblioteca Nazionale, III.B.29), dating to the **late 12th c.**, is **one of the most important** and **oldest** primary witnesses to this text. ***Diogenes Laërtius, Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers***: This is the most comprehensive source for Thales' biography and fragments.

## Hecataios


There is **no single original manuscript or physical document** of *Hekataios (Hecataeus) of Miletus* that has survived from antiquity. His **original works**, primarily the *Periodos Ges* (*Description of the Earth*) and the Genealogiai, **are lost**. 
**World Map**: No original exists, but its **reconstruction** is based on **descriptions in later texts**. It visualized the earth as a flat disc surrounded by a circumfluent ocean.

The oldest sources we have for his work are indirect fragments preserved as **quotes or references by later ancient authors**: 


1. ***Herodotus (5th c. BC)***: The **most famous early source**. In his *Histories, Herodotus* discusses *Hekataios’s* role in the *Ionian Revolt* and famously critiques his maps and geographical theories.
2. ***Strabo (1st c. BC/AD): The geographer Strabo refers to *Hekataios* as one of the first writers of scientific geography and **as a successor** to ***Anaximander***. 
3. **The Oxyrhynchus Papyri (1st c. AD)**: some of the oldest physical scraps of text *quoting Hekataios* have been found among Egyptian papyrus fragments dating back to the Roman period. 
4. ***Stephanus of Byzantium (6th c. AD)***: Though writing much later, his *geographical lexicon* ***Ethnica*** is the **primary source** for the majority of the surviving fragments of *Hekataios's Periodos Ges*.

There are **no surviving original medieval manuscripts** of the works of Hekataios of Milet (Hecataeus of Miletus). His two known major works—the geography-focused ***Ges Periodos*** (Description of the Earth) and the mythological **Genealogiai***—have **not survived** in their entirety. 

The text of Hekataios **exists only through fragments** preserved *as citations or summaries** in the works of later authors. These *fragments* are found in various medieval and Byzantine manuscript traditions, the most important of which include: 

Primary Sources of Fragments

1. ***Stephanus of Byzantium***: The **6th c. AD** geographer's lexicon, ***Ethnica***, is the single most important source, **containing** hundreds of short **quotes** (fragments) from the *Ges Periodos*.
2. **Byzantine Anthologies** (e.g., ***Photius***): The ***Bibliotheca*** of the **9th c. AD** Patriarch Photius preserves **specific passages**, such as Hekataios' discourse on the Jews.
3. ***Herodotus***: **Codex Laurentianus 70.3** **10th c.** While not a manuscript of *Hekataios* himself, Herodotus’ Histories (5th century BC) is the **earliest work** to engage extensively with Hekataios' ideas, often critiquing his geographic and genealogical claims. 

Modern Scholarly Collections

Since the original texts are missing, scholars have compiled these scattered quotes into definitive editions. The standard reference today is: 

- ***Felix Jacoby’s Die Fragmente der griechischen Historiker (FGrHist)***: This is the fundamental modern source used by historians. *Fragment 310, for example, is a well-known piece regarding Greek-Egyptian interactions*.

## Anaxagoras

There is **no single original source** for *Anaxagoras* because his *only known book*, typically titled ***On Nature***, has not survived in its entirety. Instead, our knowledge of his work comes from fragments (verbatim quotations) and testimonia (reports of his views) *preserved by later ancient authors*. 

The most critical and ***primary sources*** for these fragments include:

1. ***Plato*** **(c. 427-347 BC)**: One of the earliest contemporary *mentions of Anaxagoras' book* occurs ***in Plato's Apology (26d)***, where *Socrates* notes that the work could be bought for a drachma at a bookshop. Plato's ***Phaedo*** also famously describes Socrates' reaction to hearing a reading from the book.
2. ***Aristotle*** **(384-322 BC)**: A **major source** of testimonia, *Aristotle* discussed *Anaxagoras* extensively in his ***Physics and Metaphysics***, though he often viewed *Anaxagoras'* theories through his own philosophical lens.
3. ***Theophrastus*** **(c. 372-288 BC)**: A student of *Aristotle* who wrote on the history of philosophy, he is a **key source** for *Anaxagoras' views on sensation and perception*.
4. **The Derveni Papyrus** **(c. 340 BC)**: Discovered in 1962, this is the oldest surviving Greek manuscript. While not a copy of *On Nature*, it is a philosophical treatise and commentary produced in the circle of Anaxagoras. It incorporates Anaxagorean physical principles—such as *the role of Nous (Mind)*—*to explain Orphic myths*, providing early evidence of his influence. 
5. ***Diogenes Laërtius*** **(3rd c. AD)**: Provides biographical details and a catalog of *Anaxagoras'* life and work in his *Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers*. 
6. ***Simplicius of Cilicia*** **(6th c. AD)**: He is the **single most important source**, providing the majority of the verbatim fragments we possess today. As a Neoplatonist commentator on *Aristotle*, he quoted large sections of *Anaxagoras'* book to preserve them for posterity. Most of the surviving literal fragments of Anaxagoras exist because Simplicius quoted them extensively in his **commentary on Aristotle’s Physics**. He is the **primary source** for the approximately 1200 words (about 17–20 fragments) that remain.

Status of the Original Work

1. **Availability in Antiquity**: In the **5th c. BC**, Anaxagoras’s book was widely available and famously inexpensive; *Plato’s Socrates* notes in the **Apology** that it *could be bought for a drachma at most* in the Athenian marketplace.
2. **Final Disappearance**: The complete text was reportedly **available until at least the 6th c. AD** before it was eventually lost. 

Medieval manuscripts

There is **no surviving original medieval manuscript** of *Anaxagoras’* treatise *On Nature*. Like most Pre-Socratic works, his book is **lost to history** and exists only through fragments preserved in the works of later authors.

Content of the Fragments

The fragments of **On Nature** (Greek: Περὶ Φύσεως) *describe a cosmos* ***governed by Mind*** (**Nous**) and a *primordial mixture* of ***all things***: 

1. **The Principle of Nous**: *Anaxagoras* **introduced Mind** as an *infinite, self-ruled force* that *organized the universe* from a state of *chaos*.
2. **Everything in Everything**: He argued that *every* substance contains a portion of *every other* substance, meaning *nothing comes into being or passes away* but is merely mixed or separated.
3. **Celestial Bodies**: He famously claimed the *Sun is a red-hot stone* and the *Moon is made of earth and reflects the Sun's light*—theories that led to his **trial for impiety**.

## Democritus of Abdera

**None of Democritus of Abdera’s original works have survived** in their complete, original form. Although he was a prolific author credited with over 70 treatises on topics ranging from physics and ethics to mathematics and music, his entire body of work has been lost to time. 


Our modern understanding of his philosophy is reconstructed through two main types of indirect evidence: 

Direct Fragments

While complete texts are lost, several hundred fragments (brief quotations or close paraphrases) have been preserved within the works of later authors. 

1. **Sayings and Ethics**: A significant number of his ethical maxims were preserved in the 5th c. anthology of ***Stobaeus***.
2. **Scholarly Collections**: The standard academic reference for these fragments is the Diels-Kranz (DK) collection (Die Fragmente der Vorsokratiker), which compiles all known snippets from ancient sources. 


Ancient Testimonies (Testimonia)

Most of what we know comes from the reports and critiques of other ancient writers:

1. ***Aristotle***: The most important source, *Aristotle* was a **rival** who discussed *Democritus' atomic theories* extensively to **argue against** them, particularly in On *Generation and Corruption*. He even wrote a dedicated monograph on *Democritus*, though it too is largely lost.
2. ***Theophrastus***: Recorded early scientific views, sometimes attributing major works like the Great World System to *Democritus’ mentor*, ***Leucippus***, instead. 
3. ***Epicurus***: A *follower of atomism* whose works (preserved partly by *Diogenes Laertius*) **reflect and expand** upon *Democritus'* physical theories.
4. ***Diogenes Laertius***: In his *Lives of Eminent Philosophers* (**Book 9**), he provides a **detailed** list of **Democritus’** works and biographical anecdotes.


Because no direct manuscripts of his treatises exist, *earliest medieval manuscripts* refers to the **earliest surviving** copies of other authors' works that quote or summarize him. 

Primary Sources of Transmission

The **survival of Democritus' ideas in the medieval period** relies heavily on the manuscript traditions of his commentators and critics: 

1. ***Aristotle (4th c. BC)***: The most critical source for Democritean physics and atomism. Earliest extant medieval manuscripts of Aristotle’s works (such as **On Generation** and **Corruption or Metaphysics**) generally date from the **9th-10th c. AD** in the Byzantine tradition.
2. ***Johannes Stobaeus (5th c. AD)***: Compiled an anthology that preserves many of *Democritus’ ethical maxims*. The earliest substantial manuscripts for *Stobaeus* date to the **10th-11th c.**
3. ***Simplicius (6th c. AD)***: A Neoplatonist whose commentaries on Aristotle often preserve detailed fragments of earlier philosophers. His manuscripts typically date from the **11th c.** onwards. 
3. ***Diogenes Laertius (3rd c. AD)***: His *Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers* contains a comprehensive list of *Democritus’ purported works* and biographical anecdotes. The most important medieval manuscripts for this text, such as **Codex Borbonicus** and **Codex Laurentianus**, date from the **12th-13th c.**


Context of Survival

1. **Loss of Original Texts**: Despite reportedly writing over 70 treatises on topics ranging from ethics to mathematics, his books were **already becoming rare in late antiquity**. Legend claims *Plato disliked his work* so much he wished to burn all his books.
2. **Pseudo-Democritus**: In the early medieval period, a *separate* **Democritus tradition emerged in alchemy**. The earliest medieval manuscripts for these alchemical texts (often attributed to *Democritus of Abdera*) appear in Greek and Arabic traditions from the 8th-9th c.

## Herodot

The oldest original sources of Herodotus’s Histories are categorized into two types: physical fragments on papyrus and nearly complete medieval manuscripts. **No original copy** written by Herodotus himself in the 5th c. BC **exists today**. 

Earliest Physical Fragments

The oldest physical remains of Herodotus's text are fragmentary scraps found on papyrus, **primarily** from the **Oxyrhynchus Papyri** collection in Egypt. 

History Stack Exchange

1. **P. Oxy. 1619**: Dated to the late **1st c. AD**, this is one of the earliest known physical fragments of the text.
2. **P. Oxy. 1375** & **Munich Papyrus A**: Both date to the **1st-2nd** c. AD.
3. **Other fragments: Numerous** other pieces (e.g. **P. Oxy. 2099**, **1092**) date from the 2nd-4th c. AD. 

Oldest Major Manuscripts

The original sources used for modern translations are **medieval Byzantine manuscripts**, which preserve the text nearly in its entirety. 

1. **Codex Laurentianus (Codex A)**: Located in the Biblioteca Medicea Laurenziana in Florence, this is the most important extant copy. It dates to the **9th-10th c. AD** (some sources suggest the 9th century) and serves as the primary basis for modern scholarly editions.
2. **Codex Angelicanus (Codex B)**: Held in Rome and dating to the **11th c. AD**, it is closely related to Codex A.
3. **Vatican Graecus 2369** (Codex D): An **11th-12th c. AD** manuscript that is largely complete but contains some missing leaves and scribal errors. 
4. **Common Ancient Edition**: Scholars believe most surviving manuscripts and papyri **derive from a single standard edition** compiled in the early centuries AD.

First Printed Editions

The first mechanical reproductions of these manuscripts appeared during the Renaissance: 
1. **First Latin Translation**: Published in **1474** in Venice, translated by *Lorenzo Valla*.
2. **First Greek Print** (Editio Princeps): Published by the Aldine Press (Aldus Manutius) in **1502**, which helped stabilize the Greek text for future study. 

Modern Critical Editions

Because no single *perfect* copy exists, modern scholars use a *critical apparatus* to compare different manuscripts and reconstruct the original Greek text. 

1. **Oxford Classical Texts (OCT)**: Edited by Karl Hude (1926) or N.G. Wilson (2015), these are standard academic versions.
Loeb Classical Library: Provides the Greek text alongside an English translation (e.g., by A.D. Godley).
2. **Digital Access**: You can read translated versions for free on platforms like Project Gutenberg or explore parallel Greek/English versions on Sacred Texts. 

How to Cite Herodotus

In academic settings, he is typically cited by his abbreviated name and the book/chapter numbers (e.g., Hdt. 1.78).

## Plato

**Medieval manuscripts** are the **primary** reason we have *Plato’s* **dialogues** today. While Plato wrote in the 4th c. BC, the earliest complete handwritten copies that survive date to around 900 AD. 


Most Significant Medieval Manuscripts 

1. **Codex Oxoniensis Clarkianus 39** (The *Clarke Plato*): Completed in **895 AD** in Constantinople, this is the **oldest surviving complete copy** of the **first six tetralogies** (24 dialogues). It was commissioned by *Arethas of Caesarea* and written by the scribe *John the Calligrapher*.
2. **Codex Parisinus Graecus 1807**: Dating to approximately **900 AD**, this manuscript is the **primary source** for the **final tetralogies**, including the ***Republic, Timaeus, and Laws***.
3. **Codex Venetus Marcianus App. gr. IV, 1** (Codex T): A **12th c.** manuscript (previously thought to be 10th) held in Venice that preserves unique readings for several dialogues.
4. **Codex Vindobonensis Suppl. Gr. 7**: A **12th c.** manuscript from Vienna that influenced Renaissance humanists like *Marsilio Ficino*. 

The Transmission Tradition

The **dialogues were traditionally arranged into nine tetralogies** (groups of four), a system **attributed to** the 1st c. astrologer ***Thrasyllus***. 


1. **Byzantine Preservation**: ***Most*** of these manuscripts were produced and preserved in the Byzantine Empire.
2. **Western Access**: For much of the Middle Ages, Western Europe only had access to a **partial Latin** translation of the **Timaeus** by *Calcidius*. It wasn't until the 15th c. Renaissance that the full corpus became widely available in the West.
3. **Scholia**: Many medieval copies include scholia—marginal notes and commentary added by medieval scholars like *Arethas*—which provide insight into how *Plato was interpreted in the Middle Ages*.

## Aristoteles

There are **no surviving original manuscripts** (autographs) written by Aristotle himself. His works, *collectively known* as the **Corpus Aristotelicum**, have been preserved through a process of *centuries-long copying*, resulting in a **gap of over 1000 years** between his death (322 BC) and the **oldest near-complete surviving manuscripts**. 

The oldest primary sources for Aristotle's texts are categorized by their medium and completeness:

Oldest Surviving Papyrus Fragment (c. 100 AD) 

The earliest direct physical evidence of an Aristotelian text is a ***papyrus copy of the Constitution of Athens*** (Athenaion Politeia). 

1. **Origin**: Discovered in **Egypt** in the **late 19th c**..
2. **Significance**: It dates to approximately **100 AD**, roughly 400 years after Aristotle's death, making it significantly older than the major medieval codices. 

Oldest Major Greek Codices (9th-10th c. AD) 

Most of our **modern knowledge** of *Aristotle* comes from **Byzantine manuscripts** produced in Constantinople during the *Byzantine Renaissance*: 

1. **Codex Vindobonensis phil. gr. 100** (J): Dating to the **mid-9th c.**, it is considered the **oldest extant codex** *containing* a substantial portion of *Aristotle's* theoretical works, including the **Physics and Metaphysics**.
2. **Codex Parisinus gr. 1853** (E): Dating to the **early 10th c.**, it is one of the **most authoritative sources** for the **Corpus Physicum and Metaphysics**.
3. **MS 108** (Corpus Christi College, Oxford): An **early 9th c.** manuscript containing *Aristotle’s* ***biological** *works* (e.g., *History of Animals*), widely regarded as the **oldest surviving copy** for that specific field. 

Ancient Indirect Sources

Before the surviving physical manuscripts, Aristotle’s works were *shaped and cataloged by ancient scholars*: 

1. ***Andronicus of Rhodes*** (**1st c. BC**): He is credited with organizing and editing the disparate lecture notes into the structured treatises we recognize today.
2. ***Philodemus*** (**1st c. BC**): Cited as the **oldest known source** attesting to *Aristotle's* ***early lectures*** on rhetoric.
3. ***Diogenes Laertius*** (3rd c. AD): Provided one of the earliest comprehensive lists of *Aristotle's* works in his *Lives of Eminent Philosophers*. 

Critical Modern Edition

1. **Bekker Edition (1831)**: The **standard reference** for modern scholarship, compiled by *August Immanuel Bekker*. It established the **Bekker numbering system** still used to ***cite Aristotle today***.

## Epicur

The oldest original sources of Epicurus' philosophy (341–270 BC) are fragmented, as the **vast majority of his 300+ works were lost**. The primary extant materials that provide direct or nearly direct access to his thought include: 

1. **The Herculaneum Scrolls (c. 79 AD)**: These carbonized papyri, recovered from the **Villa of the Papyri**, contain the **most substantial** ***physical "original" fragments***. They include **parts** of *Epicurus'* massive 37-volume treatise **On Nature** (***Peri Physeôs***).
2. **Letters and Sayings preserved by Diogenes Laertius (3rd c. AD)**: While the biographer lived centuries later, he preserved the full text of three instructional letters attributed to Epicurus himself:
- **Letter to Herodotus**: Summarizes physical theory and atomism.
- **Letter to Menoeceus**: Covers ethical teachings.
- **Letter to Pythocles**: Discusses meteorology and astronomy (though some debate its authenticity).
- **Principal Doctrines** (***Kyriai Doxai***): A collection of 40 short aphorisms.
3. **The Oenoanda Inscription (2nd c. AD)**: A massive **stone wall inscription** in modern-day Turkey, erected by a follower named *Diogenes*, which records several ethical and physical treatises based directly on *Epicurus'* lost works.
4. **The Vatican Sayings**: A collection of **maxims discovered in a 14th c.manuscript** at the Vatican Library, many of which are attributed to *Epicurus*.
4. **Major Secondary Sources**: For reconstructing lost theories, scholars rely heavily on ***Lucretius*** (**1st c. BC**), whose poem ***De Rerum Natura*** is a faithful Latin exposition of *Epicurean physics*. 

The **primary surviving medieval** witnesses to Epicurus' own words are not direct codices of his works, which were largely lost during the Middle Ages, but rather his **texts preserved within the works of other** authors. The most significant are those found in ***Diogenes Laërtius'*** *Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers*. 


Primary Medieval sources

1. **Diogenes Laërtius, Book X**: This is the most crucial source. The manuscript tradition of Diogenes Laërtius preserves three complete letters from Epicurus (Letter to Herodotus, Letter to Pythocles, and Letter to Menoeceus) and his Principal Doctrines.

Oldest Manuscripts: The manuscript tradition is generally divided into two classes. The most important witnesses for reconstructing the text include:

1. **Codex Burbonicus** (B): Housed in Naples (Biblioteca Nazionale, III. B. 29), dating to the **12th c.**.
2. **Codex Parisinus** (P): Housed in Paris (BnF, gr. 1759), dating to the **13th c.**.
3. **Codex Laurentianus** (F): Housed in Florence, dating to the **13th-14th c.**. 


***Lucretius***: The Latin Transmission

Because direct Epicurean texts were scarce, the poem ***De Rerum Natura*** by the Roman poet ***Lucretius*** served as the **primary vehicle for Epicurean** philosophy in the West. 


1. **Codex Oblongus** (O): The **oldest major manuscript of Lucretius**, produced in a *Carolingian scriptorium* in the **early 9th c.**. It is currently held at Leiden University Libraries (MS VLF 30).
2. **Codex Quadratus** (Q): Another significant **9th-c.** manuscript, also held at Leiden (VLQ 94).
3. **Schedae Gottorpienses/Vindobonenses** (S): A collection of fragments dating to the **late 9th c.**, split between Copenhagen and Vienna. 


Other Significant Fragments

1. **British Library Papyrus 1182**: A rare **surviving ancient fragment** of an Epicurean treatise, though technically pre-medieval (Hellenistic/Roman), it was preserved as a palimpsest used for tax receipts.
2. **Herculaneum Papyri**: While not medieval manuscripts, these charred scrolls from the **1st c. AD** contain the only other extensive remains of ***Epicurus' On Nature***, discovered in the 1750s.

## Manetho Aegyptica

The Aegyptiaca (History of Egypt), written by the priest Manetho in the 3rd c. BC, is a lost work; ***no complete original** or contemporary **medieval manuscript exists**. Instead, the text survives **only in fragments and summaries** (epitomes) quoted by later authors. 

The most significant manuscript transmissions from the medieval period that **preserve these fragments** are:

1. ***George Syncellus*** (**9th c. AD**): The most comprehensive preservation of Manetho's king-lists comes from the Ekloge Chronographias by the *Byzantine monk George Syncellus*. He compiled earlier versions from Africanus and Eusebius, along with a "Pseudo-Manetho" text called the Book of Sothis.
2. ***The Armenian Version of Eusebius*** (**c. 5th-6th c. AD** source, later manuscripts): While Eusebius of Caesarea (4th c.) wrote a summary of *Manetho*, his original Greek text is lost. It is primarily preserved through an Armenian translation, which provides a crucial alternative version of the dynastic lists.
3. ***Flavius Josephus*** (**1st c. AD**): Though not a medieval source, the **oldest textual witnesses** of actual prose fragments (rather than just lists of names) are found in *Josephus's Against Apion*. Medieval manuscripts of Josephus's works are the earliest direct copies of *Manetho's* actual phrasing. 

Key Transmitters of the Text

The *Manetho* we read today is a reconstruction *based on these primary sources*: 

1. ***Sextus Julius Africanus (early 3rd c. AD)***: His version is considered the most accurate to the original, though it survives only through Syncellus.
2. ***Eusebius of Caesarea (4th c. AD)***: A Christian bishop whose version often differs from Africanus, preserved in Armenian and Latin translations.
3. ***George Syncellus (9th c. AD)***: The *bridge* to the Middle Ages who **preserved both Africanus and Eusebius** in his **universal chronicle**.

## Euclid of Alexandria (c. 300 BC)

**No original manuscripts** written by Euclid of Alexandria (c. 300 BC) survive. Because papyrus rots in damp conditions, the original scrolls likely perished within a few centuries of his life. All modern knowledge of Euclid's work, primarily the Elements, is derived from later copies, medieval translations, and a handful of ancient fragments. 


Earliest Extant Fragments

The oldest physical evidence of Euclid’s work consists of small fragments found in Egypt, where the extremely dry climate preserved them: 


1. **Elephantine Ostraca (3rd c. BC)**: Six clay shards (ostraca) found on Elephantine Island are the ***oldest surviving*** physical evidence. They contain diagrams and text related to Book XIII (polyhedra), though the phrasing differs from later standard versions.
2. **Herculaneum Carbonized Papyrus (c. 100 BC)**: Parts of Book I preserved as citations in a philosophical treatise recovered from the ruins of the eruption of Vesuvius. 
3. **Oxyrhynchus Papyrus 29 (c. 75-125 AD)**: One of the oldest papyrus fragments, containing a diagram and text for Book II, Proposition 5.

Most Significant Medieval Manuscripts 

Complete or **nearly complete versions** of Euclid only appear starting in the 9th c. AD. 

1. **Arabic Translations (8th-9th c. AD)**: During the medieval period, scholars like ***Al-Hajjaj*** and ***Thabit ibn Qurra*** translated *Euclid into Arabic*. These versions sometimes preserved traditions closer to the original text than surviving Greek copies. 
2. **MS D’Orville 301 (888 AD)**: Known as ***the Bodleian Euclid***, this is the oldest complete manuscript of a classical Greek author to bear a specific date. It was copied by ***Stephen the Clerk*** in Constantinople and is now housed at the Bodleian Library at Oxford University.
3. **Vat. gr. 190 (10th c. AD)**: A crucial manuscript found in the Vatican Library. It is **the only non-Theonine witness**, meaning it does not include the 4th c. alterations and additions made by ***Theon of Alexandria*** that appear in almost every other surviving Greek manuscript, it is considered the most important for historical accuracy. Found in 1808 by *François Peyrard*, it allowed scholars to identify later additions and *restore* Euclid's original text. 
4. **Codex Leidensis 399/1 (10th-11th c.)**:
Located in Leiden, this is a critical **Arabic manuscript** preserving the translation by ***al-Hajjaj***. It includes commentaries by ***al-Nayrizi*** and reflects the intensive study of *Euclid* during the Islamic Golden Age. 
5. **Trinity College MS 47 (c. 1150)**: Held at Oxford, this is likely the oldest complete Latin translation. Attributed to ***Adelard of Bath***, who translated it **from Arabic into Latin**. Contains Arabic words crossed out and replaced with Latin equivalents, suggesting it may be Adelard's own working copy. 

Modern Preservation

Most historical research today relies on the critical edition established by ***J. L. Heiberg (1883–1888)***, who **synthesized** the **Vatican manuscript** with various **papyri fragments** to reconstruct a text as close to *Euclid's* **original as possible**.
