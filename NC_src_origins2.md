## The Greek Dark Ages (12th-9th c. BC)

was a period in Ancient Greece (c. 1180–800 BC) characterized by societal **collapse of civilization**, where the palaces and cities of the **Mycenaeans** were either *destroyed, abandoned*, or both.

At around the same time, the **Hittite civilization** in modern-day Turkey also suffered **serious disruption and collapse**, with cities from Troy to Gaza being destroyed. 

Moreover, in Egypt, the **New Kingdom** fell into disarray, leading to the **Third Intermediate Period of Egypt**. Following this *mass destruction*, there were fewer, smaller settlements, which suggests widespread famine and depopulation. 

On the *Greek mainland*, the **Linear B script**, used by *Mycenaean* bureaucrats to write the Greek language, **ceased** to be used. The later Greek alphabet did not develop until hundreds of years later, in the beginning of the *Protohistoric Iron Age*, c. 800 BC.

## Corineus, Brutus (12th c. BC)

After **Brutus**, a descendant of the *Trojan prince* *Aeneas*, had been exiled from Italy and liberated the *enslaved Trojans in Greece*, he encountered *Corineus* and his people, who joined him in his travels. **Corineus** led the descendants of the Trojans who fled with *Antenor* after the *Trojan War* and settled on the coasts of the Tyrrhenian Sea (Monmouth, History of the Kings of Britain, 1136). The Trojans crossed to the *island of Albion*, which *Brutus* renamed *Britain* after himself, *Corineus* settled in Cornwall, which was then inhabited by *giants* (most notably *Gogmagogy*). 

*Brutus* founded a capital on the banks of the River Thames, naming it *Troia Nova*, *New Troy*, known as *Trinovantum* and later *London*. 

After *Brutus* died the rest of Britain was divided between his three sons, 

1. ***Locrinus*** (Loegria)
2. ***Kamber*** (Cambria)
3. ***Albanactus*** (Alba), he inherited *Albania* or *Albany* (Scotland).

## Hermes Trismegistos (Thot, c. 6000–3150 BC / Enoch, 3332–2967 BC)

Hermes Trismegistos (Greek for *Thrice-Greatest Hermes*) is a **legendary figure** from the **Hellenistic period**, created through the syncretic **blending of** the Greek god **Hermes** and the Egyptian god **Thoth**. He is revered as the ***mythical founder of Hermeticism***, a philosophical and religious tradition that significantly influenced Western alchemy and astrology. 

### Historical & Legendary Timeline

#### Antiquity (3rd c. BC – 3rd c. AD):

1. **3rd–2nd c. BC**: The earliest *Technical Hermetica*—practical texts on astrology and magic—began to **appear** in *Greek-speaking Egypt*.
2. **1st–3rd c. AD**: The ***Philosophical Hermetica***, including the famous ***Corpus Hermeticum***, were composed in Roman Egypt. These texts blended *Greek* philosophy (*Platonism and Stoicism*) with *Egyptian mysticism*.

#### Medieval & Islamic Tradition (8th – 11th c. AD):

Arabic scholars often identified *Hermes* with the biblical patriarch ***Enoch*** (or the Quranic prophet *Idris*).

1. **Some traditions spoke of three distinct Hermeses**:
- the first *before the Great Flood*, 
- the second in *Babylon* (who *taught Pythagoras*)
- the third in *Egypt* as the ***father of alchemy***.

#### The Renaissance (15th – 16th c.):

Scholars like *Marsilio Ficino* (who translated the ***Corpus Hermeticum*** in 1463) ***believed*** *Hermes* was a real person from remote antiquity—a ***contemporary of Moses*** who provided a *prisca theologia* (ancient theology) that ***prefigured Christianity***.

#### The Critical Turning Point (1614):

*Linguistic analysis* by *Isaac Casaubon* proved the texts were not from ancient Egypt but were written in the ***early Christian era***. This discovery effectively ended *Hermes'* status as a historical prophet in mainstream academic circles.

### Aspects of Hermes Trismegistos

1. **The Thrice-Greatest Title**: The epithet Trismegistos likely stems from Egyptian descriptions of Thoth as *great, great, greatest*. During the Renaissance, it was often interpreted as him being the greatest philosopher, priest, and king.
2. **The Corpus Hermeticum**: He is the purported author of the Hermetica, a collection of Greek texts from the 1st–3rd c. AD that explore the nature of the divine, the cosmos, and the human soul.
3. **The Emerald Tablet (Tabula Smaragdina)**: This highly influential alchemical text is famously attributed to him. It contains the foundational maxim *As above, so below* (Latin: *Quod est inferius, est sicut quod est superius*), expressing the correspondence between the microcosm and macrocosm.

### Legacy and Influence:

1. **Renaissance**: Thinkers like Marsilio Ficino and Giordano Bruno viewed him as a pagan prophet who foresaw Christianity.
2. **Alchemy**: Known as the father of *Hermetic art*, his name is the origin of the term *hermetically sealed*.
3. **Islamic Tradition**: Often identified with the prophet ***Idris*** (the biblical ***Enoch***, the seventh from Adam and lived approximately between 3332–2967 BC according to some biblical chronologies), he is seen as a *civilizing hero* who taught science and wisdom.
4. **Historical Authenticity**: While long believed to be a real contemporary of *Moses*, the scholar *Isaac Casaubon* demonstrated in 1614 that the Hermetic writings actually date to the post-Christian era. 


The timeline of Hermes Trismegistus is not a biography of a single historical person, as modern scholarship concludes he never existed. Instead, it is a history of a mythological figure formed from the **syncretism** of the Egyptian god **Thoth** and the Greek god **Hermes**. 

Worship of the Egyptian god *Thoth (Djehuty)* spanned more than 6000 years, making it one of the longest continuous periods of veneration for any deity in history. 

### Timeline of Worship

1. **Beginning (c. 6000–3150 BC)**: Evidence suggests *Thoth* was one of the earliest *Egyptian deities*, with worship starting in Lower Egypt during the Pre-Dynastic Period.
2. **Old Kingdom (c. 2686–2160 BC)**: His cult became firmly established, and he is frequently ***mentioned in the Pyramid Texts*** (c. 2400 BC).
3. **Middle and New Kingdoms (c. 2055–1077 BC)**: Royal patronage increased significantly. Pharaohs like ***Thutmose (meaning Born of Thoth)*** took his name, and grand statues were commissioned by rulers such as *Amenhotep III* and *Ramesses II*.
4. **Ptolemaic and Roman Periods (323 BC – 4th c. AD)**: His worship continued until the end of dynastic Egyptian history. **During this time**, he was often **syncretized** with the Greek god **Hermes**. 

The reference to *Thoth 36000 BC* stems from the ***Emerald Tablets of Thoth-the-Atlantean***, a modern occult text published in the 1930s. This text is not an ancient artifact but a creation of ***Maurice Doreal*** (Claude Doggins), the founder of the Brotherhood of the *White Temple*. 

### According to Doreal's text:

1. **Origin**: Thoth was an Atlantean Priest-King who supposedly lived through the sinking of Atlantis and subsequently founded a colony in ancient Egypt.
2. **Reign**: He is claimed to have ruled Egypt from approximately 52000-36000 BC.
3. **Achievements**: The text credits him with building the Great Pyramid of Giza (traditionally attributed to Khufu/Cheops) and secreting ancient Atlantean records and advanced technology within it.
4. **Immortality**: Thoth is described as an immortal who conquered death and could incarnate into different bodies, eventually becoming known as *Hermes Trismegistus*. 

### Historical vs. Modern Context

1. **Historical Thoth**: In authentic Egyptian mythology, **Thoth is the god of writing(!)**, *wisdom*, and the *moon*, with his earliest worship dating to the Pre-Dynastic period (c. 6000–3150 BC).
2. **The Emerald Tablet**: There is a historical *Emerald Tablet* (Smaragdine Tablet) that originated in Arabic alchemical texts around the 7th-8th c. AD.
3. **Doreal’s Version**: The ***36000 BC*** version is unique to Doreal's 20th c. writings and is generally regarded by historians and scientists as a *pseudohistorical hoax* or modern mystical fiction rather than a genuine ancient record.


### Ancient Origins (Pre 1st c. BC)

1. **27th–22nd c. BC**: Worship of Thoth, the Egyptian lunar deity and patron of writing, begins in Egypt's Old Kingdom.
2. **3rd c. BC**: In Ptolemaic Egypt, the identification of Thoth with the Greek god Hermes becomes official.
3. **196 BC**: The Rosetta Stone refers to Hermes as *the great, the great*, an epithet derived from the Egyptian *aa aa* (greatest), which evolved into *Trismegistus (Thrice-Greatest)*. 

### The Emergence of Hermeticism (1st c. BC – 4th c. AD) 

1. **1st c. BC – 1st c. AD**: The earliest Hermetic texts, focusing on astrology and magic (*popular* Hermetism), begin to appear.
2. **1st c  AD**: *Plutarch* makes early mentions of Hermes Trismegistus.
3. **2nd – 3rd c AD**: The core philosophical texts known as the Corpus Hermeticum are composed in Roman Egypt.
4. **4th c  AD**: The Asclepius, a Latin dialogue attributed to Hermes, circulates and later becomes a key text for medieval scholars. 

### Middle Ages and Islamic Tradition (5th – 14th c.)

1. **7th – 9th c. AD**: Islamic scholars identify Hermes Trismegistus with the prophet Idris (Enoch). The *threefold Hermes* legend develops: a civilizing hero, a Babylonian teacher, and an Egyptian alchemist.
2. **12th – 13th c.**: Western European scholars like *Roger Bacon* and *Albertus Magnus* reference Hermes as the ***Father of Philosophers***. 


### Renaissance Revival (15th – 16th c.)

1. **1460**: A monk brings a manuscript of the Corpus Hermeticum to Florence.
2. **1471**: Marsilio Ficino publishes his Latin translation of the first 14 books, believing Hermes was a contemporary of Moses and a source of all ancient wisdom (*prisca theologia*).
3. **1554**: The original Greek text of the Corpus is published in Paris. 


### Decline and Modern Esotericism (17th c  – Present)

1. **1614**: Scholar *Isaac Casaubon* proves through linguistic analysis that the Hermetic texts were written in the post-Christian era (**1st–3rd c. AD**), not in ancient Egypt.
2. **18th – 19th c.**: While dismissed by academia, Hermeticism remains central to occult groups like the Rosicrucians and Freemasons.
3. **1908**: Publication of The Kybalion, which modernizes Hermetic thought into the Seven Hermetic Principles (e.g., Mentalism, Polarity).
4. **20th c. – Present**: Scientific study of the texts is revitalized by discoveries like the Nag Hammadi library. 

There are **no original manuscripts** written by the legendary figure *Hermes Trismegistus*. Most scholars agree he is a mythical composite of the Greek god Hermes and the Egyptian god Thoth. The *original* texts attributed to him were actually composed by various anonymous authors in Roman Egypt between roughly 200 BC and 300 AD. 

The surviving manuscripts we have today are much later copies, translations, or fragments of these ancient works: 

#### ***The Corpus Hermeticum (Greek)***

This is the most famous collection of 17 or 18 treatises (including the Poimandres). 

1. **Manuscript Age**: While the content dates to the **1st–3rd c. AD**, the *surviving Greek manuscripts* used for modern editions are Byzantine copies dating from the 14th-16th c..
2. **Key Source**: Most modern translations are based on a 14th c. manuscript brought to Italy in 1460 and translated by ***Marsilio Ficino***. 

#### ***The Nag Hammadi Library (Coptic)***

In 1945, archaeologists discovered a cache of 4th c. AD *Coptic manuscripts* in Egypt. 

1. **Originality**: These are some of the oldest physical Hermetic manuscripts in existence, dating to roughly **340–370 AD**.
2. **Contents**: They include Coptic translations of texts like The ***Discourse on the Eighth and Ninth*** and fragments of the ***Asclepius***. 


#### ***The Asclepius (Latin)***

*Originally written in Greek* as the ***Logos Teleios***, the full Greek version is *now lost*. 

1. **Surviving Version**: It survives primarily through an Ancient Latin translation.
2. **Manuscripts**: The most important Latin manuscripts for this text date to the **12th-13th c.** 

#### ***The Stobaeus Fragments***

We only know of many other Hermetic works because they were ***quoted*** or excerpted by ***John Stobaeus***, a 5th c. anthologist. 

- **Significance**: These fragments provide the only surviving text for works like the ***Kóré Kósmou (Virgin of the World)***. 

#### ***Papyri and Other Fragments***

Small, physically older fragments on papyrus have been found in archaeological contexts: 

1. **Vienna Hermetica**: Four short fragments preserved on the back of **2nd c. AD** papyri.
2. **Oxford Fragments**: Short fragments in a **13th-14th c.** codex at the Bodleian Library.
3. **Emerald Tablet**: Though central to alchemy, its original Greek is lost; the earliest extant version is in Arabic, dated to around **934 AD**. 

The earliest surviving medieval manuscripts of works attributed to Hermes Trismegistus vary depending on the specific text and the language of transmission (Arabic, Latin, or Greek). 

### Earliest Medieval Manuscripts by Text

1. ***The Emerald Tablet (Arabic)***: The earliest verifiable versions of this foundational alchemical text appear in Arabic manuscripts from the late **8th-9th c.** One of the oldest recensions is found in the ***Book of the Secret of Creation (Leipzig, Vollers 832)***.
2. ***The Emerald Tablet (Latin)***: The first Latin translations appeared in the **12th c.**, notably by *Hugo of Santalla*, which introduced the work to European scholars.
3. ***Corpus Hermeticum (Greek)***: While individual treatises were quoted as early as the 2nd c. AD, the compilation we know today was first attested by the 11th c. Byzantine philosopher *Michael Psellos*. The earliest surviving physical manuscript of this collection, which contains the first 14 treatises and was later used by *Marsilio Ficino*, dates to the **14th c.** and is held at the Laurentian Library in Florence.
4. ***Asclepius (Latin)***: Unlike most other philosophical Hermetica, the *Asclepius* was available in the Latin West throughout the Middle Ages via an early Latin translation from late antiquity.
5. ***The Definitions of Hermes Trismegistus to Asclepius (Armenian)***: This collection of aphorisms (Redewendung) survives primarily in a **6th c. AD** Armenian translation, though the core teachings likely date back to the 1st c. AD.
6. ***Oxford Fragments***: These are preserved in ***Codex Clarkianus gr. II***, a manuscript dating to the **13th-14th c.** held at the Bodleian Library in Oxford. 


### Collections & Repositories

1. ***Codex Clarkianus gr. II***: Contains the Oxford Hermetica fragments.
2. **Laurentian Library (Biblioteca Medicea Laurenziana)**: Houses the 14th c. Greek manuscript of the *Pimander* brought to *Cosimo de' Medici* in 1460.
3. **Bibliotheca Philosophica Hermetica (Ritman Library)**: An extensive collection in Amsterdam dedicated to *Hermetic philosophy*, containing various medieval and early modern manuscripts.

### Hermetic philosophy

Hermetic philosophy, or ***Hermeticism***, is an ancient spiritual and philosophical tradition based on the teachings attributed to *Hermes Trismegistus* (Hermes the Thrice-Greatest), a legendary figure blending the Greek god *Hermes* and the Egyptian god *Thoth*. It emphasizes the pursuit of Gnosis—direct, experiential knowledge of the divine—and the understanding of universal laws that govern both the cosmos (macrocosm) and the individual (microcosm). 

#### The Seven Hermetic Principles

The 1908 text ***The Kybalion*** outlines seven key principles that define modern Hermetic philosophy: 

1. **Mentalism**: Reality stems from a universal, mental consciousness (*The All is Mind*).
2. **Correspondence**: *As above, so below*, indicating a harmony between various planes of existence.
3. **Vibration**: Everything in the universe is in constant motion and vibrates at different rates.
4. **Polarity**: Opposites are, in fact, identical in nature but differ in degree.
5. **Rhythm**: Life operates in cycles, with everything flowing in and out like a pendulum.
6. **Cause and Effect**: Every action has a corresponding cause and effect, eliminating chance.
7. **Gender**: Masculine and feminine principles are present in all things, guiding creation.

## Elias (9th c. BC)

*Elias* is the *New Testament* and *Islamic* (*Ilyās*) name for the Hebrew *prophet Elijah*, what means *My God is Yahweh*, a central figure in *Judaism*, *Christianity*, and *Islam*, renowned for his unwavering defense of *monotheism* and his ***miraculous departure from earth***.

>"Elias, einer der grösten Propheten des alten Testaments, unter der Regierung der Könige Achab und Josaphat, war von Thisbe in Gilead gebürtig. Er prophezeyte im Jahr 912. v. C. G. dem Achab und der Jesabel eine Dürre und Hunger, welche 3. Jahr und 6. Monate anhielt. Während dieser Zeit wurde Elias wunderbarer Weise in der Wüsten von den Raben, und hernach von einer Wittwe zu Zarpath oder Sarepta ernähret, deren weder ihr kleiner Vorrath an Mehl noch ihr weniges Oel aufgieng. Er erweckte seiner Wirthinn üderdiß ihren verstorbenen Sohn wieder; bestrafte hierauf Achab wegen seines Götzendienstes, und ließ, als sein Opfer durch Feuer vorn Himmel aus sein Gebet verzehrt wurde, vierhundertund fünfzig Baalspfaffen erwürgen. Jesabel wollte sich dieserwegen an Elias rächen; er flöhe aber in die Wüsten, wo ihm ein Engel Brod und Wasser brachte. Diese Speise setzte ihn in Stand, eine Reise von 40. >Elijah, one of the greatest prophets of the Old Testament, during the reign of Kings Ahab and Jehoshaphat, was a native of Thisbe in Gilead. In 912 BC, he prophesied to Ahab and Jezebel a drought and famine that lasted three years and six months. During this time, Elijah was miraculously fed in the desert by ravens, and later by a widow in Zarephath, whose neither small supply of flour nor oil ran out. He also resurrected his hostess's deceased son; then punished Ahab for his idolatry, and when his prayer was consumed by fire from heaven, he had four hundred and fifty Baal priests slain. Jezebel sought revenge on Elijah for this; however, he fled into the desert, where an angel brought him bread and water.  This food enabled him to undertake a journey of 40 days and 40 nights to Mount Horeb. There, he received from the Lord the command to anoint Hazael king in Syria, Jehu king in Israel, and Elisha prophet in his place. He then went to Ahab and punished him for the murder he had committed against Naboth. He foretold to King Ahaziah of Israel that he would die from his fall from the window of his palace in Samaria, and caused fire to fall from heaven, which consumed two captains and 100 soldiers who had been sent to bring him to the king by force. Finally, in 895 BC, this great prophet was taken to heaven in a fiery chariot. His disciple Elisha took his mantle and, at his last request, obtained that the spirit of Elijah rest upon him twice over.  The Jews, in their custom, have also tainted the story of this prophet with many fables that do not deserve to be cited.

### Core Biblical Narrative

1. **The Struggle Against** ***Baal***: Elias famously opposed King *Ahab* and Queen *Jezebel*, who had introduced the worship of the idol *Baal* to the Israelites.

2. **The Mount Carmel Contest**: He challenged the prophets of *Baal* to a test of fire on Mount Carmel. While their god remained silent, Elias's God answered his prayer by sending fire from heaven to consume his water-soaked sacrifice.

3. **Miracles**: He is credited with several wonders, including:
- Sustaining a widow's flour and oil during a drought.  
- Resurrecting the widow's son.
- Parting the Jordan River with his mantle.

4. **Chariot of Fire**:  Elias was taken up to heaven in a fiery chariot drawn by *fiery horses*. 

### Religious Significance

1. **Christianity**: 
- He appeared alongside *Moses* during the *Transfiguration of Jesus*. 
- He is considered the spiritual *founder* of the *Carmelite Order*. 
- In the New Testament, *John the Baptist* is often identified as having come in the *spirit and power* of *Elias*.

2. **Judaism**: He is expected to *return to Earth* as a *forerunner to the Messiah*.

3. **Islam**: Mentioned in the Quran as a righteous prophet sent to the people of *Baalbek* to warn them against idol worship.

## Abaris (6th c. BC)

In Greek mythology, *Abaris the Hyperborean* (Ἄβαρις Ὑπερβόρειος, Ábaris Hyperbóreios), son of *Seuthes* (Σεύθης), was a legendary sage, healer, and priest of *Apollo* known to the Ancient Greeks. He was supposed to have learned his skills in his homeland of *Hyperborea*, which he **fled during a plague** (Pest widersprüchlich siehe ladvocat, pelopones. krieg etc.). He was said to be endowed with the gift of prophecy, and along with his *Scythian dress*, simplicity, and honesty, he created great sensation in Greece, and was held in high esteem [^1].

A particularly rich trove of anecdotes is found in Iamblichus's *Vita Pythagorica* (). Here, *Abaris* is said to have *purified* Sparta and Knossos, among other cities, *from plagues*(VP 92-93), he also appears in a climactic scene *alongside* *Pythagoras* at the court of the Sicilian tyrant *Phalaris*. The two sages discuss divine matters, and urge the obstinate tyrant towards virtue (ibid. 215-221). *Iamblichus* also attributes a special expertise at extispicy to *Abaris*, the art of divination through the examination of anomalies in the entrails of animals. The *Suda* attributes a number of books to Abaris, including a volume of *Scythian Oracles* in dactylic hexameter, a prose theogony, a poem on the marriage of the river Hebrus, a work on purifications, and an account of *Apollo's* visit to the Hyperboreans. Such works, however, if they were really current in ancient times, were no more genuine than his reputed correspondence with *Phalaris* the tyrant. He is among the authors (= FGrHist 34) whose fragments were collected in *Felix Jacoby's* *Fragmente der griechischen Historiker* ().

*Plato* (*Charmides* 158C) classes him amongst the *Thracian physicians* who practice medicine upon the *soul* as well as the *body* by means of *incantations*. A temple to *Persephone* at Sparta was attributed to *Abaris* by *Pausanias* (9.10).

Ladvocat & Lohenschiold ([1760](https://doi.org/10.3931/e-rara-22312), p. 8):

>"Abaris, Gesandter der Hyperboreer, den sie etwa 564. vor Christi Geburt nach Athen geschickt, als die Pest damals den ganzen bekannten Erdkraiß heimsuchte, und Apollo den  Ausspruch gethan, daß die Athenienser für alle andere Völker Gelübde thun sollten. Er war einer von den Barbaren, deren Tugend und Weisheit auch die Athenienser bewunderten. Uebrigens haben die Alten sehr viele Fabeln von ihm, die manche unter ihnen selber nicht glaubten."

>Abaris, an envoy of the Hyperboreans, whom they sent to Athens around 564 BC, when the plague was ravaging the entire known world, where Apollo proclaimed that the Athenians should make vows on behalf of all other peoples. He was one of the barbarians whose virtue and wisdom even the Athenians admired. Incidentally, the ancients have many fables about him, some of which they themselves did not believe.

Several dates for his arrival in Greece: 

1. **568-565 BC**: The most common historical dating provided by the *Suda* and *Hippostratos*.
2. **547/6 BC**: Linked by the poet *Pindar* to the time of King *Croesus* of Lydia.
3. **736-733 BC**: A much earlier date mentioned by some unnamed ancient commentators. 

A more securely *historical* Greco-Scythian philosopher, who travelled among the *Hellenes* in the early sixth century, was *Anacharsis*.

The ***Arrow of Abaris*** or the ***Golden Arrow of Apollo***
 
In Greek legend, *Abaris the Hyperborean* was a priest of *Apollo* who received a *magical golden arrow* as a gift from *Apollo* that allowed him  to fly across the world, cure diseases, and remain invisible, it possessed several supernatural properties (s. Heraclides Ponticus (c. 390 - 310 BC): 

1. **Magical Flight**: *Abaris* was famously said to ride the arrow through the air earning him the title *Aitherobates* (*one who walks the ether*).
2. **Healing & Prophecy**: He used it to cure diseases, *banish plagues* (notably in Sparta ((), and deliver oracles.
3. **Sustenance**: Herodotus noted that *Abaris* traveled the world with the arrow without ever needing to eat food.
4. **Connection to Pythagoras**: Later traditions claim *Abaris* gave the arrow to the philosopher *Pythagoras* after recognizing him as the *Hyperborean Apollo*.

BC), s. above (c.f. Wood, [1749](https://archive.org/details/bim_eighteenth-century_an-essay-towards-a-descr_wood-john_1749_1), p. 126). According to the final form of the legend, which appeared in John Hardyng's Chronicles of 1457, Bladud's father sent his son to be educated in the liberal arts in Athens, he built *Kaerbadum* or *Caervaddon* (Bath), creating the hot springs there by the use of *magic*. He dedicated the city to the goddess *Athena* and in honour of her, lit *undying fires*, whose flames *turned to balls* of stone as they *grew low*, with new ones springing up in their stead: an embellishment of an account from the third-century writer Solinus of the use of local coal on the altars of her temple, he is said to have constructed *wings* for himself and to have tried to ***fly*** to the temple of *Apollo* in Trinovantum (London).

## Orpheus

In Greek mythology, *Orpheus* is the legendary archetypal poet and musician whose art possessed the power to charm all living things, including animals and even stones. He is most famous for his descent into the Underworld to retrieve his wife, *Eurydice*, using only the beauty of his song to persuade the gods of the dead. 

Ladvocat & Lohenschiold ([1761](https://doi.org/10.3931/e-rara-22312), p. 570):

>"Orpheus, aus Thracien, ein berühmter griechischer Dichter, noch älter als Homerus, war, nach der Fabellehre, des Apollo Sohn, und ein so vortreflicher Musicus, daß bey dem Gesang seiner Stimme und feiner Leyer die Flüsse in ihrem Lauf stille stunden, die Felsen sich zu ihm hinbewegten und die wildesten Thiere zahm wurden. Er stieg zur Höllen hinab, um seine Gemahlinn Eurydice wieder aus derselben abzuholen. ## Orphism

Ancient Orphic Religion, Orphism was a mystery cult in Ancient Greece, **supposedly founded by** the mythical poet and musician ***Orpheus***. It offered its followers a path to a better afterlife through secret initiations and a specific lifestyle. 

1. **Core Beliefs**: Orphics believed in a **dualistic nature of humanity**: a *sinful* ***physical body*** inherited from the *Titans* and a *divine spark* or ***soul*** inherited from the god *Dionysus* (also called *Zagreus*).
2. **The Goal**: The main objective was to escape the *grievous circle of reincarnation (metempsychosis)* to eventually reach the **Elysian Fields**.
3. **Practices**: To purify the soul, followers practiced *asceticism*, which included a strict vegetarian diet and regular ritual purifications.
4. **Key Artifacts**: Knowledge of their beliefs comes from *sources* like the *Derveni Papyrus*—Europe's oldest surviving manuscript—and small *gold leaf tablets* found in graves that gave instructions to the dead on how to navigate the underworld.

## Archē

In ancient Greek philosophy, the *archē* (ἀρχή) is the *first principle*, *origin*, or *primary substance* from which the *entire universe* is derived. *The search for this single, unifying essence marked the transition from mythological explanations to rational, scientific inquiry*. 

**The Mileisan Quest for Archē**

The earliest Western philosophers, known as the *Milesian School*, each proposed a different material substance as the ultimate foundation of reality: 

1. ***Thales***: **Water**
As the first philosopher, Thales argued that water is the *archē*. He likely chose it because it is essential for life, found in all things, and can transform into different states—solid, liquid, and gas.
2. ***Anaximander***: **Apeiron**
A student of *Thales, Anaximander* rejected specific elements like water because they have opposites (e.g., fire) that would cancel them out. Instead, he proposed the *Apeiron*—an indefinite, boundless, and eternal substance from which all things originate and eventually return.
3.  ***Anaximenes***: **Air**
*Anaximenes* returned to a tangible element, positing air as the *archē*. He introduced a *mechanism of change*: through **rarefaction (thinning), air becomes fire**; through **condensation (thickening), it becomes wind**, **clouds**, **water**, **earth**, and finally **stone**. 

**Later Philosophical Interpretations**

As philosophy evolved beyond Miletus, the concept of archē expanded: 

1. ***Heraclitus***: **Fire**
For Heraclitus, the primary principle was fire, symbolizing a universe in a state of constant flux and transformation, governed by the Logos (rational order).
2. ***Pythagoras***: **Numbers**
The Pythagoreans shifted from physical matter to *abstract principles*, suggesting that *numbers and mathematical ratios* were the true *archē of the cosmos*.
3. ***Empedocles***: **The Four Roots**
Empedocles proposed a *pluralistic archē*, arguing the universe is made of four eternal *roots*—*Earth, Air, Fire, and Water*—which are *mixed* and separated by the forces of Love and Strife.

## Archē vs. Yle

In ancient Greek philosophy, *Arche (or archē)* refers to the fundamental substance or *first principle* of the universe, while *Hyle (or hyle)* refers to the *matter* or *material* that makes up physical objects. 
The distinction between these two concepts is central to how early thinkers, **particularly Aristotle**, explained the structure of reality. 

Key Conceptual Differences

**Table 1**. Arche vs. Yle.
~~~
Concept 	Translation						Role in Philosophy

Arche		Beginning, Origin, Principle	The foundational source or cause from which everything originates and to which it returns.
Hyle		Matter, Wood/Timber				The underlying material that receives form to become a specific object.
~~~

### Arche: The Originating Principle

The concept of arche was the primary focus of the Pre-Socratic philosophers (the Milesians). They sought a single, eternal substance that explained the diversity of the world. 

1. ***Thales***: Identified the *arche* as **Water**, believing it was the source of all life and change.
2. ***Anaximander***: Proposed the *Apeiron* (the Boundless), an indeterminate and infinite *substance* that *precedes* all specific elements.
3. ***Heraclitus***: Viewed **Fire** as the *arche*, symbolising constant change and the Logos (universal reason). 

### Hyle: The Material Basis

Hyle (literally *timber* or *raw material*) became a technical term later, most notably in ***Aristotelian*** philosophy. 

1. **Material Cause**: *Aristotle* used *hyle* to describe one of his Four Causes. It is the *stuff* out of which something is made (e.g., bronze is the *hyle* of a statue).
2. **Potentiality**: In his *metaphysics*, *hyle* represents pure potentiality. Matter has no definite shape or identity until it is acted upon by Morphe (Form).
3. **Relation to Arche**: While the Pre-Socratics' arche was often a *specific type* of matter (like water or air), *Aristotle's* hyle is a more *abstract concept* used to explain the **metaphysical structure** of individual objects. 

Summary of the Relationship

In the earliest philosophy, the two concepts were often blurred: the *arche* was simply the ***first*** *matter*. 

As philosophy matured, Arche became the logical or divine *starting point* of the cosmos, whereas *Hyle became* the *physical substrate* that undergoes change and takes on different *forms*.

## Ancient Greek philosophy schools

The primary location associated with ancient Greek philosophers is **Athens**, which served as the intellectual heart of the Greek world. However, early thinkers—known as the *Pre-Socratics*—originated from various city-states across the Mediterranean, including **Ionia** (modern-day Turkey), southern **Italy**, and **Sicily**. 

Ancient Greek philosophy (6th - 1st c. BC) is **generally divided** into **3 major eras**: 

1. **Pre-Socratics**
2. **Socratic/Classical period**
3. **Hellenistic schools**

These schools **transitioned** from investigating the **physical cosmos** to focusing on **human ethics** and the *good life*. 

### Pre-Socratic Schools (6th – 5th c. BC) 

These early thinkers sought the ***primary substance*** (**arche**[^2]) of the *universe* and relied on **reason over mythology**. 

1. **Milesian School**: Founded by ***Thales***, focusing on material monism (e.g., water as the source of all), these were **the first to move away from myth**.
- ***Thales of Miletus***: Often called the *first philosopher*, he proposed that **water** is the fundamental substance of all things.
- ***Anaximander***: A *student of Thales* who identified the **arche** as the apeiron—an infinite, boundless, and indefinite source.
- ***Anaximenes***: Proposed that **air** is the primary substance, *forming all other things* through condensation and rarefaction.

2. **Pythagoreanism**: Founded by ***Pythagoras***; emphasized **mathematics**, the **soul**, and a communal, **ascetic lifestyle**. This school held that *numbers and mathematical ratios* are the basis of reality. They also believed in the transmigration of souls (metempsychosis). 
- Revival in Tarentum under the leader ***Archytas***, **4th c. Pythagoreanism (400-350 BC)**. 
- Revival of the lifestyle and mystical number theories **Neo-Pythagoreanism (1st c. BC – 3rd c. AD)**.

3. ***Heraclitus of Ephesus***: Known for the doctrine of **universal flux** (*Everything flows*), he argued that change is the only constant and that **fire** is the primary element. He introduced the concept of the *Logos* as the *rational order of the universe*. 

4. **Eleatic School**: (Die Eleaten) The Eleatic school of pre-Socratic philosophy was founded in the early 5th c. BC (c. 500–450 BC). Founded by ***Parmenides*** ; they argued that **reality is unchanging** and singular, argued that change and **motion are illusions**. Nach früherer Ansicht wurde die eleatische Lehre etwa 540 v. Chr. von ***Xenophanes*** in Elea begründet, *Xenophanes* wird aber nach gegenwärtigem Wissensstand nicht mehr zu den *Eleaten* gerechnet. Zuvor sah man *Parmenides* als einen Schüler von *Xenophanes* (* um 570 v. Chr.) an.
- ***Xenophanes of Colophon***: Although he was active in the mid-to-late 6th c. BC (c. 540 BC), some ancient sources and modern scholars regard him as the **school's precursor** or original founder because he laid the theological and metaphysical groundwork *Parmenides* later refined.
- **Foundation of Elea**: The city of Elea itself (now Velia, Italy) was founded by *Phocaean Greeks* around 535–540 BC. 
- ***Parmenides of Elea***: Most historians credit him with establishing the school's core metaphysical doctrines—such as the belief in an unchanging, indivisible *Being*—in the early 5th c. BC. Asserted that *what is* is **eternal and unchanging**; pluralism is impossible because nothing can come from nothing.
- ***Zeno of Elea***: Famous for his *paradoxes* (e.g., Achilles and the Tortoise) intended to prove that **motion and plurality** are logically **impossible**.


5. **The Pluralists**: founded by ***Anaxagoras*** and ***Empedocles***, tried to reconcile the unchanging *being* of Parmenides with the changing world observed by the senses.
- ***Empedocles***: Proposed the **four classical elements** (*earth, air, fire, water*) acted upon by the twin forces of Love and Strife.
- ***Anaxagoras***: Argued that **everything** contains **seeds** of all substances, which are **organized by** a **cosmic mind** called nous.
- ***Archelaus*** (5th c. BC): A student of *Anaxagoras* and reputed **teacher of** ***Socrates***.

6. **Atomism**: Foubded by ***Leucippus*** and expanded by ***Democritus***, proposing that the universe is made of **indivisible particles** called **atoms**,  they theorized that the universe is composed of tiny, indivisible particles called atoms **moving through** an empty **void**.

7. **The Sophists**: did not have a single founder, as they were a diverse group of professional traveling teachers, Professional teachers like ***Protagoras*** (*Man is the measure of all things*) and ***Gorgias*** who shifted focus toward rhetoric, politics, and the subjectivity of truth. 
 


### Classical Athenian Schools (4th c. BC)

Following *Socrates*, his students founded formal institutions that defined Western education for centuries.


1. **The Academy**: Founded by ***Plato*** (c. 387 BC) in Athens. It is often cited as the **first university** and focused on the Theory of Forms and political philosophy.
2. **The Lyceum** (**Peripatetic School**): Founded by ***Aristotle*** (335 BC). It emphasized empirical observation, logic, and biology. Aristotle’s followers were called *Peripatetics* because of his habit of *walking* while lecturing. 
3. **Cyrenaic School** (4th c. BC): Founded by ***Aristippus of Cyrene***. A hedonistic school that claimed **pleasure** was the only ultimate good.
4. **Megarian School** (4th c. BC): Founded by ***Euclid of Megara***, it focused on **logic** and the nature of **the Good**.

Key Characteristics

- **Ethical Focus**: Almost all these schools *centered on the Socratic question* of ***how to live the best life***, though they reached vastly different conclusions (e.g., **asceticism vs. hedonism**).
- **Dialectical Method**: They continued the use of the *Socratic Method*—a form of cooperative argumentative dialogue to stimulate critical thinking.
- **Foundational Legacy**: These schools provided the **philosophical base** for *later* Hellenistic movements, such as *Stoicism* (influenced by the Cynics) and *Skepticism* (influenced by the Academy and Megarians).


### Hellenistic Schools (Late 4th-1st c. BC)

In the Hellenistic period (323–31 BC), Greek philosophical schools shifted focus **from abstract metaphysics** and politics **toward practical ethics** and individual tranquility.
In the uncertain world after *Alexander the Great*, these schools focused on individual happiness (eudaimonia) and tranquility (ataraxia). These schools served as *therapies of desire*, providing students with tools to achieve *ataraxia* (undisturbedness) in an increasingly large and uncertain world. 

1. **Stoicism**: Founded by ***Zeno of Citium*** (c. 300 BC) at the Stoa Poikile. It taught **virtue as the only good** and advocated for emotional self-control.

2. **Epicureanism**: Founded by ***Epicurus*** (c. 306 BC) at **The Garden**. It advocated for simple pleasures and the avoidance of pain and fear. Also **Epicurean Atomist School** (306 BC): A formal school of atomism was founded in Athens in 306 BC by *Epicurus*. This school, often referred to as *The Garden*, **revived** and expanded upon the *earlier theories* of ***Democritus***.

3. **Cynicism**: Famously represented by ***Diogenes of Sinope***. It rejected social conventions and material wealth in favor of a **life in accordance with nature**. They were known for their **asceticism** and **provocative public behavior** designed to **challenge** artificial **societal norms**. 

4. **Skepticism**: Originating with ***Pyrrho of Elis***, it argued for the **suspension of judgment** to achieve peace of mind. This movement included **two main branches**:
- **Pyrrhonism**: Founded by ***Pyrrho***, it advocated for the suspension of judgment (epoché) on all matters, arguing that **certain knowledge is impossible**.
- **Academic Skepticism**: Developed within *Plato’s Academy*, thinkers like ***Arcesilaus*** and ***Carneades*** used *Socratic* methods to argue that **nothing** can be **known for certain**.

**Continued** Classical Schools

The famous schools of the Classical period also continued to operate in Athens during this era: 

1. **The Academy**: Originally founded by *Plato*, it underwent several *transformations*, notably shifting into a **center for Skepticism** during the *Hellenistic age*.
2. **The Lyceum (Peripatetic School)**: Founded by *Aristotle*, this school **continued** to focus on **empirical research, science, and logic**. 

Scientific and Educational Centers

Beyond philosophy, the Hellenistic world **established formal institutions** for advanced research and general education: 

1. **The Mouseion (Museum) of Alexandria**: A state-funded research institute that **housed the Great Library**, supporting scholars in *astronomy, mathematics, and medicine*.
2. **The Gymnasium**: While **originally for physical training**, it evolved into an essential educational hub where young men studied *literature, music, and rhetoric* as a prerequisite for Greek citizenship.

## Pluralist school

The term pluralist school primarily refers to a branch of Ancient Greek philosophy, but it is also used in modern contexts concerning educational policy and economics. 

Ancient Greek Philosophy (The Pluralist School)

The Pluralist School was a group of pre-Socratic philosophers who sought to reconcile the **idea of an unchanging reality** with the observable, changing world. Unlike *monists (who believed in one single substance)*, *pluralists* argued that reality is composed of *multiple, eternal elements*. 

Core Belief: 

The **universe** is **made of many** distinct **substances**, not just one.

Key Figures:

1. ***Empedocles***: Proposed that all things are made of **four roots**: 
- **earth**
- **air**
- **fire**
- **water**

moved by the forces of **Love (aggregation)** and **Strife (separation)**.

2. ***Anaxagoras***: Introduced the concept of **Nous (mind)** as an **ordering force** and believed **everything** contains portions of **everything else**.

3. ***Democritus & Leucippus***: Often *associated with this school*, they proposed that *infinite, indivisible* **atoms** moving in a *void* form all perceptible objects.

## Academy (387-89 BC)

Plato's Academy, founded in 387 BC in Athens, is widely considered the world's *first institution of higher learning* and the ultimate **ancestor of the modern university**. Located in a sacred grove originally *dedicated to* the hero ***Academus***, it served as a center for research, intellectual discussion, and scientific inquiry for nearly 900 years until its closure in 529 AD. 

Core Purpose and Teachings

Rather than a formal university with a fixed curriculum, the Academy was an informal society of intellectuals who gathered to discuss deep questions of life. 

1. **Methodology**: Teaching relied heavily on the **dialectical method** (dialogue and questioning) and lectures to help students learn *how to think* rather than what to think.
2. **Subjects**: While famous for *philosophy*, the Academy also investigated **mathematics, astronomy, biology,** and **political** science.
3. **Mathematical Focus**: Plato placed a high value on mathematics; a famous *sign at the entrance* allegedly read, ***Let no one ignorant of geometry enter here***. 

Notable Members

1. ***Plato***: The founder and primary teacher who led the school for roughly 40 years.
2. ***Aristotle***: The Academy's most famous student, who studied there for 20 years before founding his *own school, the Lyceum*.
3. ***Eudoxus of Cnidus***: A renowned *mathematician* and *astronomer* associated with the institution. 

Historical Legacy

The Academy's influence persists today, most visibly in the word *academic* itself. It established the tradition of the *scholarch* (school leader) and the practice of scholars gathering to debate the universe. Though the **original school was destroyed** during the Roman era (**83 BC**), a **revived Neoplatonist Academy** operated until Emperor **Justinian I** ordered it **closed** to *suppress* pagan teachings. 

When *Plato* died in 348/347 BC, he was succeeded as the head (scholarch) of the Academy by his nephew, ***Speusippus***. 

Historical Succession of the Academy

While ***Aristotle*** was *Plato's* most famous student, he ***did not inherit the school***. The succession was largely dictated by Athenian law: 

1. ***Speusippus*** **(347–339 BC)**: *Plato's nephew*. Under Athenian law, property typically stayed within the family line. He *shifted* the Academy's focus **more toward mathematics**.
2. ***Xenocrates*** **(339–314 BC)**: Elected by the Academy's members after ***Speusippus'*** death, bypassing *Aristotle*, who had left Athens by this time.
3. **Later Succession**: The Academy continued through various *successions* (diadoche), eventually **shifting into Skepticism** under ***Arcesilaus*** and later being **revived as Neoplatonism** until it was **closed by Emperor Justinian in 529 AD**. 

The connection between *Aristotle* and the concept of succession primarily spans his philosophical lineage, his biological theories on inheritance, and modern thematic analyses of the HBO series Succession. 

Intellectual Succession (The Peripatetic School)

1. ***Aristotle*** established a formal model of intellectual transmission within **his school, the Lyceum**. 
2. ***Theophrastus***: He was *Aristotle's* hand-picked successor as the head (scholarch) of the Peripatetic school (**father of botanics**).
3. **Legacy**: *Aristotle* willed his library and original manuscripts to *Theophrastus*. The line continued from *Theophrastus* to *Neleus of Scepsis*, though the physical manuscripts were famously hidden in a cellar for two centuries to avoid confiscation.
4. **Historical Model**: *Aristotle* historicized philosophy as a *succession of predecessors and successors,* effectively framing himself as an intellectual *son* to *Plato*. 

Plato’s **dialogues** are the **primary surviving** works of the ancient Greek philosopher, written as *dramatic conversations* typically featuring his mentor, *Socrates*. These texts are traditionally divided into *three* chronological periods, reflecting the evolution of Plato's own thought:

Key Historical Endpoints

1. **The First Destruction (86 BC)**: During the *First Mithridatic War*, the Roman general *Sulla* laid siege to Athens. He laid waste to the Academy’s sacred groves and gymnasium to use the timber for siege engines, effectively **ending the original institution** founded by Plato in 387 BC. After this, no further heads (scholarchs) of the school are recorded.
2. **The Final Closure (AD 529)**: A revived **Neoplatonic Academy** operated in Athens from roughly AD 410. In AD 529, the Byzantine Emperor **Justinian I** issued a decree **forbidding pagans from teaching**, which **officially closed the school**. This is widely cited as the *symbolic end of classical antiquity*.
3. **Exile to Persia (531/532 AD)**: Following the closure, the last scholarch, ***Damascius***, and six other philosophers (including ***Simplicius***) fled to the court of the Sassanid King ***Khosrau I*** in Persia. They eventually returned to the Byzantine Empire under a peace treaty that guaranteed their personal safety.

## Academus

Academus (or Akademos) is a legendary **Attic hero** in Greek mythology after whom the site of *Plato's Academy* was named. He is primarily known for *saving Athens from an invasion*. 

Role in Mythology

1. **Saving Athens**: *Academus* is credited with revealing to the *Dioskouroi (Castor and Polydeukes)*, the divine twins and brothers of *Helen of Troy*, where *Theseus* had hidden their sister after abducting her. This act prevented a potential war and saved the city of Athens from their wrath.
2. **Sacred Grove**: In his honor, the land he owned near Athens was revered and left untouched by the Athenians, even when the rest of the surrounding area was destroyed. This site, a park or grove on the river Cephissus, became known as the *Akademeia*. 

Link to Education

The primary reason for Academus' modern recognition is the philosophical school founded by *Plato* in the sacred grove named after the hero. *Plato's* school at the *Akademeia* is the source of the modern word *academy*, which now universally means a place of learning.

## Lyceum, The Peripatetic school (335-86 BC)

Aristotle's Lyceum was a major philosophical school and gymnasium in ancient Athens, established in 335 BC. It served as the base for Aristotle's **Peripatetic school**, so named because he famously taught while walking along its *covered walkways (peripatoi)*. 

The site was a holistic center that combined mental and physical training, housing a *gymnasium, library, and botanical garden*. 

The Lyceum (or Lykeion) in Athens has a history spanning over two millennia, from its origins as a sacred grove to its *1996 rediscovery* as an archaeological park. While best known as the **home of Aristotle's Peripatetic school**, the site served as a vital *athletic*, *military*, and *religious* hub for centuries. As an ancient gymnasium, it included a *palaestra (wrestling school)* and bathhouses where athletes trained alongside scholars.
Historical Library: *Aristotle's* personal collection of texts formed the basis for what is considered the first great research library in Europe. 


Timeline of the Lyceum

Early History (6th-4th c. BC)

1. **c. 6th-5th c.  BC**: The site is established as a sanctuary dedicated to ***Apollo Lykeios*** (Apollo the *Wolf-God*). It serves as a public gymnasium, a **military training ground** for *hoplites* and *cavalry*, and a meeting place for early philosophers like *Socrates* and *Isocrates*.
2. **335 BC**: Upon returning to Athens *after tutoring Alexander* the Great, **Aristotle founds his school** *within the Lyceum grounds*. As a non-citizen (metic), he cannot own property and instead rents the buildings.
3. **335 – 323 BC**: The **Peripatetic School** flourishes. *Aristotle* creates the *first great library* and a research museum, lecturing while walking through the peripatoi (covered walkways). 

Hellenistic & Roman Eras (322 BC - 4th c. AD) 

1. **322 BC**: *Aristotle* fled Athens to avoid anti-Macedonian sentiment and *dies in exile*; **Theophrastus takes over** as head of the school for 36 years, officially acquiring the land for the school's use.
2. **225 BC**: The school began a **significant decline** following the death of its last certain scholar, ***Lyco of Troas***.
3. **86 BC**: During the Roman siege of Athens, General **Sulla** sacks the city and **destroys the Lyceum's superstructure**, seizing *Aristotle's manuscripts to be taken to Rome*. Roman forces under Sulla sacked Athens, causing **catastrophic damage** to the Lyceum and the surrounding groves.
4. **1st-2nd c. AD**: There was a scholarly **renaissance** at the site during the Roman era. The site undergoes *Roman reconstruction*. It sees a brief philosophical *revival under* ***Marcus Aurelius*** and is upgraded with elaborate bath complexes.
5. **267 AD**: A later *renaissance* of the school was likely ended when Heruli and **Goths sacked Athens**. The *Heruli invasion* **severely damages** the site, though minor repairs continue into the late 3rd c.
6. **Late 4th c. AD**: The Lyceum is **permanently destroyed** and falls into disuse during the Byzantine period, *eventually becoming buried*. 
7. **529 AD**: However, the school (along with all other pagan philosophical institutions in Athens) was **officially closed**, formal philosophical teaching at the site **ceased permanently under Justinian’s decree**.

Succession of Notable Leaders

The Peripatetic school was managed by a **succession of scholars** until its eventual decline: 

1. ***Aristotle (335–322 BC)***
2. ***Theophrastus (322–286 BC)***
3. ***Strato of Lampsacus (288–269 BC)***
4. ***Lyco of Troas (269–225 BC)***
5. ***Andronicus of Rhodes (c. 60 BC)***: Credited with ***editing*** *Aristotle's* works ***into the form we know today***.

## Stoicism (timeline)

The history of Stoicism is traditionally divided into **three major phases**: the Early Stoa (), the Middle Stoa (), and the Late Stoa (). 

### The Early Stoa,  foundational years in Greece (c. 301-150 BC)

1. **c. 301 BC** – ***Zeno of Citium***: Founded the school in Athens, teaching at the *Stoa Poikile (Painted Porch)*.
2. **c. 262 BC** – ***Cleanthes of Assos***: Succeeded *Zeno* as the second head, focusing on the *religious and physical* aspects of the philosophy.
3. **c. 230 BC** – ***Chrysippus of Soli***: The third head and *second founder* who systematized Stoic *logic and physics*, writing over 700 books.
4. **155 BC** – The Embassy to Rome: ***Diogenes of Babylon*** (fifth head) *traveled to Rome* as part of a diplomatic mission, *introducing* the philosophy to a Roman audience for the first time. 


### The Middle Stoa, expansion and introduction to Rome (c. 150-50 BC)

1. **c. 135 BC** – ***Panaetius of Rhodes***: *Introduced Stoicism* to the *Roman elite* and *modified some original doctrines* to make them more accessible.
2. **c. 95-46 BC** – ***Cato the Younger***: A Roman statesman whose principled life and suicide in *defense of the Republic* became a symbol of Stoic martyrdom.
3. **c. 135-51 BC** – ***Posidonius***: A polymath who *incorporated scientific interests* (*astronomy and geography*) into the Stoic framework. 

### The Late (Roman) Stoa, the Imperial Roman period (1st-2nd c. AD) 

1. **c. 4 BC-65 AD** – ***Seneca the Younger***: Advisor to Emperor *Nero* and author of prolific letters and essays on practical ethics.
2. **c. 55-135 AD** – ***Epictetus***: A *former slave* turned teacher whose lectures were compiled into the Discourses and the *Enchiridion* (*Manual*).
3. **161-180 AD** – ***Marcus Aurelius***: The *Philosopher King* and Roman Emperor whose personal journal, the *Meditations*, remains one of the *most famous Stoic texts*. 

### Decline and Modern Revival

1. **3rd – 6th c. AD**: Stoicism **declined** as *Neoplatonism* and *Christianity* rose in popularity. In ***529 AD, Emperor Justinian officially closed the ancient philosophical schools in Athens***.
2. **16th c.** – **Neostoicism**: A **Renaissance movement** led by ***Justus Lipsius*** sought to *reconcile* Stoic principles with Christian theology.
3. **20th c.** – **Modern Stoicism**: A resurgence in interest led by figures like *A. A. Long* (1971) and *Pierre Hadot*, alongside the *integration* of *Stoic techniques* into *Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)*.

## Eleatic School


The **Eleatic school** and the **Megarian school** are **closely linked** by their shared belief in the Unity of reality, though they emerged at different times and with distinct focuses. 

- **Eleatics were Pre-Socratic metaphysicians** focused on the nature of Being
- **Megarians were a Minor Socratic school** that blended Eleatic monism with Socratic ethics and advanced logic. 

Key Comparisons

**Table 2**. Eleatic School	 vs. Megarian School.
~~~
Feature 			Eleatic School																		Megarian School

Founder				Parmenides of Elea (c. 5th c. BC)													Euclid of Megara (c. 4th c. BC), a pupil of Socrates
Primary Concept		The One (Being): Reality is a single, unchanging, and indivisible "Being."			The Good: Identified the Socratic "Good" with the Eleatic "One," arguing they are the same reality.
View of Change		Change, motion, and plurality are complete illusions of the senses.					Motion and growth are figments of the senses; only the "Good" truly exists.
Methodology			Known for Zeno’s Paradoxes, used to prove that motion is impossible through logic.	Developed Eristic (the art of dispute), using logical puzzles and the reductio ad absurdum.
Legacy				Influenced Plato and established the foundations of ontology.						Influenced Stoic logic and developed famous paradoxes like "The Liar."
~~~

Key Differences & Evolutions

1. **Synthesis of Ideas**: The *Megarian school* is often viewed as a *synthesis of Socratism and Eleaticism*. ***Euclid of Megara*** took the Socratic search for the *Good* and gave it an ontological foundation by equating it with the unchanging *Being* of ***Parmenides***.
2. **Focus on Logic**: While the Eleatics used logic primarily to defend their metaphysical stance on unity, the Megarians (especially *Euclid's* successors like *Eubulides* and *Diodorus Cronus*) became famous for dialectical logic and modal logic—exploring concepts of possibility and necessity.
3. **Ethical Dimension**: Unlike the purely metaphysical Eleatics, the Megarians maintained a Socratic interest in ethics. They taught that virtue is knowledge and that the *highest object of knowledge* is the *highest Good*.
4. **Paradoxes**: Both schools utilized *paradoxes*, but for different ends. *Zeno* (Eleatic) used them to deny plurality and motion. The Megarians used them as eristic tools to challenge opponents and explore the boundaries of truth and language (e.g., the *Liar paradox*).

## Megarian School

The Megarian School of philosophy flourished from the late 5th c. BC to the early 3rd c. BC. Founded by ***Euclides of Megara***, a *pupil of Socrates*, the school *integrated* Socratic ethics with the *Eleatic* doctrine of *the One*, maintaining that the Good is a single, unchanging absolute. 

Timeline of Key Figures and Developments

1. **c. 400 BC: Foundation**, ***Euclides of Megara*** founded the school after the death of *Socrates* (399 BC). He is noted for his Socratic dialogues and the belief that *the good is one, though called by many names*.
2. **Mid-4th c. BC: The Paradox-Makers**. Euclides' successor, ***Eubulides of Miletus***, became famous for his logical paradoxes, including the *Liar Paradox* (*If I say I am lying, am I telling the truth?*) and the *Sorites Paradox* (the heap).
3. **Late 4th c. BC: Dialectical Peak**, The school's focus on logic grew so intense that members like *Diodorus Cronus* and *Philo of Megara* were sometimes considered a separate *Dialectical School*. Diodorus is best known for his *Master Argument* regarding modal logic and possibility.
4. **c. 325 BC – Early 3rd c. BC: Transition to Stoicism**, ***Stilpo of Megara***, the leading figure of the school during this period, was known for his attractive personality and for preaching the self-sufficiency of virtue. He famously taught *Zeno of Citium*, the *founder* of Stoicism, who *incorporated* Megarian logic and Socratic ethics into the Stoic system.
5. **Early 3rd c. BC: Dissolution**, The school effectively ceased to exist as a distinct entity as its members and teachings were *absorbed into* the rising *Stoic and Cynic* schools.

## Atomism

The *transition from* a ***mythological*** worldview to a ***scientific*** one began with the *Greek Atomists*. They were the *first* to propose that the universe was composed of tiny, indivisible *particles* rather than being controlled by the *whims of gods*. 
Here is a timeline of the key figures and milestones in Greek Atomism.

Timeline of Key Figures

**Table 3**. Atomism.
~~~
Era 				Philosopher		Key Contribution

c. 480 – 420 BC		Leucippus		The "Father of Atomism." He first proposed that everything is made of atoms and "the void."
c. 460 – 370 BC		Democritus		Expanded the theory; argued that atoms differ in shape and size, and their arrangement creates different substances.
c. 341 – 270 BC		Epicurus		Integrated atomism into ethics; introduced the "Swerve" theory to allow for human free will.
c. 99 – 55 BC		Lucretius		A Roman poet who preserved Greek atomist ideas in his epic poem De Rerum Natura (On the Nature of Things).

Core Concepts of the Atomist School

The Greek Atomists operated on a few **fundamental laws** that laid the **groundwork** for **modern chemistry and physics**: 

1. **The Atom (Atomos)**: The word means *uncuttable*. They believed if you kept cutting an object, you would eventually reach a particle so small it could not be divided further.
2. **The Void**: Atomists argued that *space* is not nothingness but a *vacuum in which atoms move and collide*.
3. **Conservation of Matter**: They were the first to state that *nothing can be created out of nothing*, implying that the *total amount* of *matter* in the universe is *constant*.
4. **The Swerve (Clinamen)**: *Epicurus* added this idea, suggesting that atoms occasionally *deviate* from their path. This was his way of explaining how atoms *collide* and why humans have *free* will instead of being purely deterministic machines. 

The *Quiet Period* and Rediscovery

For centuries, Atomism was overshadowed by *Aristotle*, who *rejected* the idea of a vacuum and atoms, *preferring* the theory of the *four elements* (*Earth, Air, Fire, Water*). 
Atomism was **nearly lost** to history **until 1417**, when a manuscript of ***Lucretius's De Rerum Natura*** was **rediscovered** in a German monastery. This sparked the scientific curiosity of Renaissance thinkers like *Pierre Gassendi* and, eventually, *Isaac Newton* and *John Dalton*. 

The history of atomism is a fascinating journey from philosophical speculation to concrete mathematical reality. It represents humanity's shift from asking *What is the world made of?* to *How do we prove it?*

Ancient Philosophy: The Seed of an Idea

Before microscopes and particle accelerators, atomism was a logical argument used to explain why matter has consistent properties. 

1. **c. 440 BC** – ***Leucippus*** and ***Democritus***: Proposed the concept of *Atomos* (*uncuttable*). They argued that if you keep dividing matter, you eventually reach a tiny, indivisible particle.
2. **c. 300 BC** – ***Epicurus***: *Expanded* the theory, suggesting atoms *move* through a vacuum (the void) and have weight.
3. **c. 50 BC** – ***Lucretius***: Wrote *De Rerum Natura* (*On the Nature of Things*), a poem that *preserved* Greek atomic theory for the Roman world. 

The Scientific Revolution: Foundations of Chemistry

For nearly 2000 years, *Aristotle’s view* (that matter is continuous and made of *four elements*) *dominated*. Atomism resurfaced as *chemistry* became a formal science. 

1. **1803** – ***John Dalton***: Proposed the *first Atomic Theory* based on experimental evidence. He stated that elements consist of identical atoms and that compounds are *combinations* of different types of atoms.
2. **1811** – ***Amedeo Avogadro***: Distinguished between *atoms and molecules*, proposing that equal volumes of gases contain the same number of particles.
3. **1869** – ***Dmitri Mendeleev***: Created the **Periodic Table**, organizing elements by atomic weight and predicting the existence of unknown atoms. 

The *Golden Age* of Discovery: Inside the Atom 

At the turn of the 20th century, scientists realized the *indivisible* atom actually had *internal parts*.

The Quantum Era: Modern Atomism

Modern physics moved away from seeing atoms as *little balls* and toward seeing them as ***waves and energy clouds***. 

1. 1926 – ***Erwin Schrödinger***: Developed the *Electron Cloud Model*. He used *wave mechanics* to show that we can only predict the *probability* of where an electron is located.
2. 1964 – ***Murray Gell-Mann***: Proposed *Quarks*, showing that protons and neutrons are made of even smaller subatomic particles.
3. 1981 – ***Binnig & Rohrer***: Invented the *Scanning Tunneling Microscope (STM)*, allowing humans to *see* and manipulate individual atoms for the first time.

The story of Greek atomism is a fascinating journey from purely philosophical speculation to the very foundations of modern chemistry. It represents one of the first times in history that humans tried to explain the universe through physical laws rather than divine intervention. 

The Pre-Socratic Roots (c. 500 – 450 BC)

Before atoms were proposed, philosophers debated the nature of *being.*

1. Parmenides: Argued that change is an illusion and that "nothingness" cannot exist.
2. Anaxagoras: Proposed that everything is made of infinitely divisible *seeds.*
3. The Problem: If matter can be divided forever, how can anything have a solid shape? This paradox set the stage for the atomists. 

The Birth of Atomism (c. 440 – 400 BC) 

Two key figures introduced the idea of a *smallest possible unit.*

1. Leucippus (c. 440 BCE): The shadowy founder of the school. He proposed that the universe consists of two things: atoms (the full) and the void (the empty).
2. Democritus (c. 460 – 370 BCE): The "Laughing Philosopher" and the most famous atomist.
He coined the term atomos (indivisible).
He argued atoms were eternal, unchangeable, and differed only in shape and size.
Famous Quote: *By convention sweet is sweet, by convention bitter is bitter... but in reality there are atoms and the void.*

The Rival Theory: *The Four Element* (c. 450 – 322 BC)
Atomism faced a massive *PR problem* from the two most influential thinkers of the era.

1. ***Empedocles***: Proposed the *Four Roots* (Earth, Air, Fire, Water).
2. ***Aristotle***: Rejected atomism entirely. He argued that a *void* (vacuum) was *physically impossible*. Because *Aristotle's influence* was so dominant, atomism was sidelined for nearly 2000 years. 

The Ethical Turn: Epicurus (c. 341 – 270 BC) 

Epicurus adopted atomism not just for science, but for peace of mind. 

1. The *Swerve* (parameclisis): He added the idea that atoms occasionally *swerve* randomly. This was his way of explaining free will in a universe of colliding particles.
2. Goal: To remove the fear of gods and death. If the soul is just a collection of atoms that disperse at death, there is nothing to fear in the afterlife. 

The Roman Preservation: Lucretius (c. 99 – 55 BC) 

Atomism *might* have been *lost* if not for the Roman poet *Lucretius*.
He wrote the epic poem *De Rerum Natura* (*On the Nature of Things*).
He used *poetic metaphors* to explain complex physics—***like dust motes dancing in a sunbeam***—to *prove* that invisible particles are always in motion.

The *Re-Discovery"*

The Greek idea lay dormant through the Middle Ages until a manuscript of ***Lucretius*** was rediscovered in 1417. This sparked the scientific imagination of the Renaissance, eventually leading to *John Dalton* in 1803. 

It is fascinating to look back at Greek atomism because Democritus and his teacher Leucippus managed to *intuit* the *fundamental nature of matter* over 2000 years before the invention of the microscope.

The Core Pillars of Democritus’s Theory

Democritus didn't have a lab; he used pure logic. His theory rested on a few radical (for the time) ideas: 

1. **The Atomos**: Matter cannot be divided infinitely. Eventually, you hit a *cut-proof* particle (means *uncuttable*).
2. **The Void**: Atoms require empty space to move through. This contradicted the popular belief that *nothingness* couldn't exist.
3.  **Infinite Variety**: Atoms come in different shapes and sizes. Sour things are made of jagged, needle-like atoms; sweet things are made of smooth, round atoms.
4. **Conservation of Matter**: Atoms are *eternal and indestructible*. They simply rearrange themselves into new forms.

## Philolaus of Croton vs. Democritus of Abdera

*Philolaus of Croton* and *Democritus of Abdera* were both prominent *pre-Socratic* Greek philosophers and contemporaries of *Socrates*, living during the **5th c. BC**. While they shared a focus on explaining the natural world and the cosmos, they represented two distinct and often contrasting schools of thought: **Pythagoreanism** and **Atomism**. 

### Philolaus of Croton (c. 470–385 BC) 

Philolaus was a leading figure in the **Pythagorean tradition** and is credited with writing the **first systematic book** on Pythagorean doctrine. 

1. **First Principles**: He posited that the universe is composed of *limiters* (*structured or defining things like shapes*) and *unlimiteds* (*continua like space, time, or fire*), which are *joined by harmony* (*harmonia*).
2. **Astronomy**: He is famous for the *first non-geocentric model* of the universe, suggesting that the Earth, Sun, Moon, and planets revolve around a *Central Fire* rather than the Earth being at the center.
3. **Significance of Number**: Consistent with Pythagorean thought, he used *numbers* as a primary explanatory concept for the cosmos and its phenomena. 

### Democritus of Abdera (c. 460–370 BC) 


*Democritus* was the *most influential* early advocate of *Atomism*, a materialist philosophy he developed alongside his teacher *Leucippus*. 

1. **Atomic Theory**: He proposed that the world is made of indivisible, eternal particles called atoms moving in a void (empty space).
2. **The Laughing Philosopher**: He was known in antiquity for his emphasis on *cheerfulness* (euthymia) as the **goal of life**, which he believed resulted from a **balanced and moderate soul**.
3. **Scientific Scope**: His work was encyclopedic, covering ethics, mathematics, biology, and astronomy, and his materialist view directly **influenced later** thinkers like ***Epicurus***. 

**Nothing exists except atoms and empty space; everything else is opinion**.
Democritus's *Fragment B125* from the *Diels-Kranz numbering* is a famous quote summarizing his atomist philosophy: **By convention (nomos) color, by convention sweet, by convention bitter, but in reality (eteê) atoms and a void**. This fragment highlights the distinction between sense perception and the true nature of reality. 

Original Greek Text

The original Greek text for the fragment is:
νόμῳ χροιή, νόμῳ γλυκύ, νόμῳ πικρόω, ἑτεῇ δ' ἄτομα καὶ κενόν 

Source

The fragment is preserved as a quotation in other ancient works, notably by the philosopher ***Sextus Empiricus***, and is cataloged as *B125* in the standard collection of Presocratic fragments by *Diels and Kranz*. 

Meaning and Context

1. **Subjectivity of Senses**: *Democritus* argues that **qualities** like color, sweetness, and bitterness are not inherent properties of objects themselves but are subjective experiences or *conventions* **arising from human sense perception**.
2. **Objective Reality**: The ***underlying, objective reality***, according to his theory, consists only of *indivisible particles (atoms)* moving in *empty space (the void)*. The different *properties we perceive* through our senses ***result from*** the different *shapes, sizes, and arrangements* of these ***atoms***.
3. **Epistemological Debate**: This fragment is part of a larger discussion about knowledge. In other related fragments (e.g., B11/TD22), *Democritus* distinguishes between **dark knowing** (*sense perception*) and **genuine knowing** (*reasoning about atoms and the void*), suggesting that **true understanding** requires going **beyond the senses**. Another passage describes a *Complaint of the Senses* where the senses challenge the intellect for using them as a basis only to then dismiss their input.
