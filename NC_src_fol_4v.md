## 2. Timeo: Timaeus of Locri (c. 420–380 BC)

Timaeus of Locri is a character appearing in Plato's dialogues, specifically the eponymous *Timaeus* and its sequel *Critias*. He is presented as a *wealthy aristocrat, philosopher*, and statesman from the Greek colony of Lokroi Epizephyrioi (modern-day Locri, Italy). 

Historicity and Identity

1. **Literary vs. Historical**: While ancient writers like *Cicero* and *Iamblichus* treated him as a real person, most modern scholars consider him a *literary invention by Plato*.
2. **Pythagorean Connection**: In the dialogues, *Timaeus* is depicted as a ***Pythagorean philosopher***. Some theories suggest his character was **based on** the actual Pythagorean philosopher ***Philolaus***.
3. **Pseudo-Timaeus**: A work titled ***On the Nature of the World and the Soul*** is attributed to him, but it is widely considered a later *paraphrase of Plato’s Timaeus* written between 100 BC and 100 AD. A simplified summary of *Plato's Timaeus* by an *anonymous* author. The work is *first explicitly mentioned* in the 2nd c. AD by *Nicomachus* and the commentator *Calvisius Taurus*.

The Timaeus (Greek: Timaios) is one of Plato's most influential dialogues, written around 360 BC, about cosmology, physical science and the nature of the universe. 

### Role in Plato’s Dialogues

1. **The Demiurge**: In the Timaeus, he delivers a long monologue explaining the creation of the universe by a divine craftsman known as the Demiurge. *Plato* introduces a *divine craftsman* who created the world not out of nothing, but by bringing order to pre-existing chaos, using eternal forms as a model.
2. **Natural Philosophy**: His discourse covers diverse topics including astronomy, biology, and the mathematical structure of the physical world. The universe is described as a living creature endowed with a soul and intelligence.
3. **World Soul**: He introduces the concept of the World Soul, an animating principle that provides order and life to the cosmos. 
4. **Platonic Solids**: The dialogue links the *four classical elements* (earth, air, fire, water) to specific geometric shapes: the cube, octahedron, pyramid (tetrahedron), and icosahedron.
5. **Atlantis**: The Timaeus (along with the *Critias*) provides the *earliest known account* of the legendary lost island of Atlantis.
6. **Nature of Time**: Plato famously defines time as a *moving image of eternity*, created alongside the heavens to reflect the eternal nature of the forms. 

### Historical Significance

For *centuries, especially during the Middle Ages*, the *Timaeus* was the ***only work of Plato available in Latin translation***, making it the ***primary source of Platonic philosophy*** for the Western world. It famously appears in *Raphael’s* painting The School of Athens, where *Plato* is depicted holding a copy of the *Timaeus* while pointing toward the heavens.

## 4. Plotinus: Plotinus (c. 204–270 AD)

Plotinus (c. 204/5–270 AD) was a major Hellenistic philosopher and is widely regarded as the ***founder of Neoplatonism***. He lived and taught in Rome during the 3rd c., where he developed a *complex metaphysical system* that sought to ***explain the entire universe through a single, supreme source***. 

### Life and Work

1. **Background**: Born in Roman Egypt, Plotinus studied for 11 years under the elusive teacher *Ammonius Saccas* in Alexandria before moving to Rome at age 40.
2. ***The Enneads***: His lectures and notes were later edited and arranged by his student, ***Porphyry of Tyre***, into *54 treatises* grouped into 6 *nines*, known as the *Enneads*.
3. **Philosophical Identity**: *Plotinus* ***viewed himself*** not as an innovator, but as a faithful ***interpreter of Plato***, though his work integrated significant elements of *Aristotelian* and *Stoic* thought. 

### Metaphysical System (!)

Plotinus proposed three fundamental *hypostases* or levels of reality: 

1. **The One (The Good)**: The absolutely simple, *transcendent*, and ineffable source of all existence. It is *beyond being* and cannot be fully described by language.
2. **Intellect (Nous)**: The *first emanation* from the One, containing the *Platonic Forms*. It is characterized by self-contemplation and is the *realm of true being*.
3. **Soul (Psyche)**: The *link* between the *intelligible realm* and the *material world*. It ***creates the physical universe*** as a beautiful but imperfect *image of the divine*. 

### Concepts

1. **Emanation**: *Reality flows out from the One* in a constant process of declining perfection, *similar to light* radiating from the sun.
2. **Henosis**: The ultimate *goal* of human life—a mystical *union* with the *One*, achieved through contemplation and the flight of the alone to the alone.
3. **Nature of Evil**: *Plotinus* identified evil with *matter*, viewing it as a lack or privation of goodness rather than a separate, active force. 

The ***works*** of Plotinus (c. 204–270 AD), the founder of Neoplatonism, are ***collectively known*** as ***The Enneads***. 

### The Structure of the Enneads

Plotinus did not write formal books but rather ***a collection of notes*** and essays *based on his lectures* and debates. His student ***Porphyry*** *edited and arranged* these 54 treatises into ***six sets of nine*** (hence the name Enneads, from the Greek *ennea for nine*). 

Porphyry arranged them thematically, moving from the most *accessible* human topics to the most complex metaphysical principles: 

1. **Ennead I**: Ethics and anthropology (e.g., happiness, beauty, virtue).
2. **Enneads II & III**: Physics and cosmology (the sensible world, matter, and fate).
3. **Ennead IV**: The Soul (its nature, descent into the body, and immortality).
4. **Ennead V**: The Intellect (Nous) and the *Three Primal Hypostases*.
5. **Ennead VI**: The One (the supreme source), Being, and the morality of *The One*. 

### Treatises and Themes

While all are part of the Enneads, some treatises are ***particularly famous*** in their own right:

1. ***On Beauty (I.6)***: One of his most celebrated works, exploring *how earthly beauty reflects divine form*.
2. ***Against the Gnostics (II.9)***: A defense of the *goodness of the world* against *Gnostic views of an evil creator*.
3. ***On the Three Principal Hypostases (V.1)***: Outlines his core trinity: ***The One, the Intellect, and the Soul***.
4. ***On the Good, or the One (VI.9)***: Describes the absolute, simple *source of all existence*. 

### Modern Editions and Translations


- *Complete Works*: Kenneth Sylvan ***Guthrie's*** translation presents the works in their original chronological order, which some scholars prefer over *Porphyry's* thematic arrangement.
- *Standard Academic*: The Loeb Classical Library (translated by A.H. ***Armstrong***) is the scholarly gold standard, including both Greek and English.
- *Accessible Readers*: *The Essential Plotinus* (Hackett Classics) is a common choice for students.

### Historical Influence

*Plotinus's* thought ***profoundly shaped Western Philosophy*** and the theological development of Christianity, Islam, and Judaism. Figures such as ***St. Augustine*** were ***heavily influenced*** by his *Neoplatonic framework*, which provided the intellectual language for many monotheistic concepts.

### Gnosticism

(from the Greek word gnosis, meaning *knowledge*) refers to a diverse collection of religious and philosophical movements that flourished in the Roman Empire during the 2nd-3rd c. AD. While not a single, unified religion, these groups shared several core beliefs that often set them *at odds with early orthodox Christianity*. 

#### Core Beliefs and Characteristics

The Gnostic worldview is defined by a radical dualism between the spiritual and material realms: 

1. **Dualism**: They believed the spiritual world is inherently good and pure, while the material world (including the human body) is flawed or even evil.
2. **The Demiurge**: Gnostics often distinguished between a supreme, unknowable ***True God*** and an inferior, *sometimes malevolent creator deity* known as the ***Demiurge***(or ***Yaldabaoth***). This *Demiurge* was frequently identified with the *God of the Old Testament*.
3. **Divine Spark**: Humans were believed to contain a divine spark—a fragment of the spiritual realm—trapped within the material body.
4. **Salvation through Gnosis**: Liberation from the material world was achieved not through faith or good works, but through gnosis—a secret, experiential knowledge of one's divine origin and the nature of the universe. 

#### Major Figures and Sects

Gnosticism was composed of many competing schools of thought, each with its own elaborate mythology: 

1. **Valentinians**: Founded by *Valentinus*, this was one of the most influential and sophisticated groups. They viewed the *Demiurge as ignorant* rather than evil and developed complex theories of *Aeons* (divine emanations).
2. **Sethians**: This group placed great emphasis on ***Seth***, the third son of Adam, viewing his descendants as the spiritual race destined for gnosis.
3. **Marcionites**: While some scholars debate their classification as Gnostics, *Marcion of Sinope* famously *rejected the Old Testament* entirely, believing its God was a separate, inferior deity from the Father of Jesus.
4. **Manichaeism**: A later, highly organized world religion founded by *Mani* that incorporated Gnostic dualism with elements of *Zoroastrianism and Buddhism*. 

#### Modern Discovery

For centuries, Gnosticism was known primarily through the writings of its critics, such as *Irenaeus of Lyons*, who denounced it as heresy. This changed significantly in 1945 with the discovery of the Nag Hammadi library in Egypt. This collection of 52 texts, including the *Gospel of Thomas* and the *Apocryphon of John*, provided the first direct look at Gnostic teachings in their own words. 

#### Survival and Influence

While most ancient Gnostic sects were eventually suppressed by the emerging Catholic Church, one group has survived to the present day: the ***Mandaeans of Iraq and Iran***, who revere *John the Baptist* and still practice ancient Gnostic-like rituals. In the 20th c., Gnostic ideas saw a revival through the works of psychologist ***Carl Jung*** and have influenced various modern esoteric and *New Age* movements.
