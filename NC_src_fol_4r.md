## 3. Cicero: Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BC)

A Roman statesman, lawyer, and philosopher widely regarded as one of *Rome's greatest orators*. 

### Marcus Tullius Cicero (The Roman)

1. **Role & Achievements**: He served as consul in 63 BC and was credited with suppressing the Catilinarian conspiracy, for which he was named pater patriae (*father of the country*).
2. **Influence**: *Cicero* shaped Latin prose and ***introduced Greek philosophy*** to Rome by *creating a Latin philosophical vocabulary*. His works—including over 900 letters and 58 surviving speeches—remain ***primary sources for Roman history***.

### Notable Works:

1. **Orations**: 

- ***In Catilinam*** (against Catiline)
- ***Philippics*** (against Mark Antony)

2. **Philosophy**: 
- ***De re publica*** (On the Republic) 
- ***De officiis*** (On Duties)
- ***De Natura Deorum*** (On the Nature of the Gods)
- ***De legibus*** (On the Laws)


He was executed in 43 BC on the orders of *Mark Antony* during the proscriptions of the *Second Triumvirate*. 


### De legibus (On the Laws) 

is a *philosophical dialogue* by the Roman statesman and philosopher Marcus Tullius Cicero. Written around 52 BC, it serves as a ***companion and practical application to*** his earlier work, ***De re publica***. 

#### Key Aspects of the Work

1. **The Concept of Natural Law**: Cicero argues that law is not merely a human invention but is ***rooted in nature and divine reason***. True law is ***right reason in agreement with nature***.
2. **Structure**: The work is written as a dialogue between *Cicero* himself, his brother *Quintus*, and their friend *Atticus*. It originally consisted of *at least five books*, though ***only three have survived*** in a complete or near-complete state.

#### Books and Content:

1. **Book I**: Focuses on the philosophical foundations of law, arguing that justice is based on nature rather than utility or social convention.
2. **Book II**: Discusses religious laws and the importance of piety in maintaining a stable state.
3. **Book III**: Outlines the duties and powers of magistrates (government officials), describing a balanced constitution similar to that of the Roman Republic.
4. **Platonic Influence**: Cicero modelled the work after *Plato's* Laws, intending to provide the specific legal framework for the ideal state he described in *De re publica*. 

### De Natura Deorum (On the Nature of the Gods), 

written by *Marcus Tullius Cicero* in 45 BC, is a ***foundational philosophical dialogue*** exploring *ancient theological* views. The work is divided into *three books*, each presenting the perspective of a major Hellenistic school of thought through a specific speaker. 

### Structure and Perspectives

The dialogue takes place *between three friends*, with *Cicero* himself *as a silent observer*: 

#### Book I: Epicureanism (*Velleius*)

1. **Argument**: *Velleius* argues that gods exist but are indifferent to human affairs. They are composed of *atoms* and live in a state of perfect, undisturbed happiness, free from the burden of governing the universe.
2. **Critique**: *Cotta* (the Academic Skeptic) refutes this, arguing that such indifferent gods are essentially useless to humanity and that the *Epicurean physical model is flawed*.

#### Book II: Stoicism (*Balbus*)

1. **Argument**: *Balbus* presents the *Stoic* view that the universe itself is a rational, divine organism governed by providence. He points to the order of the cosmos—such as the regular motion of the sun and stars—as *proof of divine design*.

#### Book III: Academic Skepticism (*Cotta*)

1. **Argument**: *Cotta*, representing the Academic *Skeptics*, subjects the Stoic arguments to rigorous critical analysis. He argues that human reason is often used for evil, casting *doubt* on the idea of a purely *benevolent divine* providence. 

### Key Themes

1. **The Consensus Gentium**: *Cicero* highlights the *universal agreement of all nations* as a primary argument for the existence of gods.
2. **Providence vs. Indifference**: The *central tension* lies between the *Stoic belief* in a god that *cares* for humanity and the *Epicurean belief* in *detached*, idle deities.
3. **Probabilism**: As an Academic Skeptic, *Cicero concludes* that while absolute truth may be unattainable, some arguments (specifically the Stoic ones) seem more *probable* than others. 

### Historical Significance

1. **Invaluable Source**: Because many original texts from the Epicurean and Stoic schools are lost, ***De Natura Deorum** remains *a primary source* of information for *ancient theological* debates.
2. **Influence**: The work's skeptical method and dialogue format heavily influenced later thinkers, most notably ***David Hume*** in his *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion*

## 5. Horatium: Quintus Horatius Flaccus (65–8 BC)

known simply as Horace (65–8 BC), was ***the leading Roman lyric poet*** during the age of *Augustus*. He is celebrated for his technical mastery, wit, and philosophical depth, particularly his famous exhortation ***carpe diem*** (*seize the day*). 

###Life and Background

1. **Early Life**: Born in Venusia (southern Italy) as the son of a freedman. Despite his father’s humble origins as a former slave, Horace received a high-quality education in Rome and Athens.
2. **Military Service**: During the Roman civil wars, he fought as a military tribune for the republican cause under *Brutus* but fled the battlefield at Philippi.
3. **Rise to Fame**: After being pardoned, he returned to Rome and was introduced to *Maecenas*, the influential advisor to Emperor *Augustus*. *Maecenas* became his patron, providing him with a *Sabine farm* that allowed him to focus entirely on his craft. 

### Major Works

*Horace’s* surviving works comprise a diverse range of poetic genres: 

1. **Odes (Carmina)**: Four books of lyric poems known for their intricate Greek meters and themes of love, wine, and morality.
2. **Satires (Sermones)**: Hexatmeter poems that humorously critique Roman life and advocate for moderation.
3. **Epistles**: Verse letters exploring philosophical and ethical questions.
4. **Ars Poetica**: A foundational work of literary criticism that offers advice on how to write poetry.
5. **Epodes**: Earlier iambic poems that are often more aggressive or experimental. 

### Horace and Astrology

The poet Horace ***often referenced astrology*** in his work, though his stance was frequently skeptical or literary. 

1. **Famous Quote**: In his famous ***Ode 1.11*** (which gave us the phrase ***carpe diem***), he advises his friend *Leuconoe* to *ignore all that astrology from Babylon* and instead focus on the present.
2. **Horoscope References**: In ***Ode 2.17***, he discusses his own horoscope, suggesting he might have been born under Libra, Scorpio, or Capricorn, using these signs to symbolize his shared destiny with his patron *Maecenas*.
3. **Jupiter vs. Saturn**: He famously claimed that the bright planet Jupiter counteracted the baleful Saturn to save *Maecenas* from a fever. 

### Legacy

Horace remains one of the most cited authors of antiquity. His poetry influenced countless writers, from the Renaissance to modern poets like *Robert Frost* and *W. H. Auden*.
