## Moses (c. 1526–1406 BC / c. 1391–1271 BC)

Estimating the dates of *Moses* is a complex task because he is **primarily a figure of the Bible** and **Abrahamic tradition**, with no direct archaeological evidence confirming his historical existence. Consequently, **several different timelines exist** based on religious tradition or scholarly interpretation. 

### Traditional and Religious Dates

1. **Rabbinic Judaism**: Calculates *Moses'* life as **1391–1271 BC**.

- *Specific Calendar Dates*: Jewish tradition often cites his birth and death on the **7th of Adar**.

3. **Early Church Figures**:
- ***St. Jerome***: Suggested a birth year of *1592 BC* (**c. 1592–1472 BC**).
- ***James Ussher***: Suggested a birth year of *1571 BC* (**c. 1571–1451**). 

### Scholarly and Archaeological Models

Most **debates center on the date of the Exodus**, which determines Moses' lifespan based on the biblical account that he was 80 at the time and died at 120. 


1. ***Early Date (15th c. BC)*** (**c. 1446 BC**):
- Based on a literal reading of ***1 Kings 6:1***, which places the ***Exodus 480 years before*** the construction of ***Solomon's Temple***.
- *Estimated lifespan*: **c. 1526–1406 BC**.
- *Associated Pharaohs*: Often linked to ***Thutmose III*** or ***Amenhotep II***.

2. ***Late Date (13th c. BC)*** (**c. 1290–1250 BC**):
- Supported by the ***mention of the city Rameses*** in the Bible **and archaeological evidence*** of city destructions in Canaan.
- *Estimated lifespan*: Late 14th-13th c. BC (**1391–1271 BC**).
- *Associated Pharaoh*: Generally identified as ***Ramesses II*** (The Great).

3. **Modern Historical View**: Many contemporary scholars view *Moses* as a ***legendary figure*** rather than a historical one, suggesting the ***stories were composed*** much later, around the **6th c. BC** during the **Babylonian exile**.

## Solomon (991-932 BC)

King Solomon was the **third king of the United Kingdom of Israel**, his timeline spans the ***Golden Age*** of a ***united Israel***, traditionally dated from 991 BC to 931 BC. 
Known as the **son of King David** and Bathsheba, he is celebrated across Jewish, Christian, and Islamic traditions for his legendary ***wisdom***, immense ***wealth***, and the construction of the **First Temple** in Jerusalem. 

#### Historical Accounts

1. **Extrabiblical Evidence**: There is *currently* ***no direct reference to King Solomon*** in any *surviving contemporaneous* (same-period) archaeological inscriptions or records from the ***10th c. BC***. 
2. ***Book of Kings***: The primary *historical record of Solomon's reign* was likely written or compiled during the Babylonian exile (***c. 560 BC***), roughly 400 years after his death.

#### Chronological Life of Solomon

1. **c. 991 BC**: Birth. ***Born*** in Jerusalem to *King David* and *Bathsheba*.
2. **c. 971–970 BC**: Coronation. Solomon is anointed ***king*** while *David* is still alive to secure the throne against his brother *Adonijah*.
3. **c. 967–966 BC**: ***Temple Construction*** Begins. Work begins in the fourth year of his reign, *480 years after* the *Exodus*.
4. **c. 961–959 BC**: ***Temple Completion***. The First Temple (*Solomon’s Temple*) is finished after seven years of labor.
5. **c. 948–946 BC**: ***Palace*** Completion. Solomon finishes building his own royal palace, which took 13 years to complete.
6. **c. 945 BC**: ***Queen of Sheba Visit***. The legendary visit occurs at the height of *Solomon's* wealth and wisdom.
7. **c. 939–931 BC**: Spiritual ***Decline***. In his later years, *Solomon’s* foreign ***wives*** influence him toward idolatry, leading to divine judgment.
8. **c. 931 BC**: ***Death and Division***. *Solomon* dies of natural causes at approximately age 60. His son *Rehoboam* succeeds him, but the kingdom immediately *splits into Israel* (North) and *Judah* (South). 

#### Key Aspects of His Reign

1. **Divine Wisdom**: According to the Hebrew Bible, ***God offered*** *Solomon anything* he desired; he chose a *discerning heart* to govern his people. His most famous demonstration of this was the Judgment of Solomon, where he resolved a maternity dispute between two women by proposing to cut the baby in half, identifying the true mother by her selfless plea to save the child.
2. **The First Temple**: *Solomon's* crowning architectural achievement was ***building*** the first Holy Temple to *house* the ***Ark of the Covenant***. This seven-year project utilized thousands of workers and materials like *cedar from Lebanon*.
3. **Wealth and Diplomacy**: His reign is often called the ***Golden Age*** of Israel due to vast prosperity. He established extensive trade routes and political *alliances*, most notably with *Hiram of Tyre* and the Queen of Sheba, who visited him to test his wisdom with riddles.
4. **Literary Legacy**: Solomon is traditionally credited as the author of ***several biblical books***, including the ***Book of Proverbs***, ***Ecclesiastes***, and the ***Song of Solomon***.
5. **Downfall and Division**: In his later years, *Solomon's* harem of 700 wives and 300 concubines led to his *spiritual decline*. Many of these ***foreign wives*** introduced the worship of idols, which turned *Solomon's heart away from God*. Following his death, his ***son Rehoboam's*** policies caused the kingdom to ***split*** into the Northern Kingdom of ***Israel*** and the Southern Kingdom of ***Judah***. 

#### Cultural Legacy

1. **Hiram I Alliance**: His reign was marked by a strong maritime and trade alliance with the ***Phoenician King Hiram I of Tyre***, which *fueled Israel's massive wealth*.

2. **In Islam**, *Solomon* is known as ***Prophet Sulayman***. The *Quran* portrays him as a righteous prophet with **miraculous powers**, including the ability to **communicate with animals** and **control the wind** and jinn (spirits) to assist in his building projects. 

#### Literary Legacy

1. **Biblical (Canonical) Books**: *Solomon* is traditionally credited with writing ***Solomon’s wisdom books***:

- ***Proverbs***
- ***Ecclesiastes***
- ***Song of Songs (Song of Solomon)***

2. **Beyond the primary three**, other texts are often associated with Solomon: 

- ***Lost Texts***: The Bible ***mentions*** the ***Book of the Acts of Solomon*** (1 Kings 11:41), a *historical record* of his reign that is ***now lost*** to history.
- ***Psalms***: He is specifically credited with two hymns in the Book of Psalms: Psalm 72 and Psalm 127.
- ***Wisdom of Solomon***: Included in the Catholic and Orthodox canons (Deuterocanonical, placed between the Old and New Testaments), *considered apocryphal* by *Protestants and Jews*. Scholars generally believe it was written in Greek much later, around the **1st-2nd c. BC**.


### Sources

There are **no surviving original manuscripts** written by King Solomon himself. The *autographs* (original documents) from his era (c. 970–931 BC) have long since *vanished* from the earth. 
While no primary physical documents from Solomon's lifetime exist, several categories of ***later manuscripts*** are attributed to him or contain his narrative: 

#### Biblical Manuscripts (Attributed to Solomon) 

Traditional Jewish and Christian belief attributes ***several books of the Bible*** to *Solomon*, but the oldest surviving physical copies date centuries after his death. 

- **Oldest Fragments**: ***The Dead Sea Scrolls*** (**c. 3rd c. BC - 1st c. AD**) contain the ***earliest known fragments*** of books attributed to *Solomon* (***Proverbs, Ecclesiastes***, and ***Song of Songs***).

#### Medieval Manuscripts

Earliest medieval manuscripts associated with King *Solomon* vary by their nature,  **portraying him in two primary ways**: 

- as (1) the divinely wise biblical monarch, as a *moral exemplar* for kings like *Charlemagne* or Saint *Louis IX* in illuminated Bibles (*biblical codices*) and royal chronicles
- as (2) a master of the occult in forbidden *Solomonic* grimoires in *pseudepigraphical* magical treatises. 

##### ad (1)  Early Biblical Codices & Wisdom Manuscripts

Medieval Bibles and psalters frequently used *Solomon* to represent the ideal of "royal magnificence" and judicial wisdom. 
While not purely *medieval* in the later sense, these provide the foundational text for medieval *Solomonic* literature:

1. ***Codex Vaticanus*** & ***Codex Sinaiticus*** (**4th c.**): These are the ***earliest complete*** Greek manuscripts *containing* 

- ***Proverbs***
- ***Ecclesiastes***
- ***Wisdom of Solomon***

2. ***Aleppo Codex*** (**c. 920**)[^1]: One of the oldest authoritative Hebrew manuscripts of the ***Tanakh***, including the books attributed to *Solomon*.  Originally contained the entire Hebrew Bible, but large parts of its pages were lost during anti-Jewish riots in Aleppo in 1947. The end of the Codex is currently missing, which includes the majority of the Song of Solomon.

3. ***Leningrad Codex*** (**1008 AD**): Contains the full text of the ***Song of Solomon*** as part of the Ketuvim (Writings) section.


##### ad (2) Pseudepigrapha & Solomonic Grimoires (Magic & Occult)

During the late Middle Ages and Renaissance, a body of **pseudepigraphical (falsely attributed)** texts emerged, claiming *Solomon* had the power to command demons. 
These are later texts attributed to *Solomon*, often involving *magic* or *demonology*, which scholars agree were ***not written by him***. 
Because these books were often *condemned by the Church* and included on lists of prohibited books, very few original medieval copies survive today; most extant manuscripts date from the **16th-18th c**. 

1. ***The Testament of Solomon***: A Greek manuscript likely written between the ***1st-5th c. AD*** (over 1000 years after Solomon). It describes how the archangel Michael gave Solomon a magical ring (the Seal of Solomon) to enslave and command demons to build the Temple of Jerusalem.

- ***Earliest Evidence***:  References to *Solomon's* power over demons appear as early as the 1st c. AD in the works of ***Flavius Josephus***[^2].
- ***Cairo Geniza***: A Greek version was discovered among manuscripts in the Cairo Geniza, with some scholars believing this copy could date as early as the **4th c. AD**.
- ***Vienna Papyrus Fragments (P. Rain. Cent. 39)***: These are the earliest physical remains of the text (6th-7th c.). They preserve portions of *Chapter 18*, which details the 36 decans (demonic spirits associated with the zodiac) and the divine names used to dispel them.

2. ***Solomon and Saturn*** (**9th-10th c.**, Early Medieval England): This Old English dialogue of ***anonymous authorship*** as poems and prose texts, present *Solomon* as a figure of wisdom engaging in a riddle contest, it presents **Solomon as a philosopher** engaged in a battle of wits.

- ***CCCC 422 (Red Book of Darley)***: Contains the *most complete versions*, with the first two quires dating to the **mid-10th c**.
- ***CCCC 41***: Contains a fragment of the first poem written in the margins of a **10th-c.** translation of ***Bede's Ecclesiastical History***.
- ***Cotton Vitellius A.xv***: A later prose version is found in this **12th-c.** manuscript. 

3. ***The Notory Art (Ars Notoria) (13th c.)***: The oldest surviving manuscript of this *notory art* is dated to **1225** and is held at the Beinecke Rare Book and Manuscript Library. It is a medieval treatise focusing on enhancing mental faculties through divine prayers revealed to *Solomon*, focused on **prayers** and figures meant **to grant** the practitioner **perfect memory** and **sudden wisdom**.

4. ***Ethiopic Royal Manuscripts (13th–16th c.)***: During the Early Solomonic Period (1270–1527) in Ethiopia, numerous manuscripts were **produced to support** the Solomonic dynasty's claim of **descent** from *King Solomon* and the *Queen of Sheba*.

5. **Warning Tales**: Some manuscripts, such as those of ***Gower's Confessio Amantis*** (**14th c.**), used *Solomon's* later-life idolatry and foreign wives as a cautionary tale *about the dangers of temptation*. 

6. ***The Key of Solomon (Clavicula Salomonis) (14th–15th c.)***: This grimoire contains rituals for summoning spirits and creating magical *Seals of Solomon*.

 - The earliest Latin versions of this famous grimoire date back to the late **13th-14th c**. 
- A notable early Greek version, the ***Magical Treatise of Solomon***, is found in the British Library (***Harley MS 5596***) and dates to the **15th c**. 
- Later popularized (A famous Renaissance-era *grimoire*), most surviving manuscripts date from the *16th-18th c.*

7. ***The Lesser Key of Solomon (Lemegeton)***: ***A 17th c***. compilation of *magical* texts, including the *Ars Goetia*, which claims to detail 72 demons *Solomon* supposedly summoned.

## Pythagoras (c. 570-510)

Pythagoras von Samos (; * um 570 v. Chr. auf Samos; + nach 510 v. Chr. in Metapont in der Basilicata) war ein antiker griechischer Philosoph (Vorsokratiker), Mathematiker und Gründer einer einflussreichen **religiös-philosophischen** Bewegung.

The *Pythagorean Succession* refers to the line of followers and schools of thought that carried on the teachings of Pythagoras after his death in approximately 495 BC. 

Key Successors and Influencers

Following *Pythagoras*, the school's leadership and intellectual development were maintained by several key figures:

1. ***Theano of Croton***: Tradition identifies her as *Pythagoras' wife*. She, along with her two daughters, is often credited with managing the Pythagorean School after his death and writing treatises on mathematics and medicine.
2. ***Philolaus of Croton*** (c. 470-390 BC): Regarded as the *primary* figure who developed *Pythagorean cosmology* into a more *mathematical* direction. He is often cited as the first to write down the secret Pythagorean teachings.
3. ***Archytas of Tarentum*** (fl. 400-350 BC): A statesman and mathematician who furthered the scientific rigor of the tradition and was a friend of *Plato*.
4. ***Telauges***: Alleged to be the *son* of Pythagoras, he is sometimes listed as a direct successor in various historical accounts of the *Italian School*. 

The Intellectual *Italian* Lineage

Ancient biographers like *Diogenes Laertius* traced a specific *Italian* succession that linked Pythagoras to later major philosophical movements: 

1. ***Pherekydes*** (*Pythagoras'* teacher)
2. ***Pythagoras***
3. ***Telauges***
4. ***Xenophanes***, ***Parmenides***, and ***Zeno of Elea*** (The *Eleatic School*)
5. ***Leucippus*** and ***Democritus*** (The *Atomists*)
6. ***Epicurus***

Impact on Later Philosophy

The *succession* eventually moved underground as the original schools were suppressed during political upheavals in Italy. However, its influence was immortalised through: 

1. ***Plato***: Who integrated many Pythagorean concepts such as the *immortality of the soul* and the *mathematical nature of the universe* into his own philosophy.
2. **Neopythagoreanism**: A revival of these ideas in the 1st c. BC that influenced early Christian and Renaissance thought.

## Anaxagoras (c. 499-428 BC)

Anaxagoras (; * um 499 v. Chr., wahrscheinlich in Klazomenai; + 428 v. Chr., wahrscheinlich in Lampsakos) war ein griechischer Philosoph (Vorsokratiker) aus Klazomenai in Kleinasien. Sein nur in *Fragmenten* und **hauptsächlich von Aristoteles überliefertes** philosophisches Denken wird als Zusammenführung der Ansätze *Heraklits* und der *Eleaten* gedeutet. Mit *Anaxagoras* gelangte die *ionische Aufklärung* nach Athen, denn dort verbrachte er die wichtigsten Jahrzehnte seines Lebens und stand dem leitenden Staatsmann *Perikles* als philosophischer Lehrer und Berater nahe. Auch der Tragödiendichter *Euripides* () ließ sich von ihm in das philosophische Denken und Forschen einführen. Als Mathematiker beschäftigte er sich hauptsächlich mit der *Quadratur des Kreises*.

The relationship between the playwright *Euripides* and the philosopher *Anaxagoras* is one of the most famous intellectual pairings of 5th c. BC Athens. *Euripides* is traditionally described as a *pupil* or *nursling* of *Anaxagoras*, and his tragedies are often seen as a *poetic vehicle* for the philosopher's radical *scientific* and *rationalist* ideas. 

The Intellectual Connection

*Euripides'* work frequently *mirrors* *Anaxagoras’* theories on the nature of the universe:

1. **Scientific Rationalism**: *Anaxagoras* was notorious for claiming the sun was a *red-hot mass of stone* rather than a god. *Euripides* *echoed* this in his plays, once calling the sun a *gold clod of earth*.
2. **Cosmogony**: *Anaxagoras* taught that the world began as a mixture of all things until *Mind* (*Nous*) set it in motion. This influence is visible in *Euripidean* fragments (such as those from ***Melanippe the Wise***) that *describe* the original *unity of heaven and earth* before they were separated.
3. **Skepticism and Humanism**: Both men were viewed as subversives who challenged traditional Olympian religion. This led to a shared reputation for *impiety*—*Anaxagoras* was eventually exiled from Athens for his views, and *Euripides’* plays often portrayed the gods as cruel or arbitrary.

## Euripides (c. 484-406 BC)

 was the last of the **three great tragic dramatists** of classical Athens, following **Aeschylus** and **Sophocles**. He is celebrated for his *psychological realism*, complex female characters, and a skeptical approach to traditional Greek mythology that made his work feel more *modern* than that of his predecessors. 

Key Characteristics & Innovations

1. *Psychological Realism*: Unlike the idealized heroes of earlier tragedy, Euripides portrayed mythical figures as ordinary people in extraordinary circumstances, focusing on their inner motives and flaws.
2. *Portrayal of Women*: He gave unprecedented prominence and complexity to female characters, leading some modern scholars to view him as a *prototype feminist*, though this remains a subject of academic debate.
3. *Deus ex Machina*: He frequently used the *god from the machine* (a god appearing at the end of a play to resolve the plot) to provide closure to messy human conflicts.
4. *Intellectual Skepticism*: His plays often questioned the morality of the gods and reflected the influence of contemporary thinkers like *Protagoras* and *Socrates*. 

Notable Extant Works

Of his estimated 92 plays, 18 or 19 have survived more or less complete—more than those of Aeschylus and Sophocles combined. 

1. **Medea** (431 BC): A powerful study of a woman's ruthless revenge after being abandoned by her husband, Jason.
2. **Hippolytus** (428 BC): A tragedy of fatal passion and divine vengeance involving *Phaedra* and her stepson.
3. **The Trojan Women** (415 BC): A bleak and powerful indictment of the cruelties of war.
4. **Cyclops** (408 BC): The only fully extant example of a satyr play, a comic and grotesque genre performed alongside tragedies,  retelling of Odysseus’s famous encounter with the one-eyed giant *Polyphemus* from Book 9 of *Homer’s Odyssey*.
5. **The Bacchae** (405 BC): Regarded as his *masterpiece*, it explores the conflict between rational order and the primal, irrational power of the god *Dionysus*.

Life and Legacy

Euripides was born on the island of Salamis. Although he was less successful than his rivals during his lifetime—winning only five first prizes in competitions—his popularity surged after his death. He spent his final years in the court of King *Archelaus of Macedonia*, where he died in 406 BC. His influence is seen throughout Western literature, from the New Comedy of the Hellenistic period to the works of *Shakespeare*, *Ibsen*, and *Shaw*.

## Plato (c. 427-347 BC)

Plato’s philosophy was not a solitary invention but a synthesis of several major Greek intellectual traditions. He systematically integrated the ethical focus of his teacher, *Socrates*, with the *metaphysical theories* of the *Pre-Socratics*. 

Core Predecessors and Their Influence

Plato's work is famously described as a *series of footnotes* to his own genius, yet he borrowed heavily from these specific figures: 

1. ***Socrates*** (The Ethical Foundation): Plato’s most direct influence and primary character in his dialogues. He inherited Socrates' relentless questioning (the Socratic method) and his focus on ethics and the soul over natural science.
2. ***Heraclitus*** (The World of Flux): From Heraclitus, Plato adopted the view that the physical, sensible world is in a state of constant change (*flux*), making it impossible to gain permanent knowledge from it alone.
3. ***Parmenides & Zeno*** (The World of Being): In contrast to flux, the Eleatic school taught that true reality is unchanging and unified. Plato used this to develop his Theory of Forms, arguing that while physical things change, the "Forms" (like Justice or Beauty) are eternal and stable.
4. ***Pythagoras*** (Mathematics & The Soul): Plato was deeply influenced by the Pythagorean belief in the immortality and transmigration of souls. He also shared their fascination with mathematical order as a key to understanding the universe.
5 . **The Sophists** (The Rivals): Figures like Protagoras and Gorgias served as Plato's intellectual *predecessors* by opposition. He rejected their relativism (the idea that *man is the measure of all things*) and developed his own objective philosophy in response. 

Origin and Historical Context

Plato (c. 427-347 BC) was born into an aristocratic Athenian family during the decline of Athens’ *Golden Age*. 

1. *Lineage*: His family claimed **descent from the god Poseidon** *and* the famous lawgiver ***Solon***.
2. *Political Catalyst*: Originally intending for a political career, he was disillusioned by the *Thirty Tyrants* (which included his own relatives) and the subsequent democratic government’s execution of Socrates.
3. *The Academy*: Following *Socrates'* death, *Plato* traveled for 12 years to Egypt, Italy, and Sicily to study with other thinkers before returning to Athens **to found the Academy** in 387 BC., the **first institution of higher learning in the West**.

## Hermippus of Smyrna (c. 289-204 BC)

Hermippus of Smyrna (Greek: Ἕρμιππος ὁ Σμυρναῖος) was a highly influential Greek grammarian and ***Peripatetic biographer*** who flourished during the 3rd c. BC, he was a disciple of the poet and scholar *Callimachus* and worked extensively in the *Library of Alexandria*. While his exact birth and death dates are not definitively known, scholars generally place his life and activity within the following chronological framework: 

#### Estimated Chronology

1. **Birth (c. 289–277 BC)**: Estimates place his birth in Smyrna (modern-day İzmir) during the 3rd c. BC.
2. **Education and Early Career (Mid-3rd c. BC)**: Known as *the Callimachian* (ho Kallimacheios), he was likely a *pupil or close associate* of the poet and scholar ***Callimachus of Cyrene*** at the *Library of Alexandria*.
3. **Major Literary Activity (c. 250–210 BC)**: During this peak period, he produced his ***influential biographical works***, including ***Lives (Bioi)***, which covered philosophers, poets, and lawgivers.
4. **Later Life (End of 3rd c. BC)**: His account of the life of the Stoic philosopher ***Chrysippus*** (who died c. 207 BC) **confirms** that *Hermippus* lived until at least the final decade of the 3rd c. BC.
5. **Death (c. 208–204 BC)**: Most historical reconstructions suggest he died shortly before the turn of the century. 

#### Historical Milestones

1. **Alexandrian Scholarship**: *Hermippus* leveraged the Pinakes (library catalogues) of the *Library of Alexandria* to compile extensive biographical data, though he was often criticized for prioritizing sensationalist anecdotes over strict factual accuracy.
2. **Biographical Legacy**: His work on figures like ***Pythagoras*** and ***Aristotle*** became a **primary source for** later ancient writers such as ***Plutarch*** and ***Diogenes Laërtius***.
3. **Cross-Cultural Documentation**: He is noted for early mentions of the debt ***Pythagoras*** allegedly owed to *Jewish and Thracian* doctrines, as well as writings on ***Zoroaster***. 


#### Major Works and Contributions

Although his **original writings are lost**, they were frequently **cited by** later authors such as ***Plutarch***, ***Diogenes Laertius***, and ***Flavius Josephus***. 

1. **The Lives (Bioi)**: An extensive biographical series covering philosophers, poets, orators, and lawgivers. It included the earliest known biography of Aristotle, as well as accounts of Pythagoras, Socrates, and Plato.
2. **On the Magi (Peri Magōn)**: A multi-volume work on Eastern wisdom-teachers. He is credited with providing a detailed account of the writings of Zoroaster, claiming the prophet had composed two million verses.
3. **Other Treatises**: He wrote about the Seven Sages of Greece and ancient lawgivers (Peri Nomothetōn). 


#### Style and Reliability

Hermippus is noted for a distinct approach that blended scholarly research with sensational storytelling: 

1. **Death Scenes and Anekdotes**: He had a *strong penchant* for dramatic and sometimes fictionalized death stories and sensational anecdotes to illustrate moral lessons.
2. **Methodology**: He utilized the Pinakes (catalogs) of the Library of Alexandria but often filled gaps in historical records with *audacious improvisations*.
3. **Cross-Cultural Influence**: He was one of the first Greek writers to ***suggest*** that Greek philosophy, particularly that of ***Pythagoras***, was ***influenced by Jewish and Thracian*** doctrines. 

### Sources

**No original manuscripts** of Hermippus of Smyrna (3rd c. BC) have **survived** to the present day. Like many Hellenistic authors, his extensive biographical works are considered lost, and our knowledge of them comes almost exclusively from *fragments* preserved in later literature. 

#### Nature of the Surviving Evidence

Instead of original manuscripts, scholars ***reconstruct*** *Hermippus'* work ***through***:

1. **Indirect Quotations (Fragments)**: Approximately 90 fragments of his writing survive as quotations or reports in the works of later authors.
2.**Successor Biographies**: Significant portions of his content were *recycled* or summarized by later biographers, most notably ***Diogenes Laertius*** in his *Lives of Eminent Philosophers* and ***Plutarch***.
3. **Papyrus Scraps**: Rare physical evidence includes multispectral imaging of papyri, such as ***Philodemus'*** *Index Academicorum*, which has helped verify the titles of *Hermippus'* lost works. 

#### Major Lost Works

*Hermippus* was a prolific biographer whose works (often cited under the title ***Bioi*** or Lives) included: 

1. ***On Lawgivers (Peri Nomothetōn)***: A collection comprising at least six books.
2. ***On the Seven Sages (Peri tōn Hepta Sophōn)***.
3. **Biographies of Philosophers**: He wrote lives of ***Aristotle, Pythagoras, Gorgias, Isocrates***, and ***Theophrastus***.
4. **On Zoroaster's Writings**: A work on Eastern wisdom that influenced later Greco-Roman perceptions of the Magi. 

His style was often characterized as *piquant* and storyteller-like, focusing on dramatic death-stories and personal anecdotes rather than rigorous historical fact.

## Philo of Alexandria (c. 20 BC – 50 AD)

Philo of Alexandria (c. 20 BC – 50 AD), also known as **Philo Judaeus**, was a prominent **Hellenistic Jewish philosopher** who lived in the Roman province of Egypt. He is famous for pioneering the ***allegorical interpretation of Hebrew Scripture to harmonize*** it with Greek philosophy, particularly ***Platonism and Stoicism***. 


#### Philosophical Concepts

1. ***The Logos***: *Philo's* ***most influential doctrine*** identifies the Logos (*Word* or *Reason*) as the divine intermediary between a transcendent, unknowable God and the material world.
2. ***Allegory***: He argued that the Torah contains deeper spiritual truths beneath its literal surface. For example, he interpreted **Adam as the mind** and **Eve as the senses**.
3. ***Negative Theology***: *Philo* is considered a ***founder of negative theology***, positing that while we can *know* God exists, his *essence* is entirely *beyond human comprehension*.
4. ***Creation***: He adapted *Plato's Timaeus* to suggest that God ***first created an intelligible world*** of Ideas (the **Logos**) as a *blueprint* for the ***physical universe***. 

#### Historical Significance

1. **Embassy to Caligula: In 40 AD**, *Philo* led a Jewish delegation to the Roman Emperor *Caligula* to protest the violation of Jewish religious rights in Alexandria. He chronicled this in his work *Legatio ad Gaium*.
2. **Influence on Christianity**: While largely ignored by later rabbinic Judaism, *Philo's* works were preserved and highly valued by early Christian Church Fathers like *Origen* and *Clement of Alexandria*. His *concept* of the ***Logos*** significantly *shaped the prologue* of the ***Gospel of John***.
3. **Family Background**: He came from a wealthy and politically connected family; his brother, *Alexander the Alabarch*, was one of the richest men in the Hellenistic world. 

#### Major Works, Categories of Extant Works

The surviving corpus is generally classified into **five thematic groups**: 

1. **Allegorical Commentaries** (39 treatises): Esoteric exegesis of the Torah, primarily the Book of Genesis. Key titles include 
- *Legum Allegoriae*
- *De Cherubim*
- *On the Creation*

2. **Exposition of the Law** (12 treatises): A more straightforward synthesis of the Pentateuch, including 

- biographies of the Patriarchs (*De Abrahamo*, *De Josepho*)
- the *Decalogue*

3. **Questions and Answers** (6 treatises): Verse-by-verse inquiries into Genesis and Exodus. These survive mostly in Armenian.

- *Questions and Answers on Genesis and Exodus*

4. **Historical and Apologetic** (4 treatises): Accounts of contemporary events and defenses of Judaism. Notable works include 

- *Against Flaccus*
- *Embassy to Gaius*
- *On the Contemplative Life*

5. **Philosophical Treatises** (5 treatises): Works using Greek philosophical frameworks, such as 

- *Every Good Man is Free*
- *On the Eternity of the World*
- *On Providence*

### Sources

**No original autographs** (the actual documents written by Philo's own hand) have **survived**. Like almost all ancient literature, Philo’s work is preserved through a combination of ***early papyrus fragments*** and much later ***medieval copies***. 
*Philo of Alexandria's* ***surviving works are exceptionally numerous*** for an ancient author, totaling approximately 850000 words preserved across roughly 38 to 39 treatises. While he wrote in Greek, his corpus was largely ignored by later Jewish traditions and was instead preserved by the early Christian Church, primarily in Caesarea. 


#### Earliest Extant Witnesses, Ancient Fragments (Pre-Medieval)

Before the medieval period, Philo's works existed in papyrus form, which are the earliest physical witnesses: 

1. The ***Paris Papyrus Codex (Suppl. gr. 1120/1)***: A **3rd c**. Greek codex containing *De sacrificiis Abelis et Caini* and *Legum allegoriae*. It is famously *one of the oldest books* in the world preserved *with its original cover*.
2. The ***Oxyrhynchus Fragments***: A **3rd c**. codex found in Egypt, which includes fragments of several works, such as *De ebrietate* and *De posteritate Caini*.


#### Surviving Translations, Armenian and Latin Traditions, Medieval Manuscript Traditions

Due to the loss of many Greek originals, early translations are critical.
The transmission of Philo's texts occurred through several distinct linguistic and thematic paths:

1. **Latin Translation**: A Latin translation of two books survives in 16 manuscripts, some of which are integrated into the broader medieval reception of his work. A few works, such as portions of *Questions and Answers on Genesis*, survive in a Latin translation from around **400 AD**. 

2. **Armenian Translation**: Approximately 20 books (including much of the *Quaestiones in Genesim et Exodum*) survive only in an Armenian translation dating from roughly **575 AD**. These manuscripts are vital because many of the Greek counterparts did not survive the medieval copying process.

3. **Indirect Tradition (Catenae & Florilegia)**: The Oxford Academic records that Christian writers like *Eusebius* and *Origen* preserved hundreds of citations of Philo, forming an *indirect tradition* that predates the surviving medieval manuscripts. Hundreds of citations are preserved in medieval *catenae* (verse-by-verse commentaries) and *florilegia* (anthologies of excerpts), such as the *Sacra Parallela* attributed to ***John of Damascus***. 

4. **Greek Codices**: Philo’s works were largely ignored by later Jewish tradition but were meticulously preserved by early Christian scholars. Figures like ***Eusebius of Caesarea*** and ***Clement of Alexandria*** valued his philosophical allegories, which heavily influenced early Christian theology and the development of the *Logos* doctrine. The earliest medieval manuscripts of Philo of Alexandria are part of a survival tradition primarily preserved by Christian scribes. While his original 1st c. AD writings are lost, The *direct tradition*  consists of about sixty medieval Greek manuscripts. Most medieval Philonic manuscripts are believed to originate from the Caesarean textual tradition, likely preserved in the ***great library of Caesarea Maritima*** before being copied into the medieval codices we have today. Notable examples include:

- ***Venetus A*** (**Marcianus Graecus Z. 454**) is a famous **10th c.** manuscript that, *primarily known for the Iliad*
- ***Coislinianus 276***: A ***10th c.*** codex containing extracts from Book 1 of the *Sacra Parallela*.
- ***Venetus Marcianus gr. 138***: An **11th c.** manuscript belonging to *Family D* of Philo’s transmission.
- ***Lincoln College MS. Gr. 34***: A manuscript at Oxford’s Lincoln College containing *On the Life of Moses* and *On Courage*.
- ***MS 143*** at New College, Oxford, is a significant Greek manuscript from **1533** containing 38 works. 

#### Lost Works

Many works are known only through mentions by ancient historians like ***Eusebius of Caesarea*** or through small Greek fragments found in Catenae (biblical commentary chains). Lost titles include biographies of the patriarchs *Isaac* and *Jacob* and several volumes of his historical account of the *Embassy to Gaius*. 

#### Collections and Libraries, Notable Manuscript Locations

Philo’s medieval manuscripts are housed in several major research libraries, many of which have digitized these collections: 

1. **Bodleian Library**: Holds several Greek medieval manuscripts of works like *De Josepho* and *De Vita Mosis*.
2. **New College, Oxford**: Contains significant Greek manuscripts, including MS 143 from 1533 that was instrumental in early modern Philo studies and the Lincoln College collection.
3. **Bibliothèque nationale de France**: Houses *early papyri* and medieval codices central to establishing critical editions of Philo’s text.
4. **Vatican Library & Heidelberg**: Digitized through the Polonsky Foundation Greek Manuscripts Project.

## Luke the Evangelist (c. 3 AD - 87 AD)

The primary date associated with Luke the Evangelist is his feast day on 18 October. Historically, the 1st c. physician and author is believed to have lived to the age of 84. 

Key Biographical Dates

1. **Birth**: Estimated between 1 AD -16 AD in Antioch, Roman Syria.
2. **Ministry**: Joined the *Apostle Paul* in Troas around 51 AD and remained a loyal companion through *Paul's* final imprisonment in Rome until his death in approximately 67 AD.
3. **Death**: Traditionally dated between 74 AD - 100 AD. Ancient accounts state he died in Boeotia, Greece. 


Writing Dates (Luke–Acts)

Scholars and tradition offer varying timelines for the composition of the Gospel of Luke and the Acts of the Apostles: 

1. Traditional View: Some traditions suggest he wrote his Gospel as early as 60 AD at the request of other Christians.
2. Early Church Tradition: Often ascribes the two-volume work to approximately 75 AD.
3. Modern Scholarly Consensus: Most contemporary scholars date the composition to 80–90 AD, citing the author's apparent knowledge of the destruction of Jerusalem (70 AD). 

Relic Translations

1. 357 AD: His relics were famously transferred from Thebes to Constantinople by order of Emperor Constantius.
2. Modern Day: His primary shrine and relics are now located in the Abbey of Santa Giustina in Padua, Italy.

## Hermippus, Philo and Lucas

The names *Hermippus, Philo, and Lucas* (Luke) are frequently cited together in **Renaissance** and early modern **philosophical texts** to establish the **antiquity** and divine **origin of Mosaic** wisdom. 

#### Philosophical and Historical Context

These three figures were used by scholars like ***Giovanni Pico della Mirandola*** in his **Heptaplus** (1489) to argue that the **wisdom of ancient Greece was derived from Hebrew** scripture. 
The connection between *Hermippus and Philo* most prominently appears in two contexts: the philosophical dialogues of *David Hume* and the relationship between ancient Greek biographers and Jewish philosophy.

1. **David Hume's Dialogues** Concerning Natural Religion 
In this classic philosophical work, *Hermippus and Philo* are both central, though they serve different functions in the narrative: 

- *Hermippus*: He is the addressee of the work. The entire dialogue is presented as a letter from *Pamphilus to Hermippus*, recounting a conversation about the *nature of God*.
- *Philo*: He is one of the three main characters in the dialogue. *Philo* represents the skeptical perspective, arguing that human reason is inadequate to understand the divine nature or prove God's existence through design.
- Context: The book explores *Natural Religion*—whether religious belief can be rationalized through observation of the world rather than revelation. 

2. ***Hermippus of Berytus and Philo of Byblos***
In historical records, there is a direct mentor-disciple relationship between two figures with these names: 

- *Relationship*: *Hermippus of Berytus* (2nd c. AD) was a slave who became the *disciple* of the famous grammarian *Philo of Byblos*.
- *Achievement*: Through *Philo's* recommendation, *Hermippus* rose to eminence as a scholar and eloquent speaker under the Roman emperors *Trajan* and *Hadrian*. 

3. ***Hermippus of Smyrna*** and Jewish Philosophy (***Philo of Alexandria***)
Scholars often analyze *Hermippus of Smyrna* (3rd c. BC) alongside the later Jewish philosopher *Philo of Alexandria* regarding the **Greek-Jewish connection**: 

- *Pythagoras and the Jews*: ***Hermippus of Smyrna*** is famous for claiming that the **Greek philosopher Pythagoras** derived his doctrines **from the Jews** and Thracians.
- *Philo's Integration*: ***Philo of Alexandria*** later became the primary figure known for **harmonizing Jewish theology with Greek philosophy** (*Platonism and Stoicism*), echoing the themes *Hermippus* first suggested centuries earlier.

## Saint Jerome (c. 342–347 – 420 AD)

Saint Jerome (c. 342–347 – 420 AD) was an early Christian priest, theologian, and historian. He is most famous for ***translating the Bible into Latin***, a version known as the ***Vulgate***, which became the standard text of the Roman Catholic Church for over a millennium. 

Key Facts & Legacy

1. The Vulgate: Commissioned by Pope Damasus I in 382 AD, Jerome’s translation of the Old Testament was unique because it was based directly on the Hebrew text rather than the Greek Septuagint.
2. Doctor of the Church: Due to his immense scholarship and prolific writings, he is recognized as one of the four original Doctors of the Church in the Latin tradition.
3. Patronage: He is the patron saint of librarians, translators, students, and archaeologists.
4. Temperament: Known for a sharp, often sarcastic tongue and a hot temper, Jerome was a fierce debater who frequently clashed with contemporaries like St. Augustine. 


Life & Monasticism

1. Early Life: Born in Stridon (modern-day Croatia/Slovenia). He lived a worldly life in Rome before undergoing a spiritual conversion following a fever-induced dream where he was accused of being a "Ciceronian" rather than a Christian.
2. Asceticism: He spent four years as a hermit in the Syrian desert of Chalcis to master Hebrew and penance.
3. Bethlehem: After leaving Rome following the death of Pope Damasus, he settled in Bethlehem in 386 AD. There, he founded a monastery and worked in a cave near the Church of the Nativity until his death. 


Common Iconography

In art, St. Jerome is typically depicted in one of three ways:

1. As a Scholar: In a study surrounded by books and writing materials, often wearing the red robes and hat of a Cardinal (though the office did not exist in his time).
2. With a Lion: According to legend, he tamed a lion by removing a thorn from its paw; the lion thereafter became his loyal companion.
3. As a Penitent: In the desert, emaciated and beating his breast with a stone while contemplating a crucifix.
