# Authors on Greek philosophers

---

## Diogenes Laërtius (3rd c. AD)

was a **biographer of the Greek philosophers**. Little is definitively known about his life, but his surviving work, ***Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers***, remains **a primary source** for the history of ancient Greek philosophy. 

The medieval manuscript tradition of *Diogenes Laërtius’* Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers is relatively sparse, with the **earliest surviving Greek copies** dating back only to the **12th c**. Despite the work being written in the early 3rd c. AD, there is a significant *gap* in its transmission history until its emergence in the Byzantine and later Medieval periods. 


Primary **Greek Manuscripts**

Scholars rely on three principal manuscripts, collectively referred to as the **B-P-F** group, *to reconstruct the original Greek text*: 

1. **Codex Parisinus (P)**: Dated to the late **11th-12th c.**, this manuscript is housed in the Bibliothèque nationale de France. Notably, a later hand added biography titles in the margins and blank spaces of this copy.
2. **Codex Borbonicus (B)**: **A 12th c.** manuscript held at the National Library of Naples. It is considered one of the most reliable sources for the text.
3. **Codex Laurentianus (F)**: A **13th c.** manuscript located in the Laurentian Library in Florence. 


Missing Text and the Stoic Lacuna

All extant manuscripts of the Lives are incomplete. Most notably, they all suffer from a lacuna in Book VII, which abruptly ends during the life of the Stoic philosopher ***Chrysippus***. A table of contents in one manuscript reveals that the original work continued with biographies of many subsequent Stoic philosophers, such as ***Zeno of Tarsus*** and ***Antipater of Tarsus***, which are now lost. 


**Latin Translations** and Medieval Reception

While the Greek text remained largely confined to the East during the High Middle Ages, it began to permeate Western European thought through translations and adaptations: 

1. ***Henry Aristippus***: In the **12th c.**, this Norman-Sicilian scholar is believed to have translated portions of the work into Latin, though these early versions have not survived. 
2. **De vita et moribus philosophorum**: A highly popular **14th c.** Latin compilation formerly attributed to *Walter Burley*. It drew heavily from Diogenes Laërtius and survives in over 270 manuscripts, indicating a wide medieval readership.
3. ***Ambrogio Traversari (c. 1430)***: During the Renaissance, this Florentine monk produced a definitive Latin translation that significantly boosted the work's popularity in the West.

## Simplicius of Cilicia (c. 480-560 AD)

Simplicius of Cilicia (/sɪmˈplɪʃiəs/; Greek: Σιμπλίκιος ὁ Κίλιξ; c. 480-540) was a disciple of *Ammonius Hermiae* and *Damascius*, and was one of the last of the *Neoplatonists*. He was among the pagan philosophers persecuted by Justinian in the early 6th c, and was forced for a time to seek *refuge in the Persian court*, before being allowed back into the empire. He **wrote extensively on the works of Aristotle**. 
Although his writings are **all commentaries** on *Aristotle* and *other authors*, rather than original compositions, his intelligent and prodigious learning makes him **the last great philosopher of pagan antiquity**. His works have preserved much information about earlier philosophers which would have otherwise been lost.

Medieval manuscripts of Simplicius of Cilicia (c. 480-560) are primarily Greek and Latin codices that preserve his extensive *Neoplatonic commentaries on Aristotle*. These manuscripts are critical historical artifacts, as they **often contain the only surviving fragments** of lost Pre-Socratic and Peripatetic works. 


Significant Manuscripts and Collections 

1. **Codex Marcianus Graecus 226**: A prominent **14th c.** Greek manuscript once owned by the influential scholar Basilios Bessarion, containing Simplicius' works.
2. **The Bodleian Library Collection**: Holds various medieval records and manuscript data relating to Simplicius, cataloged under his person entry in the Oxford University system.
3. **Vatican Library (Biblioteca Apostolica Vaticana)**: Houses numerous Greek manuscripts of his commentaries on Physics and De Caelo (On the Heavens) that served as the basis for early printed editions.
4. **Wellcome Collection**: Maintains a searchable archive of manuscripts and illustrations related to Simplicius. 

Key Transmitted Works

Medieval manuscripts typically organize Simplicius’ output into his major Aristotelian commentaries: 

1. **Commentary on the Categories**: Traces the history of logic through predecessors like Porphyry and Iamblichus.
2. **Commentary on Physics**: A massive work preserved in multiple volumes, notably edited in modern times by Hermann Diels from medieval sources.
3. **Commentary on De Caelo (On the Heavens)**: Preserves Eudoxus’ theory of concentric spheres.
4. **Commentary on the Enchiridion of Epictetus**: His only major commentary on a non-Aristotelian text, surviving in several later medieval copies. 


Manuscript Traditions

1. **Arabic Tradition**: His scientific and mathematical insights (including work on Euclid) were transmitted through Arabic manuscripts from the 10th c. onwards by scholars like ***Al-Nayrizi***.
2. **Byzantine Reception**: His manuscripts were heavily studied in Byzantium, where his *harmony* of *Plato* and *Aristotle* remained a central philosophical theme. 
3. **Latin Translations**: In the 13th c., ***William of Moerbeke*** translated parts of *Simplicius* into Latin, which *directly influenced* medieval thinkers like *Thomas Aquinas*.

# Technical aspects

---

## Papyrus

Papyrus was the world's primary writing surface for nearly 4000 years, essentially serving as the *precursor to modern paper*. 


1. **c. 3000-2900 BC**: **Invention** in Ancient Egypt. The earliest known blank roll was found in a tomb at Saqqara.
2. **c. 2560-2550 BC**: The oldest known written papyrus, the Diary of Merer, records the construction of the Great Pyramid of Giza.
3. **c. 1600-1550 BC**: Notable medical texts like the Edwin Smith Surgical Papyrus and the Ebers Papyrus are composed.
4. **c. 332-30 BC**: The Ptolemaic Period sees papyrus widely adopted by the Greeks for literature and administration.
5. **2nd c. AD**: **Parchment** (animal skin) **begins** to emerge as a durable but expensive rival.
6. **8th-10th c. AD**: The **Arab world introduces** the Chinese method of making pulp **paper**, which is cheaper and more durable than papyrus.
7. **1022-1057**: One of the last official uses of papyrus in Europe was for Papal bulls (decrees) from the Vatican.
8. **11th c.**: Papyrus production and use **largely disappear** as it is fully replaced by paper.

## Parchment

Parchment is animal skin (typically *sheep*, *goat*, or *calf*) that has been prepared for writing by being scraped and dried under tension. 


1. **c. 2400 BC**: Earliest known fragments of prepared animal hide used for writing are found in Egypt (6th Dynasty).
2. **2nd c. BC**: Legend attributes the *invention* of parchment to *King Eumenes II* of Pergamum (modern-day Turkey) after Egypt banned papyrus exports. In reality, the city **likely perfected** the manufacturing process rather than inventing it.
3. **3rd-4th c. AD**: Parchment begins to **replace papyrus** as the primary writing material in the West, coinciding with the **transition from scrolls to the codex** (book) format.
4. **8th-12th c.**: Medieval monasteries become the primary centers for parchment craft. During this time, *Palimpsests* (**recycled parchment** where old text was scraped off to make room for new) became common due to the high cost of new skins.
5. **13th-15th c.**: A *Renaissance* in parchment making occurs, producing extremely thin, **high-quality** *uterine vellum*.
6. **1450s-1500**: The invention of the **printing press** and the spread of cheaper paper lead to a **decline** in parchment use, though it remains a luxury choice for elite manuscripts and legal documents.
6. **19th c. - Present**: *Parchment paper* (cellulose-based paper treated with acid) is invented to mimic the properties of real parchment. Today, real parchment is used primarily for high-end art, restoration, and formal government acts.

## Paper

The history of paper spans over 5000 years, beginning with precursor materials like *papyrus* and evolving into the mass-produced wood-pulp products used today. 
While ***Cai Lun*** is traditionally credited with inventing paper in 105 AD, archaeological evidence suggests early forms *existed in China* as far back as 200 BC. 


Ancient Precursors (3000 - 200 BC)

Before true paper, civilizations used various natural surfaces to record information. 

1. **c. 3000 BC**: Egyptians develop **papyrus**, a writing surface made from the pith of the papyrus plant. While it shares the same linguistic root as *paper*, it is technically a *laminated mat*, not true paper.
2. **c. 2450 BC**: Earliest records of **parchment**, made from treated animal skins, which provided a *more durable* alternative to papyrus.
3. **200 BC**: Early forms of **paper appear in China**, often used for *wrapping or padding* rather than writing. 


The Invention and Spread of Paper (105 AD - 1400) 

This era marks the shift from localized Chinese production to a *global industry*. 

1. **105 AD**: ***Cai Lun*** **standardizes the papermaking process** in China using mulberry bark, hemp, and old rags.
2. **610 AD**: Papermaking **reaches Japan**, introduced by *Buddhist monks*.
3. **751 AD**: After the *Battle of Talas*, Chinese **papermakers** are **captured by Arabs**, leading to the *establishment of paper* mills in Samarkand and later Baghdad (793 AD).
4. **1151**: The **first European paper** mill is established in Xativa, **Spain, by the Moors**.
5. **1282**: Italian papermakers in Fabriano introduce **major innovations**, including watermarks and the use of animal glue for sizing. 


Industrialization and the Printing Revolution (1450 - 1900) 

The demand for paper surged with the invention of the printing press and mechanized manufacturing. 

1. 1450-1455: ***Johannes Gutenberg*** invents the movable-type **printing press**, creating a *massive demand* for affordable paper.
2. 1690: The **first paper mill in the United States** is built in Pennsylvania by ***William Rittenhouse***.
3. 1798: ***Nicholas-Louis Robert*** invents the **first papermaking machine**, which produces paper in *continuous rolls*.
4. 1803: The **Fourdrinier machine**, an **improved version** of Robert's invention, becomes the industry standard.
5. 1844-1850: Development of **wood pulp paper** begins, making paper significantly cheaper and ending the reliance on cotton and linen rags. 


Modern Era (1950 - Present)

Recent developments focus on efficiency, specialized coatings, and environmental impact. 

1. 1950s: Technological advances introduce **new pulping processes** and synthetic fillers.
2. 1985: Bleaching processes transition from elemental chlorine to more environmentally friendly methods like ECF and TCF.
3. Present Day: Focus shifts toward sustainability, recycling, and reducing CO2 emissions, even as *China regains its status as the world's leading producer*.

## Papyrus	- Parchment - Paper

Paper, parchment, and papyrus **represent the evolution of writing surfaces**, each defined by distinct raw materials and durability. 


**Table 1**. Comparison.
~~~
Feature 		Papyrus							Parchment							Paper

Source			Papyrus plant (reeds)			Animal skins (sheep, goat, calf)	Plant fibers (wood pulp, rags, hemp)
Origin			Ancient Egypt (~3000 BC)		Pergamon (~2nd Century BC)			China (~105 AD)
Durability		Fragile; rots in humidity		Extremely durable; lasts centuries	Moderate; ages better than papyrus
Reusability		Limited; one-sided mostly		High (can be scraped and reused)	Low; harder to erase without damage
Cost			Relatively cheap to produce		Very expensive; labor-intensive		Cheapest once mass-produced
~~~

Key Differences

1. **Manufacturing**: 

- Papyrus is made by **layering strips of the papyrus plant's** pith in a cross pattern and pressing them together. 
- Parchment involves *soaking* **animal skins** in lime, *stretching* them on a frame, and *scraping* them thin. 
- Paper is created by **breaking down fibers into a pulp**, *spreading* it on a screen, and *drying* it into sheets.

2. **Format**: 
- Papyrus was primarily used for **scrolls** because it cracks when folded. 
- Parchment’s flexibility allowed for the creation of the **codex** (the modern **book format**) as it could be folded and sewn without breaking.

3. **Modern Usage**: While 
- ancient parchment was ***skin-based***, 
- modern parchment paper used in baking is actually a ***cellulose-based*** paper treated with *silicone* or *acid* to make it heat-resistant and non-stick.
